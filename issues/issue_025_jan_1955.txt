TRANSMISSIONS FROM THE EDITOR'S DESK
"Now It Is Time to Build"
by Maxwell Sterling

Dear Reader,

Two years ago this month, I sat down at this desk and composed a letter to
a readership that did not yet exist. I spoke of grand ambitions, of a
magazine that would take the imagination seriously, of stories that would
linger in the mind long after the page was turned. I asked you to trust me,
a stranger, with thirty-five cents and an evening of your time.

You did. And then you came back. Month after month, through twenty-four
issues and one hundred and forty-three stories, you have been our partners
in this enterprise. You have written us letters of passionate praise and
equally passionate criticism. You have pressed copies of this magazine into
the hands of friends, lovers, and skeptical relatives. You have, in short,
built an audience -- and an audience, I have learned, is not a passive
thing. It is a community of minds, engaged and demanding, and it changes
the magazine as surely as the magazine changes it.

We have spent two years dreaming. Now it is time to build.

What I mean by this is something I have felt growing in the stories that
cross my desk, a maturation I find deeply exciting. Our writers are no
longer content merely to imagine the future. They want to *construct* it --
to work out the engineering, the politics, the social architecture, the
moral foundations. Hal Mercer's magnificent two-part "Dyson Initiative"
concludes in this issue, and it is the finest example of this new ambition:
a story about building something so vast that it transforms the solar
system itself, and the human consequences of succeeding too well. It is a
story about engineering, yes, but it is also a story about responsibility,
about the courage to redesign your own masterwork when the evidence demands
it, about the terrifying gap between intention and consequence.

This issue is, I believe, the strongest we have ever published. Theodore
Cain delivers a first-contact comedy that is also, beneath its wit, a
profound meditation on the nature of communication. Dr. Koslov gives us a
courtroom drama that asks whether a discovery can be too dangerous to
share. Rex Drummond -- yes, our Rex -- produces a moral thriller that
complicates every assumption you have about his work. Lucian Marsh offers a
horror story that is also a love letter to the fragile miracle of memory.
And Father Aquino writes about sanctuary with an urgency that, in this
troubled world, needs no explanation.

Year three. The dreaming continues, but now we build as well.

Yours among the stars,

Maxwell Sterling
Editor, Tales from the Future and Beyond
New York, November 1954

                     THE DYSON INITIATIVE, PART II

                     by Harold "Hal" Mercer

================================================================================

                              I.

The swarm was beautiful, and the swarm was killing Mercury.

Yuki Tanaka stood at the observation window of Sunward Station, seventy
million kilometers from the star, and watched the collectors drift. There
were nine hundred and twelve million of them now -- thin hexagonal panels,
each one four hundred meters across, their mirrored faces angled to catch
the full spectrum of solar radiation and convert it into tight microwave
beams aimed at receiver stations throughout the inner system. From this
distance they looked like silver coins scattered across a black table,
each one catching the light and throwing it back in disciplined, focused
streams. The swarm covered approximately two-tenths of one percent of the
Sun's total output area, which did not sound like much until you ran the
numbers.

Tanaka had run the numbers. She had been running the numbers for
twenty-three years, ever since the Dyson Initiative's governing board had
appointed her chief architect at the absurd age of thirty-one. She had
designed the collector geometry, specified the orbital families,
calculated the resonance avoidance parameters, supervised the fabrication
plants on Ceres and Vesta, and argued with seventeen national delegations
about construction priorities until her voice went hoarse and her patience
went to dust. She had given her marriage to the swarm, her health to the
swarm, and most of her capacity for small talk to the swarm. The Dyson
Initiative was, by any reasonable measure, the largest engineering project
in human history, and Yuki Tanaka had built it.

And now she was receiving reports that it was cooling the inner planets.

"The temperature drop on Mercury's surface is fourteen degrees Kelvin over
the last eighteen months," said Renjiro Sato, her chief of systems
analysis, reading from the pad in his hands. He was a tall, thin man with
the careful manner of someone who delivered bad news for a living. "That's
aggregate. The night-side numbers are worse -- twenty-one degrees in some
basins. The mining settlements at Caloris are reporting condensation in
their thermal extraction systems. They were designed for a four-hundred-
degree operating environment, and they're getting three-seventy."

"Thirty degrees won't collapse a thermal extraction system," Tanaka said.

"No, ma'am. But the trend line will. If collection efficiency continues to
increase at the current rate, Mercury's mean surface temperature drops
another forty degrees in five years. That puts Caloris below design
threshold." Sato paused. "And Venus is worse."

Tanaka did not turn from the window. She had designed those collectors. She
knew their efficiency curves the way a mother knows the curve of a child's
cheek. The problem was not a malfunction. The problem was that the swarm
was performing exactly as designed -- better, in fact, than the projections
she had filed with the governing board in 2087. The fabrication plants had
exceeded their quotas. The deployment drones had placed collectors faster
than the schedule demanded. The microwave transmission losses had come in
twelve percent below estimate. Everything was working. Everything was
working too well.

"What are the Venus numbers?" she asked.

Sato consulted his pad. "Mean global temperature has dropped nine degrees
over three years. The sulfuric acid cycle in the upper atmosphere is
shifting. The Venusian Atmospheric Authority says their cloud-mining
operations are seeing chemistry changes they don't fully understand yet."

"They don't understand them because nobody modeled for this scenario,"
Tanaka said. "Nobody planned for the swarm to work this well."

"No, ma'am."

She finally turned from the window. Sato was watching her with the careful,
neutral expression of a man who was too professional to say I told you so
but wanted her to know that he could, if pressed.

"How long before the colonies file formal objections?" she asked.

"Mercury filed two hours ago. Venus filed forty minutes ago. Mars has filed
a preemptive endorsement of whatever Mercury and Venus are complaining
about, because Mars always does."

Tanaka allowed herself the thinnest of smiles. "And Earth?"

"Earth is enjoying the cheapest energy in human history and has no
complaints."

"Of course not."

                              * * *

The governing board met in the amphitheater of Sunward Station, a circular
room whose transparent ceiling offered an unobstructed view of the Sun.
The delegates sat in concentric rings around a central well, their faces
lit from above by filtered starlight that gave everyone the complexion of
hammered copper. There were forty-seven delegates representing thirty-one
nations and fourteen colonial entities. Most of them looked unhappy.

Tanaka stood in the well and presented the data.

She had spent three hours preparing the briefing with Sato and her
engineering staff, stripping the presentation down to essentials: the
collection rates, the temperature curves, the atmospheric models, the
timeline. She spoke for twenty minutes without notes. She did not minimize
the problem. She did not exaggerate it. She laid out the numbers and let
them speak.

When she finished, the Mercurian delegate was the first to stand. His name
was Adriano Voss, a compact man with the deep tan and permanent squint of
someone who had spent most of his life under a sun that was too close.

"Chief Architect Tanaka," he said, and his tone made the title sound like
an accusation. "My government wishes to state, for the record, that we
warned this board about excessive collection rates eight years ago. Our
atmospheric scientists predicted exactly this scenario. We were told that
our models were 'conservative' and that the swarm's effects would be
'within acceptable parameters.' I have the minutes of that session in
front of me. I'd like to know whose parameters we're talking about."

"You are correct that Mercury raised concerns in 2098," Tanaka said. "The
board reviewed the models and determined that the projected impact fell
within the design envelope. That determination was based on the collection
rates as they existed at that time. Collection rates have since exceeded
projections by a factor of one point four."

"So you built it better than you expected, and now it's hurting us more
than you expected. Is that the essence of the situation?"

"That is a reasonable summary, yes."

Voss sat down. The Venusian delegate stood. Her name was Daria Kolokov,
and she had the cold precision of a woman who had spent twenty years
arguing Venus's interests against the gravitational pull of Earth's
priorities.

"Chief Architect, I have a simpler question. Can you fix it?"

Tanaka had been waiting for this question. She had, in fact, been working
on the answer for six weeks, since the first anomalous temperature data
had crossed her desk. She had spent those weeks in her office on Sunward
Station, surrounded by structural diagrams and orbital plots, drinking
cold tea and running simulations until the numbers blurred.

"Yes," she said. "I can fix it. But the fix requires a fundamental
redesign of the collector geometry, and it cannot be done gradually. It
must be done as a coordinated operation across the entire swarm."

The amphitheater went very quiet.

                              * * *

The redesign was, Tanaka reflected later, the hardest thing she had ever
done as an engineer. Harder than the original design, because the original
design had been theoretical -- a problem to be solved on paper and in
simulation, where the variables behaved themselves and the only
consequences of error were embarrassment and revision. The redesign had to
be performed on a living system, with nine hundred million collectors
already in orbit, already generating power, already integrated into an
energy grid on which four billion people depended.

It was like rebuilding an engine while the car was moving at highway speed.

She assembled her team in the engineering module of Sunward Station: Sato,
of course, and chief fabricator Ingrid Halvorsen, and orbital mechanics
lead Chen Wei, and power systems engineer Tomoko Arai, and twenty-three
others whose specialties ranged from materials science to political
strategy. They filled the long conference table and its overflow chairs,
and Tanaka stood at the head of the room and told them what she wanted.

"The problem is geometric," she said, pulling up the swarm schematic on the
wall display. Nine hundred million blue dots swarmed around a yellow
circle. "The collectors are arranged in twelve orbital families, each
family occupying a different inclination band. The original design
distributed them to maximize total collection area. That's what they're
doing. The problem is that maximum collection also means maximum
interception of solar radiation before it reaches the inner planets."

She touched a control, and the display shifted. The blue dots rearranged
themselves into a new pattern -- thinner, more structured, with visible
gaps between the orbital bands.

"The solution is to introduce transparency corridors. We reorient
approximately thirty percent of the collectors in each orbital family to
create gaps -- windows through which unobstructed sunlight can reach
Mercury, Venus, and the ecliptic plane. The collectors in the corridor
zones don't stop collecting. They tilt. They angle their faces to catch
light from a slightly different vector, redirecting rather than
intercepting. Total collection efficiency drops by six percent. Inner
planet insolation returns to within two degrees of pre-swarm levels."

Chen Wei, who was sixty years old and had been building things in space
since before Tanaka was born, raised his hand. "You're asking us to
reposition two hundred and seventy million collectors."

"Two hundred and seventy-three million, to be precise."

"Simultaneously."

"Within a four-month window, to minimize disruption to the power grid."

Chen Wei looked at the schematic for a long time. Then he said, "The
station-keeping fuel budgets alone will require retooling every fabrication
plant in the belt."

"Yes."

"And the orbital resonance calculations for the new geometry will take --"

"I've done them," Tanaka said. She opened her briefcase and took out a
stack of printout that was approximately eight centimeters thick. She set
it on the table with a thud that was, she admitted to herself, slightly
theatrical. "The resonance maps are complete. The tilt angles are
specified for every collector in every orbital family. The fuel requirements
are calculated. The timeline is drafted."

The room was silent.

"When did you do all this?" Sato asked.

"I haven't been sleeping much."

                              II.

The first shots were fired six days after the governing board approved the
redesign.

They came from the Mercury Colonial Militia, a paramilitary organization
that had existed in a state of legal ambiguity for a decade. The militia
operated three armed patrol vessels that were nominally tasked with
asteroid defense but which everyone understood were really Mercury's
attempt at a navy. On the morning of March 14th, 2107, the patrol vessel
_Helios_ fired two warning shots across the bow of a deployment drone
that was attempting to reorient collectors in the innermost orbital family.

The shots were plasma bolts from a mining laser that had been awkwardly
mounted on the _Helios_'s dorsal hull. They missed the drone by four
hundred meters, which suggested either poor aim or careful restraint.
Tanaka suspected the latter and found it more troubling than the former.

The message from the Mercurian delegation arrived twenty minutes later.
It was addressed to the governing board, but it was meant for Tanaka.

"The Mercury Colonial Government hereby notifies the Dyson Initiative
Governing Board that any attempt to reorient collectors in Orbital Families
One through Four without Mercury's explicit consent will be regarded as an
act of economic aggression against the sovereign territory of Mercury. The
transparency corridors proposed by Chief Architect Tanaka will restore
solar radiation to Mercury's surface, but only after a four-month
transition period during which the reorientation process itself will
temporarily *increase* solar blockage by an additional eight percent. This
temporary increase will push Mercury's thermal extraction systems past
their failure point. Mercury's mining economy will collapse. Chief
Architect Tanaka's 'fix' will destroy us before it saves us."

Tanaka read the message three times. Then she pulled up her reorientation
timeline and looked at the Phase One schedule for Orbital Families One
through Four. The Mercurians were right. The reorientation process required
collectors to pass through a perpendicular orientation as they tilted from
their current angle to their new one. During that perpendicular phase,
each collector presented its maximum cross-section to the Sun -- which
meant maximum light blockage. For any individual collector, the
perpendicular phase lasted less than ninety seconds. But with two hundred
and seventy-three million collectors reorienting in sequence, the
cumulative effect across the inner orbital families would be a temporary
deepening of the shadow.

She had accounted for this in her calculations. The aggregate temperature
effect was an additional 1.3 degrees Kelvin over the four-month
reorientation window. Significant but not catastrophic -- unless your
thermal extraction systems were already operating at their revised,
lower margin. Which Mercury's were.

"They're right," she said to Sato. "Not about the eight percent -- that's
their worst-case number, and it assumes every collector in Families One
through Four reorients simultaneously, which my timeline doesn't call for.
But the principle is correct. Phase One will make things worse before it
makes them better."

"Can you resequence?" Sato asked. "Start with the outer families and work
inward?"

"If I start with the outer families, Venus screams. Venus is getting most
of its blockage from Families Five through Eight. Every delegation wants
their problem fixed first, and fixing any one of them first makes the
others temporarily worse."

"So you need to do them all at once."

"I need to do them all at once. Which I can't do, because I don't have
enough deployment drones."

She sat down at her desk and stared at the orbital plots on her display.
Nine hundred and twelve million collectors, each one exactly where she
had told it to be. She had built the most successful engineering project
in human history, and now she had to take it apart and rebuild it while
the people who depended on it shot at her.

                              * * *

The Venus situation escalated three days later.

The Venusian Atmospheric Authority -- which, unlike Mercury's militia, had
actual legal jurisdiction over its orbital space -- announced that it was
deploying a network of reflector satellites between the swarm and Venus.
The reflectors would bounce additional sunlight down to the planet's cloud
tops, compensating for the swarm's blockage independently of Tanaka's
redesign.

The problem was that the reflectors would also scatter unfocused light into
the orbital corridors that Tanaka's deployment drones used for transit.
The drones navigated by star-tracker, and scattered light blinded their
sensors.

"They're not trying to stop the redesign," Tanaka said, studying the
reflector deployment plan on her display. "They're trying to make it
unnecessary. If their reflectors work, they don't need my transparency
corridors, which means they don't need to go through the reorientation
pain. They solve their own problem and leave Mercury to deal with the
consequences."

"Will their reflectors work?" Sato asked.

"Short term, partially. Long term, no. They can't match the energy output
that the swarm is intercepting. And their scattered light will interfere
with my operations for as long as they're deployed."

"So Venus is accidentally sabotaging your fix while trying to avoid it."

"Nothing about this is accidental," Tanaka said.

                              III.

She solved the problem the way she solved all problems: with math,
stubbornness, and a phone call she did not want to make.

The math came first. She locked herself in her office for forty-eight
hours with Chen Wei and Arai and worked out a new reorientation sequence
that she called the cascade protocol. Instead of reorienting collectors
family by family, the cascade protocol treated the entire swarm as a
single dynamic system. Collectors would reorient in a rolling wave that
started at the swarm's outermost edge and swept inward, each collector
beginning its tilt exactly 0.7 seconds after its nearest neighbor. The
wave would take eleven days to cross the full diameter of the swarm. At
any given moment, only a thin shell of collectors would be in their
perpendicular phase, and the shadow effect would be distributed so evenly
across the inner system that no single planet would experience more than
a 0.4-degree temperature fluctuation.

It was, Chen Wei said, either brilliant or insane.

"The timing tolerances are absurd," he told Tanaka. "Zero-point-seven
seconds between adjacent collectors, propagated across nine hundred
million units. If the wave desynchronizes by more than three seconds at
any point, you get harmonic oscillations in the tilt angles that could
shatter collectors by the thousands."

"Then we don't desynchronize," Tanaka said.

"The communication lag alone --"

"We pre-program the sequence into every collector's onboard computer.
No real-time commands. No communication lag. Every collector carries its
own clock and its own instructions. They execute autonomously."

"And if a collector's clock drifts?"

"We built these collectors, Chen. We specified the oscillators. The clocks
are accurate to ten nanoseconds per day. Over an eleven-day cascade, the
maximum drift is one hundred and ten nanoseconds. The tolerance is three
seconds. We have a margin of approximately twenty-seven million to one."

Chen Wei looked at her for a long moment. Then he smiled -- a rare
expression on his weathered face. "You really haven't been sleeping."

"No."

The stubbornness came next. She presented the cascade protocol to the
governing board and spent four hours defending it against objections that
ranged from the technically legitimate (the fuel cost was eleven percent
higher than the original reorientation plan) to the politically motivated
(the Martian delegate wanted to know why Mars wasn't getting its own
transparency corridor, even though Mars was beyond the swarm's shadow
zone and received no blockage whatsoever).

She answered every question. She addressed every concern. She did not
raise her voice, she did not lose her patience, and she did not allow
anyone to table the vote for further study. When the Mercurian delegate
accused her of "gambling with the lives of four hundred thousand
colonists," she pulled up her temperature models and walked him through
the numbers, degree by degree, until he conceded that the 0.4-degree
fluctuation was within survivable parameters.

The vote was thirty-nine to eight in favor, with Mars abstaining on
principle.

                              * * *

The phone call she did not want to make was to Commander Adriano Voss of
the Mercury Colonial Militia.

She placed it from her office, alone, at 0300 station time. Voss answered
from the bridge of the _Helios_, which was still patrolling the inner
orbital families. His face on the screen was tired and defiant.

"Commander Voss," Tanaka said. "I am calling to inform you that the
cascade protocol will commence in seventy-two hours. Deployment drones
will enter all orbital families simultaneously. I am requesting that you
stand down your patrol vessels for the duration of the operation."

Voss's jaw tightened. "You know I can't do that without authorization from
the Colonial Governor."

"I know. I also know that the Colonial Governor will not give that
authorization unless he believes the cascade protocol will work. So I am
going to explain it to you, Commander, and you are going to explain it to
him. Not because I have faith in the chain of command, but because I have
read your service record, and I know that you are an engineer before you
are a soldier."

There was a pause. On the screen, Voss's expression shifted -- something
hard behind his eyes giving way, just slightly, to something curious.

"I'm listening," he said.

She explained. It took an hour and a half. She walked him through the
cascade geometry, the timing tolerances, the temperature models, the
fail-safe protocols. She answered his questions -- and they were good
questions, the questions of an engineer who had studied the swarm's
architecture and understood its vulnerabilities. Twice he caught errors
in her backup calculations that she had missed, and she corrected them
on the spot, showing him the corrections so he could verify them.

When she finished, Voss was quiet for a long time.

"Your cascade protocol will work," he said finally. "Your math is right.
But I need something from you."

"Name it."

"When the cascade wave passes through Families One through Four, I want
a Mercury engineer in your control room. Someone who can verify the
telemetry in real time and confirm that Mercury's exposure stays within
your projected envelope. Not a board observer. One of my people."

"Done," Tanaka said.

"And I want your personal guarantee -- not the board's, yours -- that if
the temperature drop exceeds your projections by more than one degree, you
will pause the cascade until we understand why."

Tanaka hesitated. A one-degree pause threshold was tight. It would give
her almost no margin for the normal fluctuations of a complex dynamic
operation. If a single collector cluster tilted slightly off-spec, the
local temperature variation could trigger the threshold and halt the
entire cascade -- and a halted cascade was exponentially harder to restart
than a continuous one.

But Voss was asking her to put her reputation on her math. And her math
was right.

"You have my guarantee," she said.

Voss nodded. "I'll talk to the Governor. Stand by for confirmation."

The confirmation came eighteen hours later. The _Helios_ and its sister
ships withdrew to Mercury orbit. Venus, seeing Mercury stand down,
recalled its reflector satellites. Mars issued a statement expressing
general disapproval of everything.

                              IV.

The cascade began at 0600 UTC on April 3rd, 2107.

Tanaka watched it from the control room of Sunward Station, surrounded by
her engineering team and a bank of displays that showed the swarm in real
time. The Mercurian engineer -- a young woman named Esperanza Vega who
had graduated top of her class from the Caloris Technical Institute --
sat at a console in the corner, monitoring Mercury's insolation telemetry
with the focused intensity of someone whose home was at stake.

The outermost collectors began their tilt at 0600:00.000. Each hexagonal
panel rotated on its central axis, pivoting from its current collection
angle through perpendicular and onward to its new orientation. The
rotation took four minutes and twelve seconds per collector. The wave
propagated inward at the speed Tanaka had calculated: 0.7 seconds between
adjacent collectors, a ripple of motion spreading through nine hundred
million panels like a breeze crossing a wheat field.

On the displays, it was mesmerizing. The swarm's visual signature shifted
as the wave passed through -- collectors flashing bright as they hit
perpendicular, then dimming as they settled into their new angles, the
overall pattern flowing like liquid mercury. Tanaka had seen computer
simulations of the cascade a hundred times, but the reality was different.
The reality was beautiful.

"Wave front passing through Family Twelve," Chen Wei reported. "Timing
deviation: zero-point-zero-zero-three seconds. Within tolerance."

"Family Eleven in twelve minutes," Sato added.

Tanaka stood at the center of the control room and watched the numbers.
She had done everything she could. The math was right, the engineering was
sound, the politics were managed. Now it was execution -- the pure,
mechanical unfolding of a plan she had built from numbers and will and six
weeks of sleepless nights.

The wave crossed Family Twelve without incident. It entered Family Eleven
and proceeded smoothly. Families Ten, Nine, Eight -- each one reported
nominal deviations, timing tight, temperatures stable. Venus's
atmospheric monitoring stations confirmed a slight uptick in surface
insolation as the wave passed -- the first increase Venus had seen in
three years.

By the end of the first day, the wave had crossed the outer six families.
Total collection efficiency had dropped by 2.1 percent -- within
projections. No collector had failed. No timing deviation had exceeded
half a second.

Tanaka allowed herself a cup of tea. It was the first warm beverage she
had consumed in seventy-two hours.

                              * * *

The crisis came on Day Four, when the wave entered Family Three.

Family Three was the densest orbital family, containing nearly two hundred
million collectors in a tight band between Mercury's orbit and Venus's.
The collectors here were closer together than in any other family -- their
average separation was only eighteen kilometers, compared to forty
kilometers in the outer families. The tighter spacing meant that the
timing tolerances were correspondingly tighter, and the thermal shadow
during the perpendicular phase was proportionally deeper.

At 1447 UTC, Esperanza Vega's console sounded an alarm.

"Mercury insolation dropping," she said, her voice calm but taut. "Rate
of decrease exceeding model by zero-point-six degrees."

Tanaka was at her side in three seconds. She looked at the telemetry.
The temperature curve for Mercury's Caloris basin was dipping faster than
her models predicted -- not dramatically, not catastrophically, but
faster. The deviation was small. It was also growing.

"Source?" Tanaka asked.

Chen Wei was already running diagnostics. "Collector cluster 3-Alpha-7700
through 3-Alpha-7900. Two hundred collectors. Their tilt angles are
running one-point-two degrees ahead of the cascade schedule."

"Ahead? They're tilting too fast?"

"Their onboard clocks are running slightly fast. Oscillator drift. Within
spec individually, but they're all in the same fabrication batch, and the
drift is correlated."

Two hundred collectors, tilting 1.2 degrees ahead of schedule. It meant
they were hitting their perpendicular phase -- their maximum shadow phase
-- slightly before the wave was supposed to reach them. The result was a
momentary deepening of the shadow over Mercury's nearest surface.

Tanaka looked at the numbers. The deviation was 0.6 degrees and climbing.
Her threshold with Voss was one degree. She had perhaps forty minutes
before the deviation hit the threshold and she was obligated to halt the
cascade.

Halting the cascade in Family Three would be difficult. Restarting it
would be a nightmare. The timing synchronization for nine hundred million
collectors had been calculated as a single, continuous operation. Stopping
it midway was like stopping an orchestra mid-symphony and expecting every
musician to come back in at the right beat.

"Can we adjust?" she asked Chen Wei.

"We can upload a corrective tilt command to the affected cluster. Override
their onboard timing. But the command has to propagate through the
communication relay, and the relay lag to Family Three is eleven seconds."

"Eleven seconds is fine. The deviation is changing slowly."

"But we've never uploaded a mid-cascade correction before. It's outside
the tested protocol."

Tanaka looked at Vega. The young Mercurian engineer was watching her with
an expression that was not fear, exactly, but something close to it --
the focused attention of someone whose entire world was balanced on the
next decision.

"If I correct the cluster," Tanaka said to her, "the deviation will
reverse within twenty minutes. If I halt the cascade, I can guarantee
Mercury stays below threshold, but the restart will take at least two
weeks, and during those two weeks the shadow stays exactly where it is."

Vega processed this. "Two weeks at current shadow levels means Caloris
drops another three degrees. Our extraction systems can't take that."

"No."

"Then correct the cluster."

Tanaka nodded. "Chen, upload the correction."

The correction went out. Eleven seconds of light-speed lag. Then the
telemetry shifted. The two hundred collectors in cluster 3-Alpha-7700
through 7900 adjusted their tilt rates, slowing by 1.2 degrees to match
the cascade schedule. The shadow over Caloris steadied, wavered, and
began to retreat.

Vega watched her console for ten minutes without speaking. Then she said,
quietly, "Deviation falling. Zero-point-four degrees. Zero-point-three.
Returning to model."

Tanaka exhaled. She had not been aware that she was holding her breath.

                              * * *

The cascade completed on Day Eleven, at 2347 UTC on April 14th, 2107.

Nine hundred and twelve million collectors settled into their new
orientations, their mirrored faces angled to create the transparency
corridors that Tanaka had designed in six sleepless weeks. The corridors
were invisible from any single vantage point -- they were gaps in a
structure that was itself too vast to perceive as a whole. But their
effects were immediate and measurable.

Mercury's surface temperature stabilized within forty-eight hours and
began a slow recovery that Tanaka's models predicted would return to
pre-swarm baselines within eighteen months. Venus reported similar
stabilization. The Venusian Atmospheric Authority confirmed that the
sulfuric acid cycle was normalizing. Mars issued a statement expressing
cautious satisfaction, which for Mars was practically a standing ovation.

Total energy collection had dropped by 6.2 percent -- slightly more than
projected, but the power grid absorbed the reduction without disruption.
Earth's energy costs rose by an amount that was statistically significant
and practically invisible. The lights stayed on. The factories hummed. The
cities glowed.

Tanaka stood at the observation window of Sunward Station and watched the
swarm in its new configuration. The transparency corridors were there,
if you knew where to look -- subtle variations in the density of silver
coins against the black, like the spaces between words on a page. They
were, she thought, the most important thing she had ever built: not the
collectors themselves, but the gaps between them. The places where she had
chosen to let the light through.

                              V.

The commendation ceremony was held three months later, in the same
amphitheater where she had presented the original crisis data. The
governing board awarded her the Initiative's highest honor, the Solar
Cross, which was a platinum medallion in the shape of a stylized sun
surrounded by six small planets. Thirty-nine delegates voted in favor.
Eight abstained. Mars voted yes, which stunned everyone.

Commander Voss attended as Mercury's official representative. He shook
Tanaka's hand afterward, in front of the cameras, and said, "You kept
your word."

"You kept yours," she said.

Esperanza Vega was promoted to chief engineer of Mercury's orbital
monitoring service. She sent Tanaka a message that read, in its entirety:
"Thank you for not halting the cascade. I would not have blamed you if
you had."

Tanaka kept the message on her desk, next to the original resonance maps
and the eight-centimeter stack of printout that had started the whole
thing. She kept working. The swarm needed constant attention -- minor
adjustments, maintenance cycles, the occasional collector replacement.
And there were new proposals on her desk: extensions, optimizations,
second-generation designs that pushed the collection envelope even further.

She reviewed each proposal carefully. She ran the numbers. She checked
the margins. And she asked, each time, the question that the first
twenty-three years had taught her was the most important question an
engineer could ask:

What happens if it works too well?

The swarm turned in the sunlight, nine hundred and twelve million panels
catching and redirecting the energy of a star. Beneath them, the inner
planets warmed. The corridors held. The light came through.

It was, Tanaka decided, good engineering.

                            [THE END]

                          THE EMISSARY

                        by Theodore Cain

================================================================================

                              I.

The alien ambassador arrived at the United Nations compound in Geneva on a
Tuesday morning in April, and the first thing it did was rearrange the
lobby furniture.

David Ng, senior protocol officer for the UN's newly formed Office of
Extraterrestrial Affairs, watched through the security monitor as the
being -- a shimmering column of amber light approximately two meters tall
and one meter in diameter -- drifted through the main entrance, paused in
the center of the lobby, and began to move things. The reception desk
shifted three meters to the left. The row of leather chairs along the
north wall migrated to the south wall. The potted ficus tree that had
stood in the corner for as long as anyone could remember levitated six
inches off the ground, rotated forty-five degrees, and set itself down
again with a soft thud.

The security team, to their credit, did not open fire. They had been
briefed. They had been told that the Emissary -- the name the being had
given itself in its initial radio transmission, or rather, the closest
English approximation of a concept that had taken three linguists and a
mathematician two weeks to decode -- would communicate through actions
rather than words. They had been told to observe, not to intervene.

They observed. They observed the reception desk moving to a position that
blocked the east corridor. They observed the leather chairs arranging
themselves in a tight circle facing inward. They observed the ficus tree
settling into its new orientation with an air of botanical satisfaction.

"What does it mean?" asked Undersecretary Marguerite Fleury, who was
standing next to Ng in the monitoring room, gripping her briefing folder
with the intensity of someone holding a life preserver.

"I don't know," Ng said. He was a careful man -- Hong Kong-born, Oxford-
educated, with twenty years of diplomatic experience spanning four
continents and seven languages, none of which appeared to be relevant to
the current situation. His training had prepared him to interpret tone,
gesture, facial expression, cultural context, and the seventeen varieties
of diplomatic silence. It had not prepared him for furniture.

"Can you guess?" Fleury pressed.

"In diplomacy, Undersecretary, guessing is what starts wars."

"In diplomacy, Mr. Ng, not guessing is what prolongs them."

He conceded the point with a slight nod. "It moved the desk to block the
east corridor. The east corridor leads to the administrative offices --
bureaucracy, in other words. Blocking it could mean: 'I do not wish to
deal with your bureaucracy.' Or it could mean: 'I wish to protect your
bureaucracy from something.' Or it could mean that the desk was in a
position that the Emissary found aesthetically displeasing."

"Aesthetically displeasing."

"We have no basis for ruling it out."

"What about the chairs?"

"A circle facing inward suggests conversation. Intimacy. 'Let us sit
together and talk.' Or possibly: 'I am containing you.' Or possibly: 'I
find right angles offensive.'"

"And the tree?"

"Madam Undersecretary, I have no theory about the tree."

                              * * *

The welcoming ceremony had been planned for months. It was the most
carefully choreographed diplomatic event in human history, and it was
held in Conference Room Seven, which the advance team had spent six weeks
preparing. The room featured a custom-built table (round, to avoid
implications of hierarchy), seating for forty-eight delegates (each chair
equidistant from the center, to avoid implications of hierarchy), and a
precisely calibrated ambient temperature of 20 degrees Celsius (selected
because the Emissary had, in its radio transmissions, expressed comfort
with "the mid-range of your thermal spectrum," which three teams of
scientists had debated for a week before agreeing on 20 degrees, plus or
minus one).

The Emissary entered Conference Room Seven at 10:00 AM sharp and
immediately disassembled the table.

Ng watched from his position near the door as the amber column of light
drifted to the center of the room, paused, and then -- delicately,
precisely, without any visible tool or appendage -- began removing the
table's components. The surface lifted off the base. The base separated
into its four legs. The legs drifted to the corners of the room and stood
upright, like sentinels. The surface hovered for a moment at chest height,
then descended gently to the floor, where it lay flat, like a low
platform or a stage.

The forty-eight delegates sat in their equidistant chairs and stared.

Secretary-General Andrade, who was chairing the ceremony, looked at Ng
with an expression that communicated, in the universal language of
bureaucratic panic: *Do something.*

Ng stepped forward. He had prepared a welcoming address -- three paragraphs
of carefully vetted prose that had been reviewed by sixteen committees and
translated into Mandarin, Arabic, French, Spanish, Russian, and the
mathematical notation that the Emissary's radio transmissions had used.
He had memorized it in all seven versions. He now suspected that none of
them would be relevant.

"Emissary," he said, addressing the column of light. "On behalf of the
people of Earth, I welcome you. We are honored by your presence. We hope
that this meeting will be the beginning of a lasting understanding between
our peoples."

The Emissary pulsed. Its amber light brightened, then dimmed, then
brightened again in a rhythm that Ng could not interpret. Then it moved to
the nearest delegate -- the representative from Brazil -- and gently
lifted his briefcase off his lap. The briefcase drifted to the center of
the room and settled on the flat table surface. The Emissary opened it.

Inside the briefcase were the usual accoutrements of a diplomat: papers,
pens, a calculator, a packet of breath mints, and a photograph of the
delegate's family. The Emissary removed each item and arranged them on
the table surface in a pattern that was not random but which Ng could not
immediately decode. The papers went in a fan-shaped array. The pens formed
a small triangle. The calculator was placed equidistant between the papers
and the pens. The breath mints were stacked in a small tower. The family
photograph was propped upright, facing outward, at the arrangement's
center.

The Emissary pulsed again, then moved to the next delegate.

Over the next ninety minutes, the Emissary repeated this process with
every briefcase, handbag, portfolio, and jacket pocket in the room. It
was methodical, unhurried, and scrupulously gentle -- not a single item
was damaged, not a single document was creased. The delegates sat in their
chairs and allowed it to happen, partly because they had been instructed
not to interfere and partly because there was something about the
Emissary's movements that was unexpectedly soothing, like watching a
very slow, very purposeful tide.

When it finished, the table surface was covered with a mosaic of personal
items arranged in interlocking patterns. Family photos formed a ring at
the center, all facing outward. Writing implements radiated from the ring
like spokes. Papers fanned around the edges in overlapping arcs. One
delegate's reading glasses sat balanced on another delegate's cigarette
case, forming a small, improbable sculpture.

Ng stared at it. He stared at it for a long time.

"It's a portrait," he said quietly.

Fleury, who had materialized at his elbow, said, "What?"

"A portrait. Of us. It's used our possessions to make a portrait of what
we are. The families at the center -- because that's what's most important
to us. The tools of communication radiating outward -- because that's what
we do, we reach out. The papers around the edges -- our ideas, overlapping,
connecting. The glasses on the cigarette case -- our contradictions, our
vanities, our fragile balancing acts."

Fleury was silent for a moment. "Or it's sorting our things by density."

"It could be sorting our things by density."

"How do we find out?"

"We keep watching."

                              II.

The banquet was scheduled for that evening, in the Grand Dining Hall. The
catering team had agonized for weeks over the menu, eventually settling
on a selection of dishes from every major culinary tradition, presented
on neutral white plates with no national insignia. The wine had been
chosen by a committee. The flowers had been chosen by a different
committee. The seating chart had been the subject of what one exhausted
aide described as "the most intense negotiation since Yalta."

The Emissary arrived in the dining hall thirty minutes before the guests
and deconstructed the buffet.

Ng, who had posted himself in the dining hall's service corridor in
anticipation of exactly this kind of development, watched through the
cracked door as the amber column dismantled the evening's meal with the
precision of a master chef working in reverse. Serving dishes lifted off
the buffet table. Lids removed themselves. Foods separated from their
plates and arranged themselves in the air -- hovering, rotating slowly,
as though being examined.

The Emissary spent particular attention on a roast duck. It lifted the
bird off its platter, rotated it slowly through three axes, and then --
with a delicacy that Ng found almost tender -- separated the meat from
the bones. The bones drifted to one side of the room. The meat drifted
to the other. The skin floated to a point midway between them and hung
there, translucent in the light.

Then it did the same to a wheel of Gruyere. And a bowl of couscous. And
a platter of sushi. And a tureen of borscht, which was particularly
impressive, as the Emissary managed to suspend the liquid in a perfect
sphere, rotating slowly, with the sour cream forming a white spiral
through the red like a small galaxy.

"It's analyzing our food," said Dr. Kenji Mori, the xenobiologist who had
been assigned to Ng's observation team. Mori was pressed against the
door beside Ng, his eyes wide, his voice a hoarse whisper. "It's breaking
down the components. Proteins, carbohydrates, fats, minerals. It's
studying how we nourish ourselves."

"Or it's offended by the presentation," Ng said.

"Nobody is offended by the presentation. We spent three weeks on the
presentation."

"Dr. Mori, this being communicates by rearranging furniture. I think we
can safely say that presentation matters to it."

They watched as the Emissary completed its deconstruction. Every dish had
been separated into its constituent parts, and those parts hung in the
air throughout the dining hall in a three-dimensional map of human
cuisine. Grains clustered near the ceiling. Proteins hung at eye level.
Vegetables and fruits formed a ring around the room's perimeter. Liquids --
wine, water, broth, sauces -- orbited each other in the center of the
room in a slow, stately dance.

And then, as Ng watched, the Emissary began to reassemble.

But not as the catering team had arranged things. The Emissary combined
ingredients across traditions, across continents, across every cultural
boundary that the carefully neutral menu had been designed to respect.
Sushi rice wrapped around lamb from the Moroccan tagine. Borscht became
the sauce for the Peking duck. The Indian naan folded itself around the
Greek dolmas. The French Gruyere melted over the Japanese tempura.

When it finished, the buffet table held a single, continuous meal -- one
long composition of food that flowed from one end of the table to the
other without interruption, each element merging into the next, every
tradition present but none separate. It was, Ng realized, the culinary
equivalent of the lobby's furniture arrangement and the conference room's
personal-item mosaic: a portrait of humanity as a single thing, its
components rearranged to reveal a unity that the original arrangement had
obscured.

Or it was a being that liked to play with its food. Ng was not ruling
anything out.

                              * * *

The delegates arrived for the banquet and found their meal transformed.
Most of them stared. Several asked questions. The delegate from France
looked at the Gruyere-tempura with an expression that suggested he was
reconsidering his commitment to interspecies diplomacy.

Secretary-General Andrade looked at Ng. "Can we eat it?"

Ng looked at Mori. Mori looked at the food. "The nutritional content is
unchanged. It's just been... recombined."

"Is it safe?"

"The Emissary handled every ingredient with more care than our catering
staff. If anything, it's more hygienic than it was before."

Andrade nodded. "Then we eat it."

They ate it. And here was the thing that Ng had not expected, the thing
that made him stop mid-bite and set down his fork and look at the Emissary
with something that was not understanding, exactly, but was the first
distant glimmer of it: the food was good. Not just edible, not just
nutritionally adequate, but *good*. The combinations that should have been
absurd -- the borscht-duck, the naan-dolmas, the rice-lamb fusion --
worked. They worked because the Emissary had understood something about
the individual flavors that the original chefs, bound by tradition and
convention, had not thought to explore. It had found harmonies across
boundaries. It had heard a chord where they had heard separate notes.

The French delegate was on his third helping of the Gruyere-tempura.

                              III.

After the banquet, Ng sat in his office and wrote his report. It was, he
suspected, the strangest diplomatic brief in the history of the profession.

He wrote: "The Emissary communicates by rearranging the physical world.
It does not speak, it does not write, it does not gesture in any way that
our kinesics experts can interpret. Instead, it takes what it finds --
furniture, personal possessions, food -- and reorganizes it to express
ideas. The mode of communication is the rearrangement itself."

He paused. He crossed out the last sentence and replaced it: "The mode of
communication IS the rearrangement itself. Not a symbol for an idea. The
idea itself, made tangible."

He continued: "Based on three observed rearrangements (lobby, conference
room, banquet), I offer the following preliminary interpretation: the
Emissary is expressing a single, consistent message. That message,
translated as closely as I am able into diplomatic language, is: 'You are
one thing. You have divided yourselves into many things -- nations,
cultures, traditions, hierarchies. But you are one thing. I can see it.
Let me show you.'"

He put down his pen and rubbed his eyes. Twenty years of diplomacy. Seven
languages. Four continents. Seventeen varieties of silence. And the most
eloquent communicator he had ever encountered was a being that couldn't
say a word.

                              * * *

The piano was the last thing.

It stood in the corner of the reception lounge on the compound's third
floor -- a Steinway concert grand, donated by the Austrian delegation
in 1949, kept in tune by a blind Swiss technician named Herr Gruber who
came every Thursday and who refused to discuss the instrument in anything
other than German, regardless of the questioner's nationality.

The Emissary found the piano on the morning of its third day. Ng was not
present for this -- he was in a meeting with Fleury, reviewing the
overnight surveillance footage -- but the security cameras captured it.

The amber column drifted into the reception lounge, paused in the center
of the room, and then moved to the piano. It circled the instrument
slowly, as though examining it from every angle. Then it opened the lid.

It did not play the piano. What it did was, by Ng's later estimation,
considerably more interesting.

It disassembled the piano.

Not destructively. Not violently. With the same delicate precision it had
shown with the buffet, it separated the Steinway into its component parts.
The keys lifted from their beds and arranged themselves in a long,
floating line, white and black alternating in a pattern that was not the
standard keyboard layout but something else -- something that Ng, watching
the footage later, could not immediately identify. The hammers detached
from their mechanisms and formed a circle in the air, their felt heads
pointing inward. The strings -- all two hundred and thirty of them --
unwound from their pins and stretched themselves across the room like a
harp made of light, each string vibrating at its natural frequency, filling
the lounge with a sound that was not music, exactly, but was not not music
either. It was the sound of the piano's potential -- every note it could
play, every harmony it could produce, all sounding simultaneously in a
shimmer of overtones that the security microphones could barely capture.

And in the center of it all, the soundboard -- the curved wooden heart of
the instrument, the surface that gave the piano its voice -- hovered at
chest height, glowing faintly with reflected amber light. Ng could see,
in the footage, that the Emissary had positioned the soundboard so that
the vibrations from the suspended strings resonated through it, and the
resonance was not random. It was shaped. The Emissary was using the
deconstructed piano to produce a sound that a fully assembled piano could
not make -- a sound that required the instrument's components to be free
of each other, each element contributing its individual voice to a whole
that was greater than the sum of its parts.

Ng played the footage four times. On the fourth viewing, he noticed
something he had missed before. The keys, arranged in their floating line,
were not in a random order. They were arranged by frequency -- lowest to
highest -- but the white and black keys were interleaved differently than
on a standard keyboard. On a standard keyboard, the arrangement reflected
the Western diatonic scale, with its pattern of whole and half steps. The
Emissary's arrangement had no half steps. Every interval was equal. It was
a keyboard redesigned for a scale that treated every note as equivalent --
no sharps, no flats, no hierarchy of tones.

No hierarchy.

Ng sat back in his chair and laughed. It was the first time he had laughed
since the Emissary arrived, and it startled Fleury, who was reading over
his shoulder.

"What is it?" she asked.

"It's the same message," Ng said. "The same message it's been saying since
it walked through the door. 'You are one thing. Let me show you.' The
lobby, the conference room, the food, the piano -- it's all the same
message, in different languages. It's learning our languages -- furniture,
food, music -- and using each one to say the same thing."

"Which is?"

"'You have built walls between yourselves, and the walls are not real. Let
me take them down and show you what you look like without them.'"

Fleury considered this. "That's a very optimistic interpretation."

"It is."

"It could also be saying: 'Your divisions are inefficient, and I am
correcting them.'"

"It could."

"Or: 'I am demonstrating my power by taking your things apart.'"

"Also possible."

"So which is it?"

Ng looked at the frozen image on his screen: the piano's strings stretched
across the lounge like bridges, the keys floating in their egalitarian
line, the soundboard glowing at the center of it all, making music that no
assembled instrument could make.

"I don't know," he said. "But I want to find out. And I think -- I think
that wanting to find out is the right response. I think that's what it's
hoping for."

"You're guessing."

"Yes. In diplomacy, Undersecretary, guessing is sometimes how
understanding begins."

Fleury looked at him for a long moment. Then she smiled -- the first
genuine smile he had seen from her since the Emissary's arrival -- and
said, "Then I suggest you keep guessing, Mr. Ng."

He did. He kept guessing. And the Emissary kept rearranging. And slowly,
one piece of furniture at a time, they began to understand each other.

Herr Gruber, informed that the piano had been disassembled, said only:
"If it puts it back together in better tune than I left it, I will be
very annoyed."

It did. He was.

                            [THE END]

                       THE PROMETHEUS VERDICT

                       by Dr. Vincent Koslov

================================================================================

                              I.

The paper was seven pages long and contained no equations that a competent
graduate student could not follow. This was, in retrospect, the most
dangerous thing about it.

Dr. Adela Ruiz submitted "On the Extraction of Zero-Point Energy from the
Quantum Vacuum: A Practical Framework" to the Physical Review Letters on a
Monday morning in September, uploading it through the journal's electronic
submission portal from her office at the University of São Paulo. The
paper described a method for drawing effectively unlimited energy from the
quantum vacuum -- the seething, invisible substrate of empty space, where
virtual particles flickered in and out of existence in accordance with
Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. Physicists had known for decades that
the vacuum contained energy. What Ruiz demonstrated, in seven pages of
lucid prose and straightforward mathematics, was how to get it out.

She submitted the paper at 9:14 AM São Paulo time. By 9:47, the journal's
automated system had forwarded it to three peer reviewers. By 10:30, two
of the three reviewers had read it and independently concluded that the
physics was correct. By 11:15, the third reviewer -- a Nobel laureate
at MIT who had spent his career arguing that vacuum energy extraction was
impossible -- had read it, checked the math twice, and sent a one-line
email to the journal's editor-in-chief that read: "She's right. God
help us."

By noon, the paper had been forwarded, through channels that were
technically confidential but practically porous, to the physics divisions
of seven national governments. By 3:00 PM, those governments had
classified it. By 6:00 PM, Dr. Adela Ruiz had been arrested.

                              * * *

The arresting officers were polite. There were four of them -- two from
the Brazilian Federal Police and two from an agency that declined to
identify itself. They arrived at Ruiz's office at 6:22 PM, showed her a
warrant that cited national security statutes she had never heard of, and
asked her to accompany them. They did not handcuff her. They did not raise
their voices. They addressed her as "Professora" and allowed her to
collect her coat, her bag, and a photograph of her daughter that she kept
on her desk.

She was taken to a federal building in downtown São Paulo, escorted to
a windowless room on the fourteenth floor, and left alone for two hours.
The room contained a table, two chairs, and a carafe of water. There was
no clock. She drank the water and waited.

At 8:40 PM, a man entered the room and introduced himself as Director
Cardoso of the National Science Security Division -- an organization
Ruiz had not known existed. He was a trim, gray-haired man in a dark
suit, and he carried a folder that contained, Ruiz could see, a printout
of her paper.

"Dr. Ruiz," Cardoso said, sitting down across from her, "do you
understand why you are here?"

"Because I published a physics paper."

"Because you published a physics paper that describes how to build an
unlimited energy source from materials available at any university
laboratory." Cardoso opened the folder and turned to the second page.
"Section Three of your paper specifies the apparatus. A superconducting
resonance cavity, a coherent field oscillator, and a quantum decoherence
amplifier. You estimate the total materials cost at approximately forty
thousand U.S. dollars."

"Thirty-eight thousand, if you shop carefully."

Cardoso did not smile. "Dr. Ruiz, are you aware that every government that
has reviewed your paper has reached the same conclusion?"

"That it's correct?"

"That it is catastrophically dangerous."

Ruiz was quiet for a moment. She had anticipated this reaction -- not the
arrest, which had genuinely surprised her, but the fear. She had known,
when she completed the derivation six months ago, that the implications
would terrify people. Unlimited energy meant unlimited power. It meant
the end of oil, coal, natural gas, nuclear fission, solar panels, and
every other energy infrastructure on Earth. It meant the end of energy
scarcity, which meant the end of the economic systems built on energy
scarcity, which meant the end of -- well, that was where the projections
diverged, depending on whether you were an optimist or a pessimist.

"Director Cardoso," she said, "I am a physicist. I discovered a physical
law. I published it because that is what physicists do."

"Physicists who work on nuclear weapons do not publish their results."

"I did not build a weapon. I described a phenomenon."

"The phenomenon you described will allow any nation, any corporation, any
sufficiently motivated individual to generate enough energy to power a
city. Or to destroy one. The distinction, Dr. Ruiz, lies in the
application, and application is precisely what your paper enables."

"Then the danger lies in the application, not the knowledge."

Cardoso leaned back in his chair. He had the patient expression of a man
who had conducted this type of conversation before -- perhaps not about
physics, but about the boundary between knowledge and its consequences.

"Dr. Ruiz, every major government on Earth has classified your paper. It
has been removed from the Physical Review's submission queue. The three
peer reviewers have been contacted and asked to treat their knowledge of
the paper's contents as classified information. Your university's computer
systems are being searched for copies, and your personal devices will be
examined. The goal is containment."

"You can't contain a physical law," Ruiz said. "I discovered it. Someone
else will discover it. The mathematics is not obscure. It follows from
principles that are taught in every graduate program in quantum field
theory. If you suppress my paper, you are buying time, not preventing
discovery."

"Time," Cardoso said, "is sometimes all we can buy."

                              II.

The trial began four months later, in a closed courtroom in Brasilia.

It was not called a trial. The official designation was "Inquiry into
Matters of National Scientific Security," because no existing law
specifically prohibited the publication of a physics paper, and the
Brazilian government's legal team had spent four months trying to find --
or create -- a statute under which Ruiz could be charged. They eventually
settled on an obscure provision of the National Defense Act that
prohibited the unauthorized disclosure of "strategic technologies or
processes," interpreting "disclosure" to include publication in a
peer-reviewed journal and "strategic technologies" to include theoretical
physics.

Ruiz's defense attorney was a constitutional lawyer named Beatriz Lourdes,
a tall, sharp-featured woman who had made her reputation defending
journalists and whistleblowers. Lourdes had taken the case pro bono,
telling Ruiz, "This is either the most important free-speech case in
history or the most important science case. Either way, I want it."

The presiding judge was Justice Eliana Tavares, who had been chosen
because she held a doctorate in biochemistry in addition to her law
degree and was therefore considered capable of understanding the technical
arguments. She was a small, composed woman with gray hair and the alert,
skeptical expression of someone who was accustomed to being the smartest
person in the room and polite enough not to mention it.

The courtroom was small and windowless -- a converted conference room in
the federal courthouse, hastily fitted with a judge's bench, tables for
prosecution and defense, and a gallery of twelve seats, all occupied by
representatives of foreign governments who had petitioned for observer
status. The United States, China, Russia, and the European Union each
had a representative. So did Japan, India, Brazil (obviously), and five
smaller nations whose interest in the proceedings was not immediately
clear but who had argued, successfully, that a case involving unlimited
energy was everybody's business.

Director Cardoso sat in the gallery as well, his folder on his lap, his
expression neutral.

The prosecution was led by Federal Prosecutor Helena Braga, a methodical
woman with a voice like a cello, who opened the government's case with
a statement that was, Ruiz had to admit, devastatingly effective.

"This is not a case about science," Braga told the court. "The government
does not dispute the accuracy of Dr. Ruiz's research. We do not question
her mathematics, her methodology, or her conclusions. We accept, for the
purposes of this inquiry, that her paper describes a viable method for
extracting unlimited energy from the quantum vacuum.

"This is a case about responsibility. Specifically: does a scientist have
the right -- the unrestricted, unqualified right -- to release any
discovery into the world, regardless of its consequences? The defense will
argue that knowledge is inherently neutral, that the pursuit and
publication of truth is the foundational principle of science, and that
any attempt to restrict it is a step toward tyranny. These are powerful
arguments, and we do not dismiss them lightly.

"But we ask the court to consider a different question. If a chemist
discovers a formula for a nerve agent that can be synthesized from
household products, does she have the right to publish it? If a biologist
sequences a pathogen that could cause a pandemic, does he have the right
to post the sequence online? If a physicist devises a method for
generating unlimited energy -- energy that can power a civilization or
vaporize a continent -- does she have the right to make that knowledge
available to every government, every corporation, every militia, and
every madman on Earth?

"We argue that she does not. We argue that some discoveries carry
obligations that supersede the right to publish. We argue that Dr. Ruiz,
in submitting her paper without consulting any governmental authority,
without considering the geopolitical implications, without taking any
precaution whatsoever beyond the standard peer-review process, committed
an act of reckless endangerment on a civilizational scale."

                              * * *

Lourdes's opening statement was shorter.

"Your Honor," she said, "the government's argument reduces to a single
proposition: that the truth can be too dangerous to tell. I submit that
this proposition, if accepted, does not merely restrict science. It
abolishes it. Science is the process by which human beings determine what
is true about the physical universe. It requires publication, peer
review, replication, and open debate. Remove any of these elements and you
do not have science. You have alchemy -- knowledge hoarded by the
powerful, dispensed at their discretion, available only to those they
trust.

"My client is a physicist. She discovered a law of nature. She published
it, as physicists have done for four hundred years. The government asks
this court to declare that publication a crime. If the court agrees, then
every physicist, every chemist, every biologist, every scientist in every
discipline must henceforth ask the government's permission before sharing
their work with the world. Knowledge becomes a controlled substance.
Truth becomes contraband.

"The government says this is about responsibility. I agree. It is about
the responsibility of a free society to face the truth, even when the
truth is frightening. Dr. Ruiz gave the world a gift. The government's
response was to arrest the gift-giver."

                              III.

The trial lasted three weeks. Ruiz testified for two days.

She was, the observers agreed, an effective witness -- not because she
was theatrical or eloquent, but because she was precise. She answered
questions in the same clear, unadorned language in which she had written
her paper. When the prosecutor asked her whether she had considered the
military applications of her discovery, she said, "Of course I did." When
asked why she had published anyway, she said, "Because the alternative
was to hide a law of nature, and I do not believe that any individual
or government has the right to decide what the universe is allowed to
tell us."

The prosecution's expert witnesses were impressive. A retired general
testified about the weaponization potential of unlimited energy. An
economist described the global financial disruption that would result
from the collapse of the energy industry. A political scientist argued
that vacuum energy extraction would destabilize the international order
by eliminating the resource dependencies that bound nations into
cooperative frameworks. Each witness was credentialed, articulate, and
persuasive.

The defense called three witnesses: a historian of science, a
constitutional scholar, and a physicist.

The historian, Dr. Miriam Sosa, traced the pattern of scientific
suppression through the centuries. "Galileo," she said. "Semmelweis.
Oppenheimer. Sakharov. Every era produces discoveries that powerful
institutions wish to suppress. Every era produces arguments for why
suppression is necessary, responsible, even moral. And in every case --
every single case -- suppression fails, and the attempt to suppress causes
more harm than the discovery itself. You cannot lock the door of knowledge
after someone has opened it. You can only punish the person who opened it,
and that punishment does not close the door."

The constitutional scholar, Professor Luis Mendez, argued that the
National Defense Act's "strategic technologies" provision was
unconstitutional as applied to basic research. "The government's
interpretation would classify every physics textbook as a potential
national security document," he said. "Quantum field theory is taught at
every major university in the world. The principles underlying Dr. Ruiz's
discovery are in the public domain. Her contribution was not the physics;
it was a particular arrangement of known physics into a novel practical
framework. If that arrangement is criminal, then creativity itself is
criminal."

The physicist was Dr. James Chen of Stanford, who had spent twenty years
researching vacuum energy and who had, by his own admission, been working
toward the same breakthrough that Ruiz had achieved.

"I was approximately two years behind her," Chen testified. "Possibly
less. The theoretical foundations have been in place since the 1990s. The
specific insight that Dr. Ruiz had -- the use of coherent field
oscillation to couple the resonance cavity to the vacuum state -- is
elegant, but it is not obscure. I would have reached it. Others will
reach it. If this court suppresses Dr. Ruiz's paper, the knowledge it
contains will be independently rediscovered within five years. Probably
sooner. The only question is whether the discoverer will be another
academic who publishes in a peer-reviewed journal, or a government
scientist who delivers it directly to the military."

Braga, in cross-examination, asked Chen: "If you had made this discovery,
would you have published it?"

Chen was quiet for a long moment. Then he said, "I don't know. And the
fact that I don't know is exactly why it's important that Dr. Ruiz did."

                              * * *

Justice Tavares delivered her ruling on a Friday afternoon, to a courtroom
that was standing room only. The gallery had expanded to twenty-four
seats, and every one was filled. Director Cardoso sat in his usual place,
his folder closed on his lap.

Tavares spoke for forty minutes. Her opinion was methodical, exhaustive,
and, in its final pages, unexpectedly personal.

She reviewed the legal arguments, finding that the National Defense Act's
"strategic technologies" provision was "constitutionally problematic as
applied to basic research" but not definitively unconstitutional. She
reviewed the expert testimony, noting that both sides had presented
"credible, well-supported arguments that lead to fundamentally
irreconcilable conclusions." She reviewed the factual record, observing
that Dr. Ruiz had followed every standard protocol for academic
publication and had violated no existing regulation.

Then she set aside her prepared text and spoke directly.

"This case asks a question that no court is equipped to answer," she said.
"It asks whether the truth can be too dangerous to know. Not too
dangerous to *use* -- we have laws governing the use of dangerous
knowledge, and they are imperfect but necessary. But too dangerous to
*know*. Too dangerous to publish. Too dangerous to share.

"I have spent three weeks listening to arguments on both sides, and I find
them both compelling. The government is correct that Dr. Ruiz's discovery
has the potential to destabilize the global order. The defense is correct
that the suppression of scientific knowledge is incompatible with the
principles of a free society. Both of these things are true, and they
cannot both prevail.

"But I am a judge, not a philosopher, and I must decide the case before
me. And the case before me is narrow: did Dr. Adela Ruiz commit a crime
by publishing a physics paper? The answer to that question is no. She
did not violate any existing law. She followed every applicable protocol.
She submitted her work to a peer-reviewed journal, which is the standard
and expected practice of her profession. The government's attempt to
retroactively classify her research and charge her under a statute
designed for military secrets is a legal overreach that this court cannot
endorse."

Tavares paused. In the gallery, fourteen governments held their breath.

"Dr. Ruiz is acquitted of all charges. Her paper shall be declassified
and returned to the publication queue of the Physical Review Letters. All
copies of her research that were confiscated during the investigation
shall be returned. Any restrictions placed on the three peer reviewers
shall be lifted immediately."

She paused again. And then she said something that was not in any legal
textbook, something that the court reporters would transcribe and the
newspapers would print and the historians would debate for decades:

"I am aware that this ruling may be wrong. Not legally wrong -- I am
confident in the law. But consequentially wrong. It is possible that this
paper, once published, will lead to destruction that I cannot foresee and
that no law can prevent. I accept that possibility. I accept it because
the alternative -- a world in which governments decide what scientists
are allowed to discover -- frightens me more than any bomb.

"Dr. Ruiz did not create the quantum vacuum. She did not invent the
physical laws that govern it. She observed them, described them, and
shared her observations with the world. If we punish her for that, we are
not protecting civilization. We are blinding it.

"The defendant is free to go."

                              IV.

Ruiz walked out of the courthouse into São Paulo's late-afternoon light.
The sun was low and golden, throwing long shadows across the plaza. A
small crowd had gathered -- journalists, mostly, but also a handful of
students who held signs reading CIENCIA LIVRE and KNOWLEDGE IS NOT A
CRIME. One of them, a young woman in a university sweatshirt, pressed
forward and asked Ruiz to sign her copy of the paper.

Ruiz signed it. Then she stood on the courthouse steps and made a brief
statement to the cameras.

"The court has ruled that the truth is not a crime," she said. "I am
grateful for that ruling. But I want to be clear about something. The
prosecutor was not wrong to be afraid. This discovery will change the
world. It will change the balance of power between nations. It will
eliminate the scarcity that has driven human conflict for millennia. And
yes, it will create new dangers that we cannot fully predict.

"But the solution to dangerous knowledge is not less knowledge. It is
more wisdom. And wisdom is not something that governments can classify
or courts can suppress. It is something we must build, together, in the
open, where everyone can see it and everyone can contribute to it.

"My paper is seven pages long. The work of making its discovery safe and
beneficial will require the efforts of millions. That work begins now."

She descended the steps and walked to a waiting car. Director Cardoso
was standing beside it.

"Congratulations, Dr. Ruiz," he said. His tone was not bitter. It was
not warm, either. It was the tone of a man who had done his job, had
lost, and was preparing to do his job differently.

"Director."

"For what it's worth, I hope the judge is right."

"So do I."

He opened the car door for her. She got in. As the car pulled away, she
looked back through the rear window and saw Cardoso standing on the
sidewalk, his folder under his arm, watching her go. Behind him, the
courthouse caught the last of the day's light, its windows blazing orange,
its shadow stretching long across the plaza toward the east, where the
evening was already beginning and the first stars were becoming visible
in the darkening sky.

Stars that burned, she knew now, with the same energy that filled the
empty spaces between them. Energy that had always been there, patient and
vast and waiting. Waiting for someone to notice.

She had noticed.

Now everyone would.

                            [THE END]

                      THE FOXHOUND OF MARS

                        by Rex Drummond

================================================================================

                              I.

Kit Ravenal tracked the fugitive into the Valles Marineris on the third
day, and that was where things went sideways.

She'd picked up the contract on Burroughs Station -- a standard skip-trace,
posted by the Colonial Authority, offering five thousand credits for the
live recovery of one Mikael Drost, wanted for sedition, incitement, and
"acts prejudicial to colonial order," which was the Authority's polite way
of saying he'd made somebody important very angry. The bounty was decent.
The target's file photo showed a thin, tired-looking man in his forties
with academic's spectacles and the kind of face that belonged behind a
desk, not on the run in the Martian outback. Easy money, Kit figured.

She figured wrong.

Drost had a three-day head start, a stolen crawler, and -- Kit discovered
when she found the crawler abandoned at the rim of Coprates Chasma --
either a death wish or a better knowledge of the Martian backcountry than
his file suggested. Nobody drove a crawler into Coprates. The canyon was
eight kilometers deep, the walls were vertical basalt, and the floor was a
maze of rockfall, dust drifts, and the ruins of what the archaeologists
called Pre-Human Site Seven, which everyone else called the Bone Yard on
account of the fossilized structures that jutted from the canyon floor
like the ribs of some vast, buried animal.

Kit stood at the canyon rim, her boots crunching on iron-red regolith,
and looked down. The wind -- thin and mean, carrying a freight of dust
that sandblasted any exposed skin -- whipped the tails of her duster coat
and tried to push her over the edge. Her rebreather mask fogged with each
exhale. Through the fog, the canyon yawned below her, its depths lost in
amber haze.

"Son of a gun," she said to nobody.

She went over the side.

                              * * *

Kit Ravenal was thirty-four years old, Mars-born, and built for the
gravity -- lean and long-limbed, with the ropy muscle of someone who'd
grown up at 0.38 g and had never set foot on Earth. She'd been a Colonial
Authority deputy for six years before she went freelance, and the reason
she went freelance was the same reason most deputies went freelance: she
got tired of arresting people for crimes that weren't crimes.

That thought came back to her now, climbing down the canyon wall with her
gecko-grips biting into the basalt and eight kilometers of nothing below
her. Sedition. Incitement. Acts prejudicial to colonial order. The
Authority's menu of charges for people who said things the Authority
didn't like. She'd arrested two dozen of them in her deputy years --
journalists, union organizers, a schoolteacher who'd written a letter to
the Phobos Gazette about water rations. Every one of them had gone
quietly. Every one of them had looked at her with the same expression:
not anger, not fear, but disappointment. As if they'd expected better
from someone who wore the badge.

She'd turned in the badge after the schoolteacher. Went freelance. Took
contracts for skip-traces and asset recovery -- honest work, or at least
work where you didn't have to think too hard about who deserved what.
Fugitives ran. She caught them. The Authority paid her. Clean and simple.

Except when it wasn't.

She reached the canyon floor in four hours, rappelling the last five
hundred meters on a carbon-fiber line anchored to a basalt outcrop that
she hoped was sturdier than it looked. The floor of Coprates was a
tumbled chaos of boulders, sand drifts, and the strange, pale structures
of the Bone Yard -- curved spars of fossilized something that arched
out of the ground at odd angles, some of them twenty meters tall, their
surfaces pitted by a million years of wind. The archaeologists said they
were organic. The structure was cellular, layered, grown rather than
built. Something had lived here, once, something enormous, and it had
left its bones behind.

Kit checked her tracker. Drost's biosignature -- the Colonial Authority
tagged all its persons of interest with subcutaneous transponders, a
practice that the civil liberties crowd objected to and the Authority
defended as "a public safety measure" -- was pulsing faintly, two
kilometers to the northwest, somewhere in the densest part of the Bone
Yard.

She drew her sidearm, a Matsuda Model 7 flechette pistol that she'd
carried for eight years and maintained with the tenderness of a parent.
Then she started walking.

                              II.

The bandit camp was the first surprise.

It sat in a hollow between two massive fossil spars, a cluster of
inflatable habitat modules arranged around a central fire pit -- which,
on Mars, meant a catalytic heater glowing cherry-red under a thermal
blanket. There were a dozen people visible, all of them armed with an
assortment of weapons that ranged from military-surplus plasma rifles to
mining tools that had been modified with more enthusiasm than expertise.
They had lookouts posted on the fossil spars, which Kit spotted only
because one of them shifted position at the wrong moment and caught the
light.

Kit went to ground behind a boulder and studied the camp through her
scope. Bandits were common in the Martian backcountry -- failed miners,
mostly, or colonists who'd gone off-grid rather than submit to the
Authority's ration system. They survived by raiding supply convoys and
trading stolen goods through a network of black-market depots that
stretched from Hellas Basin to the north pole. Most of them were more
desperate than dangerous, but a cornered bandit with a plasma rifle was
still a cornered bandit with a plasma rifle.

Drost's tracker signal was coming from inside the camp.

Kit considered her options. She could wait for Drost to leave the camp
and take him in the open. She could circle the camp, look for a gap in
the lookout coverage, and slip in at night. She could call for Authority
backup, which would arrive in approximately six days and would approach
the situation with all the subtlety of a sledgehammer.

She chose option two.

Night on Mars came fast and cold. The temperature at the bottom of
Coprates dropped to minus ninety Celsius after sunset, which was cold
enough to crystallize the CO2 in the thin atmosphere and coat everything
with a fine white frost. Kit pulled her thermal liner tight, sealed her
suit joints, and moved.

The lookouts were good but not great. They watched the approaches -- the
gaps between the fossil spars where a crawler or a group of people might
come through. They did not watch the spars themselves. Kit climbed.

The fossil structures were rough-surfaced and easy to grip, and in Mars
gravity she could move fast. She went up the western spar, over its
curving ridge, and down the inner face, arriving on a narrow ledge
fifteen meters above the camp. From here she could see the entire
settlement: the habitat modules, the heater, the people moving between
them. And Drost.

He was sitting outside one of the modules, wrapped in a thermal blanket,
talking to a woman. The woman was tall, dark-haired, and wore the
patched coveralls of a backcountry colonist. She was listening to Drost
with the focused attention of someone hearing something important.

Kit couldn't hear the conversation. She was too high, and the thin
Martian atmosphere carried sound poorly. But she could see Drost's face,
and it was not the face of a man on the run. It was the face of a man
who had arrived where he was going.

She settled in to wait. Dawn would come in five hours. She'd take him
then, when the camp was still waking and the lookouts were at their
groggiest. Standard procedure. Quick and clean.

She waited. And while she waited, she watched. And what she saw changed
everything.

                              * * *

The camp woke before dawn. Not gradually, not lazily, but with purpose.
People emerged from the habitat modules already dressed, already moving,
already organized. They assembled around the heater and ate a quick
meal from ration packs, passing food with the casual efficiency of
people who had done this many times. Then they broke into groups and
went to work.

Work. That was the word that stopped Kit.

They were building something. In the pale pre-dawn light, she could see
materials stacked along the base of the eastern spar -- structural beams,
insulation panels, air processors, water recyclers. Not stolen goods.
Construction materials. The kind of equipment you needed to build a
permanent settlement.

One group was excavating a foundation. Another was assembling an air
processor housing. A third was running conduit -- power lines and data
cables, buried in shallow trenches cut into the canyon floor. It was
organized, systematic, competent work, performed by people who knew what
they were doing.

And Drost was in the middle of it. Not hiding. Not cowering. Teaching. He
stood at the center of a group of six people, a pad in his hand, drawing
diagrams and explaining something with the animated gestures of a man who
loved his subject. The people around him asked questions. He answered them.
Kit could not hear the words, but she could see the dynamic: this was a
class. A lecture. A professor and his students.

She pulled up Drost's file on her wrist display and read it again,
carefully this time. Mikael Drost. Age forty-three. Born Elysium Colony.
Education: Ph.D. in civil engineering, Olympus University. Employment:
Associate Professor of Engineering, Olympus University. Charge: Sedition,
incitement, acts prejudicial to colonial order.

She scrolled to the charge details. They were thin. Drost had published
a paper -- not a physics paper, not a weapons paper, an engineering
paper -- arguing that the Colonial Authority's settlement design
standards were inadequate for long-term habitation. He had presented data
showing that the Authority's habitat modules, which were manufactured on
Earth and shipped to Mars at enormous cost, were inferior in every
measurable way to structures that could be built locally from Martian
materials using techniques he had developed. He had argued that the
Authority's monopoly on habitat construction was not a safety measure but
an economic control mechanism -- that by keeping the colonists dependent
on Earth-manufactured housing, the Authority maintained the dependency
that justified its authority.

The paper had been published in the Olympus University Journal of
Engineering. It had been read by colonists across Mars. It had been
discussed, debated, and -- in several outlying settlements -- acted
upon. Colonists had begun building their own habitats, using Drost's
designs, without Authority approval.

The Authority had responded by revoking Drost's academic appointment,
confiscating his research, and issuing a warrant for his arrest on charges
of sedition.

Kit lowered her wrist display and looked at the camp below her. The
excavation crew was making good progress on the foundation. The air
processor housing was taking shape. The conduit team was testing their
power connections. And Drost was teaching six people how to build a life
on Mars without asking permission from Earth.

She holstered the Matsuda.

                              III.

She came down at noon, in plain sight, her hands visible, her weapon
holstered.

The lookouts spotted her immediately. Three people with plasma rifles
materialized from behind the fossil spars and covered her from angles
that were, she noted professionally, well-chosen. The tall woman she'd
seen talking to Drost the night before stepped forward, her hand on a
sidearm at her hip.

"That's close enough," the woman said. "Who are you?"

"Kit Ravenal. I'm a skip-tracer."

The rifles tightened. Kit kept her hands up and her voice level.

"I'm here for Mikael Drost. I have a Colonial Authority recovery
contract."

The woman's expression didn't change, but something behind her eyes
hardened. "Professor Drost is under our protection."

"I can see that."

"Then you can see that your contract isn't going to be easy to execute."

"I can see that too." Kit paused. "I'd like to talk to him."

"Why?"

"Because I've been watching your camp since last night, and I've seen some
things that don't match the file I was given."

The woman studied her for a long moment. Kit held still and kept her
hands where they could be seen. In her experience, people who were hiding
in canyons with plasma rifles were not interested in rapid movements.

"Mika," the woman called over her shoulder. "Someone here for you."

Drost emerged from behind the air processor housing, wiping his hands
on his coveralls. He looked at Kit with the mild, slightly distracted
expression of a man whose mind was mostly on his work. Then he saw the
skip-tracer badge on her belt and his expression sharpened.

"You're the foxhound," he said.

"The what?"

"That's what they call you bounty hunters in the settlements. Foxhounds.
Because you chase people who aren't foxes."

"I've been called worse." Kit lowered her hands slowly. "Professor Drost,
I've read your file. I've read the charge details. I've watched your
people building down here. And I have a question."

"Ask it."

"The habitats you're designing. The ones you wrote about in your paper.
Do they work?"

Drost blinked. Whatever question he had expected, it wasn't that one.
"Yes. They work. The thermal mass of Martian regolith provides natural
insulation. The basalt fiber reinforcement gives tensile strength
comparable to Earth-standard construction steel. The air processing
systems use locally sourced perchlorates as oxygen feedstock. Everything
I described in my paper has been tested and validated. That's why the
Authority suppressed it."

"Because it works."

"Because it works. If the colonists can build their own habitats from
local materials, they don't need the Authority's prefabs. If they don't
need the prefabs, they don't need the supply ships. If they don't need
the supply ships, they don't need the Authority. The entire colonial
control structure is built on dependency, Ms. Ravenal. My crime was
showing that the dependency is artificial."

Kit looked at the construction site. The foundation was laid. The walls
were going up. In the thin Martian light, the structure looked solid and
real and permanent in a way that the Authority's flimsy inflatable modules
never did.

"Your bounty is five thousand credits," she said.

Drost's expression hardened. "I'm not going back."

"I didn't say you were." She reached into her coat, slowly, and pulled
out her tracker pad. Drost's biosignature pulsed on the screen, bright
and clear. She tapped the display. The signature vanished.

"Your transponder signal just experienced a catastrophic malfunction,"
she said. "Happens sometimes in the deep canyons. All that basalt
interferes with the transmission frequency. Very unfortunate."

Drost stared at her. The woman with the sidearm stared at her. The three
people with the plasma rifles stared at her.

"The Authority will send another foxhound," the woman said.

"They will. But they won't know where to look, because the last tracker
fix puts Drost's signal at the canyon rim, not the canyon floor. And the
rim is four hundred kilometers of broken terrain. It'll take them weeks
to work it out, and by then" -- she gestured at the construction site --
"you'll be dug in."

"Why?" Drost asked. Not suspiciously. Genuinely curious. The question of
a man who studied causes and effects.

Kit looked at him for a long time. She thought about the schoolteacher
she'd arrested five years ago, the one who'd written a letter about water
rations. She thought about the disappointment in that woman's eyes.

"Because I got tired of chasing people whose only crime was being right,"
she said.

She turned and started walking back toward the canyon wall. Behind her,
she heard the tall woman say something to Drost in a low voice. She
didn't catch the words. She didn't need to.

The climb out took five hours. At the top, she stood on the rim and
looked down into Coprates one last time. The camp was invisible from here
-- lost in the amber haze and the maze of ancient bones. But she knew it
was there. And she knew that down in the darkness, in the ribs of a world
that had been dead for a billion years, people were building something
new.

She turned her back on the canyon and started walking. Five thousand
credits, gone. Her reputation with the Authority, damaged. Her conscience,
for the first time in years, clear.

The Martian wind pushed at her back, cold and thin and tasting of iron.
Ahead of her, Burroughs Station was a three-day walk across open desert.
Behind her, the canyon held its secrets in its ancient, stony hands.

Kit Ravenal walked. The foxhound had lost her quarry. And the quarry had
never been a fox.

                            [THE END]

                        THE MEMORY PALACE

                         by Lucian Marsh

================================================================================

                              I.

The patient was dying in the way that old men die in hospitals -- slowly,
unwillingly, his body retreating from the world one system at a time,
like a house shutting its windows against a storm. His name was Edgar
Tolliver. He was seventy-eight years old. He had been a shoe salesman in
Brockton, Massachusetts, for forty-one years, and he had never traveled
farther than Albany.

This made the memories difficult to explain.

Dr. Helen Vassar discovered them on a Tuesday, during a routine
neurological assessment that was, strictly speaking, no longer necessary.
Tolliver had been admitted to Mercy General three weeks earlier with
advanced liver failure, and the prognosis was measured in days rather
than weeks. But Vassar was thorough -- a habit she had developed during
her residency and never abandoned -- and she continued the assessments
because she believed that a patient deserved the full attention of his
physician until the end, regardless of whether that attention could change
the outcome.

She was mapping Tolliver's cognitive function with a portable
encephalograph, placing the cold sensor pads against his temples and
recording the electrical activity of his dying brain, when the readings
went wrong.

Not wrong in the way that suggested equipment failure. Wrong in the way
that suggested she was reading the wrong brain.

The encephalograph measured several things: baseline electrical activity,
response to sensory stimuli, and -- most relevant to cognitive
assessment -- the patterns of activation that occurred when the patient
recalled specific memories. Vassar's standard protocol was to ask a
series of simple prompts: "Think of your childhood home. Think of a
meal you enjoyed. Think of a person you love." The encephalograph
recorded the brain's response, and Vassar compared it to the baseline to
assess the integrity of the patient's memory systems.

Tolliver's responses to the first two prompts were normal. His brain
lit up in the expected regions -- hippocampus, temporal cortex, the
olfactory centers that stored the sense-memories of food and home. The
patterns were weak but coherent, the fading signal of a mind that was
still, despite everything, itself.

The third prompt was where it went wrong.

"Think of a person you love," Vassar said.

Tolliver's brain activated. But not in the expected pattern. Not in any
pattern Vassar had ever seen. The hippocampus fired -- that was normal.
But so did regions of the brain that should not have been involved in
personal memory: the spatial processing centers, the language areas,
sections of the visual cortex that were associated not with memory
retrieval but with real-time visual processing, as though Tolliver were
seeing something that was happening now, in this room, in front of him.

And the signal was strong. Not the weak, attenuated flicker of a dying
brain recalling a faded memory. A strong signal. A signal with the
amplitude and coherence of a young brain at the height of its powers.

Vassar checked the equipment. She recalibrated the sensors. She ran the
prompt again.

The same result. The same impossible signal, blazing in a brain that
should barely have been able to produce a whisper.

"Mr. Tolliver," she said carefully, "who are you thinking of?"

The old man's eyes were closed. His breathing was shallow and slow. When
he spoke, his voice was thin but clear -- clearer than it had been in
days.

"Her name was Caterina," he said. "She wore a blue dress and her hair
smelled of rosemary. We stood on the bridge in Florence and watched the
sunset turn the river into gold."

Vassar looked at him. "You've been to Florence?"

"No," Tolliver said. "I've never been to Florence. But I remember it."

                              * * *

Over the next three days, as Tolliver's body continued its slow
withdrawal from the world, Vassar documented the memories.

There were hundreds of them. They came in waves -- unprompted, vivid,
detailed with a specificity that no imagination could fabricate. Tolliver
described sunsets he had never seen, in cities he had never visited:
the light falling on the rooftops of Prague, the sound of rain on a
tin roof in Rangoon, the smell of jasmine in a garden in Kyoto at
twilight. He described people he had never met -- a woman named Caterina,
a man named Abiodun, a child named Mei who laughed at sparrows. He
described meals he had never eaten, music he had never heard, storms he
had never weathered.

And each memory, when Vassar mapped it on the encephalograph, produced
the same impossible signal: strong, coherent, originating from regions
of the brain that were not Tolliver's own memory centers but something
else, something deeper, something that pulsed with a vitality that his
dying brain could not account for.

Vassar was a methodical woman. She had been trained in the rational
empiricism of medical science, and she did not believe in the
supernatural. She believed in data. And the data was telling her
something that she did not want to hear.

The memories were not Tolliver's. They were too numerous, too varied,
too detailed. A man who had never left Massachusetts could not remember
the taste of mulled wine in a Vienna Christmas market or the sound of
temple bells in the hills above Kathmandu. These memories belonged to
other people -- dozens of them, perhaps hundreds, their experiences
stored in Tolliver's brain like books in a library he had never visited.

But the most disturbing thing was not the memories themselves. It was
their quality. Each one was perfect. Not the faded, compressed,
selectively edited version of events that constitutes normal human
memory, where details blur and timelines compress and the brain fills
gaps with plausible fabrication. These memories were pristine. Complete.
As though they had been preserved by something that did not forget, that
could not forget, that consumed experience the way a collector mounts
butterflies -- pinned, perfect, and forever still.

                              II.

Vassar began her investigation by working backward through Tolliver's
medical history.

He had been a patient at Mercy General for three weeks, but his records
stretched back decades -- the accumulated documentation of a life lived
within the radius of a single town. Childhood vaccinations. A broken arm
at age twelve. Appendectomy at twenty-three. The usual progression of
minor ailments that marked the passage from youth to middle age to
decline. Nothing unusual. Nothing that suggested a cause for foreign
memories in a shoe salesman's brain.

She expanded her search. She contacted Tolliver's family -- a son in
Worcester, a daughter in Springfield, both of whom visited dutifully if
infrequently. Had their father ever exhibited unusual knowledge? Had he
spoken of places he had never been? Had he displayed abilities or
awareness that seemed inconsistent with his experience?

The son said no. The daughter hesitated.

"There was one thing," the daughter said. She was a practical woman in
her fifties, uncomfortable with the conversation, uncertain why a
neurologist was asking about her father's travel history. "When I was
little -- maybe eight or nine -- Dad took me to a museum in Boston. The
Museum of Fine Arts. We were looking at the paintings, and he stopped in
front of one. It was a painting of a bridge. A bridge in Italy, I think.
And he started crying. Not loud. Just tears, running down his face. And
when I asked him what was wrong, he said, 'I remember this place.' And
I said, 'Have you been there?' And he said, 'No. But someone I carry
has.'"

"Someone he carried," Vassar repeated.

"I thought he meant a memory. Like a story someone told him, or a
photograph he'd seen. But now that you're asking, the way he said it was
strange. He said 'someone I carry.' Not 'something.' Someone."

Vassar thanked the daughter and ended the call. She sat in her office, in
the quiet fluorescent light of the hospital's fourth floor, and looked
at the encephalograph readings she had spread across her desk. The
signals were there -- layer upon layer of memory, pulsing in a brain
that was running out of time to hold them. And somewhere beneath those
layers, threaded through them like mycelium through soil, something else.
Not a memory. Not a thought. A presence.

She went back to Tolliver's room.

The old man was awake. His eyes were open, focused on some middle distance
that existed between the ceiling and wherever the memories lived. The
machines around his bed hummed and clicked their clinical lullaby.

"Mr. Tolliver," Vassar said, sitting in the chair beside his bed. "I
need to ask you something, and I need you to answer as precisely as you
can."

"I'll try, Doctor."

"The memories. The ones that aren't yours. When did they start?"

Tolliver was quiet for a long time. The machines measured his silence in
heartbeats and respiration rates.

"I don't remember a time without them," he said finally. "They've always
been there. When I was a boy, I thought everyone had them -- other
people's memories, other people's lives, running alongside your own like
a river running next to a road. I thought that was just how minds worked.
It wasn't until I was older that I realized the memories belonged to
people who had lived before me. People who were dead."

"How do you know they're dead?"

"Because the memories end. Each set of memories -- each person -- they
have a beginning and a middle and then they just stop. Like a book with
the last pages torn out. And after the stop, there's a gap -- a space
of nothing, of dark -- and then a new set begins. A new person. A new
life. New memories filling up the dark."

Vassar felt the hair on her arms rise. She was a rational woman. She
dealt in data. But she was also a physician, and she knew that the body
sometimes knew things that the mind could not articulate, and the
gooseflesh on her arms was her body telling her that she was close to
something that rational empiricism had not prepared her for.

"Mr. Tolliver, how many sets of memories do you carry?"

"I don't know. Dozens. Maybe more. They overlap. They blur into each
other at the edges. But the core of each one is clear -- a life, a
person, the things they saw and tasted and touched and loved. All
preserved. All perfect. All still alive in here." He tapped his temple
with one yellowed finger. "Alive in here, even as the rest of me is
dying."

"And you never told anyone."

"Who would I tell? My wife? My children? 'Hello, I carry the memories
of dead strangers in my head.' They'd have put me in a different kind of
hospital, Dr. Vassar." He paused. "Besides, it wasn't painful. It was
company. A shoe salesman in Brockton, living a small life, and inside my
head a whole world of lives I'd never lived. Sunsets I'd never seen.
People I'd never met. It was like having a library in my skull, and every
book was someone's life, and I could open any one of them and be
somewhere else for a while."

He closed his eyes. The machines noted a slight decrease in heart rate.

"But it's getting louder now," he said. "The memories. As my brain shuts
down, the memories get louder. As if whatever is holding them is losing
its grip and they're spilling out. Or as if whatever is holding them
knows I'm dying and is... is getting ready."

"Ready for what?"

Tolliver opened his eyes and looked at her. And in that look, Vassar
saw something that she would spend the rest of her career trying to
understand: a flicker, behind the old man's faded blue irises, of
something that was not Edgar Tolliver. Something old, and patient, and
very, very hungry.

"Ready to move on," he said.

                              III.

Vassar did not sleep that night. She sat in her office with the
encephalograph data and the medical records and a pot of coffee that
went cold three times, and she constructed a theory that she did not
believe and could not disprove.

The theory was this: something lived in Edgar Tolliver's brain.

Not a tumor. Not an infection. Not a psychiatric condition. Something
else -- an organism, a parasite, a thing that had evolved to inhabit
the neural architecture of the human brain the way a hermit crab
inhabits a shell. It fed on experience. It consumed memories -- not
destroying them, but absorbing them, preserving them with an inhuman
fidelity that no biological memory could match. It lived in the spaces
between neurons, in the electrochemical shadows that conventional
neuroscience did not have the instruments to detect. And when its host
died, it moved to a new one, carrying its accumulated library of
consumed lives with it.

A parasite that fed on human experience. That passed from host to host
across generations, across centuries, growing its collection, adding
life after life to its palace of stolen memory. Edgar Tolliver was its
current host. Before him, there had been others -- the woman named
Caterina who had stood on a bridge in Florence, the man named Abiodun,
the child named Mei who laughed at sparrows. Each one had carried the
parasite, and the parasite had consumed their lives, and when they died
it had moved on, finding a new host, beginning a new collection while
preserving the old.

It was, Vassar thought, the most elegant horror she could imagine. A
thing that ate your life and kept it perfect forever. A thing that
treasured what it consumed. A collector, a curator, a librarian of the
dead, dwelling in the warm dark of the human mind and feeding on the
light of experience.

She returned to Tolliver's room at dawn. The old man was weaker. His
breathing had taken on the irregular, effortful quality that signaled the
body's final retreat. The machines tracked his decline in green and amber
numbers.

"Mr. Tolliver," she said. "I think I know what's happening to you."

"You figured it out." It was not a question.

"There's something in your brain. Something that isn't you. It feeds on
memory. It's been feeding on your memories, and the memories of others
before you, for a very long time."

Tolliver nodded, slowly, the motion costing him effort. "I've known for
years. Not what it is, exactly. But that it's there. I can feel it, the
way you feel your heartbeat -- not as a thought, but as a presence. A
weight. A passenger."

"It's a parasite, Mr. Tolliver."

"Is it?" He smiled -- a thin, exhausted smile that held no bitterness.
"A parasite takes and gives nothing. But it gave me Florence, Dr.
Vassar. It gave me Prague and Kyoto and a hundred sunsets I would never
have seen. It gave me Caterina and Abiodun and Mei. It gave me lives.
Yes, it took my memories too -- I can feel it, the way some of my own
past has gotten clearer, more vivid, more *preserved* than it should be.
It's been eating my life, but it's been keeping it, too. Every moment.
Perfect. Forever."

"That doesn't make it benign."

"No. But it makes it something more complicated than a parasite." He
closed his eyes. "It's a witness, Dr. Vassar. It witnesses human lives.
It consumes them and preserves them and carries them forward. Every
person it has lived in -- their entire experience, everything they ever
saw or felt or loved -- is still alive in here. Still perfect. Still
real. When I die, it will move to someone else, and it will carry me
with it, and I will be part of its palace forever."

"And you're at peace with that?"

"I am a seventy-eight-year-old shoe salesman dying of liver failure in
a hospital in Brockton. I have lived a small life, Dr. Vassar. A
perfectly adequate, thoroughly unremarkable life. And because of the
thing in my head, that life will be preserved with the same fidelity as
a sunset in Florence. My Tuesday afternoons selling oxfords to
businessmen will be kept alongside Caterina's view from the bridge and
Abiodun's harvest songs and Mei's sparrows. My small life, in the
palace, forever."

His voice was fading. The machines registered the decline. His breathing
had become the shallow, irregular rhythm that Vassar recognized as the
body's penultimate act.

"It's getting ready," he whispered. "I can feel it. Gathering. Preparing
to move. When my brain stops, it will reach out -- I don't know how, I've
never understood the mechanism -- and it will find a new host. Someone
nearby. Someone whose brain is still young enough to house it."

Vassar felt the gooseflesh again. She was sitting beside a dying man
in a hospital full of living brains. Patients, nurses, doctors, orderlies
-- all of them potential hosts. All of them unaware that something ancient
and hungry was preparing to move.

"Mr. Tolliver, is there any way to stop it?"

"I don't know. I don't think so. It's survived this long. It's adapted
to us -- to our neurology, our biochemistry, our pattern of living and
dying. It's been doing this for longer than I can imagine. The oldest
memories I carry are fragments -- sensory impressions without language,
without context. Just the experience of being alive, of seeing light and
feeling warmth. They could be centuries old. They could be older."

He opened his eyes one last time. The flicker was there again -- that
presence behind his gaze, old and patient and vast, watching Vassar
through a dying man's eyes with an attention that was not human and not
hostile and not anything she had a word for.

"Don't be afraid of it," Tolliver said. "It loves what it eats. That's
the terrible thing, Dr. Vassar. The terrible, beautiful thing. It loves
every life it has ever consumed. It keeps them out of love. And when it
comes for you -- if it comes for you -- it will love yours, too."

The machines sang their alarm. The numbers dropped. The breathing stopped.

Edgar Tolliver died at 6:47 AM on a Wednesday morning in Brockton,
Massachusetts, and something that had lived behind his eyes for
seventy-eight years gathered itself like a tide withdrawing from a shore
and reached out, through the dark, toward the nearest light.

                              * * *

Vassar stood beside the empty bed for a long time after the orderlies
had taken the body. She stood and she listened -- listened to the silence
of the room, to the ambient hum of the hospital, to the sound of her own
breathing and the beat of her own heart. She listened for something that
she could not name and could not describe and was not sure she wanted to
find.

She felt nothing unusual. No presence. No weight. No passenger.

But as she turned to leave, she paused. There was something at the edge
of her awareness -- not a memory, exactly, not a thought, but a quality
of light. A golden quality. The kind of light that falls on a river in
a city she has never visited, late in the day, when the sun is low and
the water turns the color of old coins and someone is standing on a
bridge, looking down, and the air smells of rosemary.

She stood very still.

The light faded. The hospital was the hospital. The fluorescent ceiling
buzzed its monotone. Somewhere down the hall, a patient's call button
chimed.

Vassar picked up her encephalograph case, walked out of the room, and
closed the door behind her. She did not look back. She walked to her
office, sat at her desk, and began writing her report. Her hands were
steady. Her prose was clinical. She documented everything Tolliver had
told her, everything the encephalograph had shown, everything she
theorized and everything she feared.

She did not document the golden light.

She was, after all, a rational woman.

But that night, in her apartment, in the dark, she dreamed of Florence.

                            [THE END]

                           SANCTUARY

                     by Father Thomas Aquino

================================================================================

                              I.

The first of the Verath arrived at the Church of Our Lady of the Stars
on an ordinary Thursday, during the seven o'clock Mass.

Father Mateo Ibarra was at the altar, consecrating the bread -- the
ancient words falling from his lips with the ease of thirty years'
practice, his hands performing the elevation with the steady grace that
the faithful expected and the faithless found theatrical -- when the
rear door of the church opened and something that was not human walked
in.

It was small. Perhaps a meter tall. Its body was a compact arrangement
of overlapping plates -- not metallic, not quite organic, but something
between the two, a living geometry of interlocking surfaces that shifted
and resettled as it moved, like the scales of a creature designed by a
mathematician. Its head, if the uppermost protrusion could be called a
head, bore two large, dark eyes that reflected the candlelight with a
warmth that was, Father Ibarra thought in the fraction of a second
before his training took over, unmistakably sentient. Unmistakably alive.
Unmistakably afraid.

The congregation -- twenty-three souls, this being the sparsely attended
Thursday evening Mass on Space Station Bethany, a mining platform in the
asteroid belt that the diocese had designated as a parish more out of
optimism than demographic necessity -- turned and stared.

The creature walked down the center aisle. Its gait was unsteady, and
Ibarra could see, as it passed beneath the nave lights, that several
of its overlapping plates were cracked and dark with a fluid that might
have been blood. It was wounded. It was struggling. And it was walking
directly toward the altar.

It stopped three meters from Ibarra, looked up at him with its dark,
reflective eyes, and spoke. The voice came from somewhere within the
arrangement of plates -- a vibration, a resonance, as though the
creature's entire body were a kind of instrument.

"Sanctuary," it said. The word was heavily accented but unmistakable. It
was a word that the creature had learned, specifically and deliberately,
in order to say it in this place.

Father Ibarra set down the chalice. He stepped around the altar. He
looked at the creature -- at its wounds, at its fear, at the desperate
intelligence in its dark eyes. And he said the only thing a priest could
say.

"You have it."

                              * * *

Her name, as best Ibarra could render it in human phonetics, was Selith.
She was Verath -- one of a species that humanity had encountered six
years earlier in the outer belt, a people who lived in the hollowed
interiors of asteroids and who had, by all accounts, been living there
for longer than human civilization had existed on Earth. The Verath were
quiet, reclusive, and technologically modest -- they had achieved
space travel of a sort, moving between asteroids in slow, elegant
vessels that ran on principles human engineers did not fully understand,
but they had never ventured beyond the belt and showed no interest in
doing so.

They had also, Ibarra learned from Selith in the hours after her
arrival, been marked for extermination.

"The Consortium," Selith said. She was sitting in the church's small
sacristy, wrapped in a blanket that Ibarra had taken from the parish
supply closet, drinking water from a cup that she held in appendages
that were not hands but served the same purpose. Her wounds had been
cleaned and bandaged by Dr. Anand Chakravarti, the station's physician,
who had arrived within minutes of a frantic call from one of the
parishioners and who had treated the Verath's injuries with the focused
calm of a man who had decided that xenobiology was a problem to solve
later and bleeding was a problem to solve now.

"The Mining Consortium," Selith continued. "They want our asteroids. The
Verath have lived in the deep belt for" -- she paused, searching for the
word -- "millennia. Our homes are carved into the rock. Our lives are
in the stone. The Consortium has surveyed our asteroids and found that
the ores are" -- another pause -- "valuable. Very valuable. More valuable
than our lives."

"And they're removing you."

"They are killing us." Selith's voice was flat with the effort of
controlling emotion in a foreign language. "Systematically. Asteroid by
asteroid. They come with their ships and their soldiers and they give us
a period of time to leave -- thirty days, sometimes less. Those who leave
are relocated to barren rocks with no resources, no infrastructure, no
means of survival. Those who stay are removed by force. The word they use
is 'cleared.' My asteroid -- my home for four hundred years -- was
'cleared' six weeks ago. There were eight hundred of us. Three hundred
survived the clearing. We scattered. I came here."

"Why here?" Ibarra asked. "Why a church?"

Selith looked at him with her dark, reflective eyes. "Because I have
studied your species, Father. I have read your histories and your laws
and your scriptures. And I found, in your traditions, a concept that
does not exist in any other system I have examined. A concept that says:
there are places where the hunted cannot be touched. Places where the
power of the state, the power of commerce, the power of violence itself
must stop at the door. You call this concept sanctuary."

"It is an ancient concept," Ibarra said carefully. "And it has not
always been honored."

"But it has been invoked. And the invocation has power. Not legal power,
perhaps. Moral power. The power to say: here, in this place, under the
eyes of whatever you believe is sacred, the killing stops."

Ibarra was quiet for a long time. He looked at the sacristy around him
-- the modest room where he stored his vestments and his communion wine
and the small, battered crucifix that he had carried from parish to
parish for thirty years. It was not a grand space. It was not a
cathedral. It was a converted storage closet on a mining station in the
asteroid belt, and its congregation numbered twenty-three on a good day.

But it was a church. And the creature sitting before him, wounded and
afraid, had walked down the aisle and asked for sanctuary. And Father
Mateo Ibarra, whatever his doubts about the practical wisdom of what he
was about to do, had no doubts whatsoever about the theological clarity
of his obligation.

"How many are you?" he asked.

"Here on the station, just me. But there are others -- refugees,
scattered through the belt. If they knew there was a place of
sanctuary, they would come."

"How many?"

"Perhaps fifty. Perhaps more."

Ibarra closed his eyes. Fifty Verath refugees in a church that seated
forty humans. On a station governed by the Colonial Authority, which was
funded in substantial part by the Mining Consortium. Under the
jurisdiction of a bishop on Earth who would learn of this situation
approximately eight minutes after the communication lag and would have
an opinion that Ibarra could predict with depressing accuracy.

He opened his eyes.

"Tell them to come," he said.

                              II.

They came in ones and twos, over the next ten days. Small, wounded
figures in the corridors of Bethany Station, their overlapping plates
dull with exhaustion and marked with the evidence of violence. They
came in the holds of sympathetic freighter captains, in the maintenance
crawlways between the station's outer hull and its inhabited sections,
in one case clinging to the exterior of a water tanker that docked at
the station's supply port. Each one walked into the Church of Our Lady
of the Stars, looked at Father Ibarra, and said the word.

Sanctuary.

By the end of the second week, there were forty-one Verath in the church.
They filled the nave, the sacristy, the small office where Ibarra wrote
his homilies, and the corridor leading to the parish storeroom. They
were quiet, courteous, and desperately grateful. They slept on the pews,
shared what food they had, and helped Dr. Chakravarti tend to the wounded
among them. Several of them attended Mass, sitting in the back rows with
expressions of attentive curiosity, understanding nothing of the ritual
but recognizing, with the instinct of a deeply spiritual people,
something sacred in the act of communal worship.

The station's human population reacted with a spectrum of responses that
ranged from compassion to fury. Several of Ibarra's parishioners
volunteered to bring food and supplies. Others withdrew from the parish
in protest. The station's administrator, a practical woman named
Director Haruki, called Ibarra to her office on the third day and told
him, in terms that were polite but unmistakable, that he was causing a
problem.

"Father Ibarra, I am sympathetic to the Verath's situation. But this
station operates under the authority of the Colonial Administration,
which has a formal agreement with the Mining Consortium regarding
resource extraction in the belt. The Consortium has filed a complaint.
They say you are harboring individuals who are in violation of
relocation orders."

"I am providing sanctuary to refugees who are fleeing violence."

"The Consortium characterizes it as lawful relocation."

"Director Haruki, the Verath word for what the Consortium is doing
translates, roughly, as 'the eating of homes.' I have seen their wounds.
I have heard their accounts. Whatever legal framework the Consortium
operates under, the reality on the ground -- on the rock -- is forced
removal, destruction of habitats, and killing. I cannot, in conscience,
hand these people over to that."

"You may not have a choice. The Consortium is requesting that I compel
the Verath to leave the station. If they refuse, the Consortium has
indicated that it will seek authorization to send a security team to
remove them."

"From a church."

"From a building on a station that is subject to Colonial Authority
jurisdiction, yes."

Ibarra looked at Haruki. She was not a cruel woman. He had known her for
three years, had shared meals with her, had listened to her talk about
her children on Earth with the wistful affection of a parent separated
by distance and obligation. She was doing her job. She was following
the chain of authority that governed her station and her career and her
pension. He did not blame her.

"Director," he said, "I understand your position. And I want to be
clear about mine. The right of sanctuary is older than the Colonial
Authority. It is older than the Mining Consortium. It is older than the
nation-states that fund them both. It dates to the earliest days of my
faith, when churches offered protection to the accused and the
persecuted and the condemned, not because the Church had the legal
authority to override secular power, but because it had the moral
authority to say: not here. Not in this place. Whatever happens outside
these walls, within them, the violence stops."

"Father, we're on a space station in the asteroid belt. This isn't
medieval Europe."

"No. But the principle is the same. And the need is the same. And my
obligation, as a priest, is the same."

Haruki sighed. It was the sigh of a woman who knew she was going to
lose this argument and was calculating the cost.

"I'll relay your position to the Colonial Authority," she said.
"They'll make the final decision."

"I know."

"And the Consortium will not be patient."

"I know that too."

                              * * *

The bishop's response arrived four days later, via encrypted
communication from the Diocese of the Solar Frontier, headquartered in
a comfortable office on the campus of Georgetown University,
approximately three hundred million kilometers from the Church of Our
Lady of the Stars.

The message read: "Father Ibarra, you are instructed to cooperate with
the lawful authorities of Bethany Station and the Colonial Administration
in the resolution of this situation. The right of sanctuary, while
historically significant, has no standing in current canon law and
cannot be invoked to obstruct the legitimate exercise of civil authority.
You are reminded that the Church's mission is the salvation of souls, not
the adjudication of political disputes. Comply with all lawful directives.
Acknowledge receipt."

Ibarra read the message twice. Then he sat at his desk, in the small
office that was now also a bedroom for three Verath refugees, and
composed his reply.

"Your Excellency, I acknowledge receipt of your instructions. I
respectfully inform you that I am unable to comply. The Verath refugees
in my care are fleeing the systematic destruction of their people. To
hand them over to the authority that is conducting that destruction would
be, in my judgment, a grave moral failure that I cannot reconcile with
my obligations as a priest, as a Christian, or as a human being. I invoke
the right of sanctuary not as a legal instrument but as a moral
imperative. I am prepared to accept the consequences of this decision,
including suspension, censure, or removal from my position. I commend
myself to your prayers, as I commend you to mine. In Christ, Father
Mateo Ibarra, S.J."

He sent the message and closed his terminal. Then he went into the nave,
where forty-one Verath refugees were sleeping on pews and blankets and
the worn carpet of a church that had never been designed to hold them,
and he knelt at the altar and prayed.

He did not pray for guidance. He had received all the guidance he needed,
not from the bishop, but from a small, wounded creature who had walked
down his aisle and asked for the oldest promise the Church had ever made.

He prayed for courage.

                              III.

The Consortium security team arrived on the twelfth day.

There were twenty of them, in the gray-and-white uniforms of the
Consolidated Mining Security Service, armed with crowd-control weapons
and wearing the blank, professionally neutral expressions of people who
were trained to follow orders and not to think too carefully about what
those orders meant. They assembled in the corridor outside the church
and waited.

Their commander was a woman named Captain Varga. She was compact,
efficient, and, Ibarra sensed from their brief initial exchange, deeply
uncomfortable with her assignment.

"Father Ibarra," Varga said, standing in the church doorway. She did not
enter. Ibarra noted this and found it significant. "I have authorization
from the Colonial Authority to enter this facility and remove the Verath
individuals who are present in violation of relocation orders. I am
requesting your cooperation."

"You have my respect, Captain. You do not have my cooperation."

"Father, I don't want this to be difficult."

"It is already difficult, Captain. The question is whether it will also
be violent."

Varga's jaw tightened. "I am authorized to use reasonable force."

"Against unarmed refugees in a church."

"Against individuals in violation of lawful orders, regardless of the
building they happen to occupy."

Ibarra stepped forward, into the doorway, so that he stood between
Varga and the interior of the church. Behind him, the Verath were
awake, alert, their dark eyes catching the light. Several of his human
parishioners had come as well -- Mrs. Okonkwo, who baked bread for the
parish; young Tomasz, who served as altar boy; Dr. Chakravarti, who
had brought his medical bag. They stood in the nave, among the Verath,
and their presence said what their words did not need to: we are here.

"Captain Varga," Ibarra said, and his voice was steady, and he was
surprised by how steady it was, because his hands were trembling and his
heart was beating so hard he could feel it in his teeth. "I am going to
tell you a story. It will take less than a minute."

Varga waited. Behind her, twenty armed security officers waited. In the
corridor, the station's security cameras recorded everything.

"In the year 600, in the kingdom of Kent, a man accused of murder fled
to the church of Canterbury and claimed the right of sanctuary. The
king's soldiers came to take him. The bishop -- a man named Augustine,
who would later be called a saint -- stood in the doorway and said: 'This
man is in God's house. If you wish to take him, you must come through
me.' The soldiers withdrew. Not because the bishop was armed. Not because
he had legal authority. But because they could not bring themselves to
commit violence in a place of worship, against a man of God, in view of
their neighbors and their own consciences."

He paused.

"I am not a saint, Captain. I am a tired priest on a mining station in
the asteroid belt, and my church is a converted storage closet with a
leaking roof. But the principle is the same. These people came to me and
asked for sanctuary. I gave it. If you wish to take them, you must come
through me."

The corridor was very quiet. Twenty security officers stood in their
gray-and-white uniforms and looked at the priest in the doorway, and
behind him the dark, frightened eyes of forty-one beings who had no
weapons and no power and no defense except the moral weight of a
promise made in a house of God.

Captain Varga looked at Ibarra. She looked past him, into the church,
at the Verath huddled on the pews, at Mrs. Okonkwo standing with her
arms crossed, at young Tomasz in his altar boy's cassock, at Dr.
Chakravarti with his medical bag. She looked at the cross above the
altar, which was plain and wooden and small and which, in this moment,
seemed to Ibarra to be the largest thing on the station.

"Father," she said, "I have my orders."

"And I have mine."

A long silence. The security cameras recorded it. The station's systems
hummed around them. Somewhere in the belt, mining ships tore open
asteroids that had been someone's home for four hundred years.

Captain Varga took one step forward. Then she stopped. She looked down at
her boots, at the threshold of the church door, at the line between the
corridor and the nave. It was a physical line -- a strip of metal where
the station's flooring met the church's carpet. It was also, in that
moment, something else entirely.

She did not cross it.

"I'm going to contact my superiors," she said. "I'm going to tell them
that the situation requires further review."

"Thank you, Captain."

"Don't thank me, Father. I haven't solved anything. I've delayed it."

"Sometimes," Ibarra said, "a delay is all that mercy requires."

Varga turned and walked back to her security team. She spoke to them in
a low voice. They holstered their weapons. They withdrew down the
corridor, their boots echoing on the metal floor, the sound fading until
the church was quiet again.

Ibarra stood in the doorway for a long time after they left. Then he
turned back to his church -- to the Verath, to his parishioners, to
the small and sacred space that he had drawn a line around with nothing
but words and faith and the stubborn conviction that some things were
more important than orders.

Selith approached him. Her dark eyes held something that Ibarra
recognized, because he had seen it in the eyes of every person who had
ever knelt at his altar and received the bread and the wine and the
promise that came with them.

"They will come back," she said.

"Yes."

"And you will stand in the doorway again."

"Yes."

"How long?"

Father Mateo Ibarra looked at the cross above his altar, and at the
forty-one souls in his care, and at the darkness of the belt beyond
the station's hull, where the stars burned with a cold, indifferent
light that warmed nothing and forgave nothing and illuminated everything.

"As long as it takes," he said.

He closed the door. Not to lock it -- churches, he believed, should
never be locked. But to make a point. The door was there, and it was
closed, and on this side of it, the violence stopped. On this side, in
this small room, in this converted storage closet on a mining station in
the asteroid belt, the ancient promise held.

Sanctuary.

He turned back to his congregation -- human and Verath together, crowded
and frightened and safe, for now, in the shelter of a faith that was
older than space travel and more stubborn than commerce and strong enough,
perhaps, to hold.

He began the evening prayer.

                            [THE END]

                        ABOUT THE AUTHORS

================================================================================

HAROLD "HAL" MERCER is a civil engineer and writer whose stories have
appeared in Astounding Science Fiction, Galaxy, and these pages since
issue #7. A veteran of the Army Corps of Engineers and a designer of
highway interchanges for the Ohio Department of Transportation, he brings
to his fiction an unshakeable conviction that the universe is a
construction problem and that construction problems can be solved. "The
Dyson Initiative" is his most ambitious work to date, and its two-part
structure reflects, he says, "the fact that big projects don't stop when
you want them to." He lives in Akron, Ohio, with his wife Dorothy and
four sons, two of whom are already showing alarming aptitude with a
slide rule.

THEODORE CAIN is the most prolific contributor to these pages, having
published at least one story in nearly every issue since our founding. A
former autoworker, infantryman, and technical writer, he approaches
science fiction with the versatility of a man who has done many jobs and
found something worth writing about in all of them. "The Emissary" marks
his first foray into comedy, and we suspect it will not be his last. He
reports that the story was inspired by watching his wife rearrange the
living room furniture for the third time in a month and thinking, "There
must be a message in this." He lives in Dearborn, Michigan, and writes
every evening from eight to midnight.

DR. VINCENT KOSLOV is a physicist at Caltech whose fiction has appeared
in these pages since issue #3. His stories are distinguished by their
rigorous scientific accuracy and their conviction that the most important
questions in physics are also the most important questions in human
ethics. "The Prometheus Verdict" was inspired, he says, by "a conversation
with a colleague about what would happen if we actually succeeded in
doing what we've been trying to do." He notes that the physics in the
story is "slightly ahead of current theory but not, I fear, as far ahead
as we would like." He lives in Pasadena, California, with his wife Elena
and three children.

REX DRUMMOND continues to produce stories of interplanetary adventure
from his home in San Diego, where he lives with his second wife, his
dogs, and a collection of maps that now covers an entire wall of his
den. "The Foxhound of Mars" represents what we believe is a new
direction for this most reliable of action writers: a story in which the
hero's most important decision is not to fight. When asked about this
departure, Drummond reportedly said, "Even a cowboy hangs up his guns
eventually. The trick is knowing when." He adds that he has never
actually been to Mars but considers it "just West Texas with worse air."

LUCIAN MARSH writes from an undisclosed location on the New England
coast, where he lives alone with his books, his manuscripts, and what
he describes as "the productive companionship of the fog." His stories
have appeared in these pages since issue #11, and each one has pushed
the boundaries of what science fiction can do with atmosphere, dread,
and the uncertain territory between the mind and the unknown. "The
Memory Palace" is, by his own account, "the most personal story I have
written," though he declines to explain what he means by this. We have
learned not to press the point.

FATHER THOMAS AQUINO, S.J., is an ordained Jesuit priest and professor
of philosophy at Loyola University in Baltimore. His stories, which have
appeared in these pages since issue #17, explore the intersection of
faith and science with an intellectual honesty and a moral seriousness
that has earned him devoted readers across the theological spectrum.
"Sanctuary" was written, he says, "because the question it asks is the
oldest question the Church has ever faced: what do we owe to the stranger
at our door?" He adds that the answer, for a Christian, "should never be
in doubt, even when the stranger is not human." He lives in the Jesuit
residence at Loyola and writes slowly, carefully, and with the patience
of a man who has spent a lifetime listening for answers.

Dear Mr. Sterling,

I have just finished Issue #24, and I must write to say that "The Magi
Protocol" by Dr. Koslov is one of the finest Christmas stories I have
ever read, in or out of the science fiction genre. The image of three
scientists from rival nations standing together before a newborn star,
their political differences made irrelevant by the sheer wonder of what
they are witnessing, stayed with me for days. Koslov has a gift for
finding the sacred in the scientific, and this story is perhaps his most
luminous example.

                              -- Dr. Margaret Lennard
                                 Department of Astronomy
                                 Mount Wilson Observatory, California

Dear Sterling,

Pemberton's "Midwinter Night's Dream" in the December issue made me
laugh so hard I spilled coffee on my cat, who has not forgiven me. The
idea of an AI Santa delivering what people actually want instead of what
they need is the most incisive piece of social commentary I've read this
year, and the image of the senator clutching a teddy bear while the
five-year-old reads classified documents may be the single funniest
scene in the history of your magazine. More Pemberton, please. My cat
concurs.

                              -- Howard Rennick
                                 Evanston, Illinois

Dear Editor,

I write to express my concern about "Silent Night, Frozen World" by
Agnes Whitfield. While the story is beautifully written, its depiction
of a post-nuclear winter seems calculated to promote defeatism at a
time when our nation must maintain the resolve to defend itself against
Communist aggression. Science fiction has a responsibility to inspire
confidence in the future, not to paint visions of apocalyptic despair.
I trust that future issues will maintain a more constructive tone.

                              -- Col. (Ret.) James P. Braddock
                                 Arlington, Virginia

Colonel Braddock: We appreciate your concern and respectfully disagree.
It is precisely because we wish to avoid the future that Mrs. Whitfield
depicts that we believe it is important to imagine it clearly. A
magazine that shows only comfortable futures is not inspiring confidence;
it is encouraging complacency. As for the suggestion that science fiction
has a "responsibility" to any particular political position, we refer the
Colonel to the entire history of the genre and invite him to search it
for a single great story that was written to make its readers feel safe.
                                                               -- M.S.

Dear Mr. Sterling,

I am fourteen years old and I have read every issue of Tales from the
Future and Beyond since the first one. I want to be a writer when I grow
up, and I want to write for your magazine. What advice would you give a
young person who wants to write science fiction?

                              -- Patricia Alvarez
                                 Tucson, Arizona

Miss Alvarez: Read everything. Write every day. Study the sciences,
because the universe is the raw material of our genre, and you cannot
work in material you do not understand. Be bold in your imagination and
humble in your craft. And send us a story when you are ready. Our door
is open, and we are always looking for new voices. We will be here.
                                                               -- M.S.

                    THE BOOKSHELF OF TOMORROW
                         Book Reviews

================================================================================

MISSION OF GRAVITY by Hal Clement
(Doubleday, 1954, $2.75)

Reviewed by Maxwell Sterling

Mr. Clement has written a novel that is, in the strictest sense of the
word, wonderful -- it fills the reader with wonder. The setting is
Mesklin, a massive, rapidly spinning planet where the surface gravity
varies from three times Earth-normal at the equator to nearly seven
hundred times at the poles. Into this extraordinary environment, Clement
drops a story of exploration and cooperation between humans and the
native Mesklinites -- centipede-like beings who have adapted to crushing
gravity with a culture as rich and practical as their environment demands.
The science is meticulous, the world-building is superb, and the
Mesklinites are among the most convincing alien characters in the genre.
If the human characters are somewhat less vivid than their alien
counterparts, this is a minor flaw in a major achievement. This is hard
science fiction at its finest: a story that could not exist without
science and could not succeed without fiction. Highest recommendation.

I AM LEGEND by Richard Matheson
(Gold Medal Books, 1954, $0.25)

Reviewed by Maxwell Sterling

Mr. Matheson's slim, devastating novel takes the vampire legend and
subjects it to the rigors of science fiction, and the result is a work of
sustained horror that is also, remarkably, a meditation on loneliness,
perseverance, and the meaning of monstrosity. Robert Neville may be the
last living man on Earth, surrounded by a world of vampires, and his
daily routine of fortification, research, and survival is rendered with
a grim, meticulous realism that makes the fantastic premise feel
disturbingly plausible. Matheson is a master of the ordinary detail --
the routines, the tools, the small domestic acts that anchor his hero to
sanity -- and he uses this mastery to make us feel Neville's isolation in
our bones. The ending is a masterpiece of reversal, and the title takes
on a meaning that the reader does not expect and will not forget. A
landmark. Essential reading.

A MIRROR FOR OBSERVERS by Edgar Pangborn
(Doubleday, 1954, $2.75)

Reviewed by Maxwell Sterling

Mr. Pangborn's Hugo-winning novel is a quiet, deeply humane work about
alien observers who have lived among humans for millennia, watching our
species with a mixture of hope and despair. When two of these observers
take opposing positions on the fate of a gifted human child, the novel
becomes a philosophical contest between faith in human potential and
despair at human cruelty. Pangborn's prose is graceful and precise, his
characterization is subtle, and his vision of the alien-among-us is free
of the paranoia that usually attaches to the concept. This is not a
thriller. It is a novel of ideas, and the central idea -- that beings of
vast intelligence might choose to live among us not to exploit or control
us but simply because they find us interesting -- is both flattering and
disquieting. A thoughtful, rewarding book for patient readers.
