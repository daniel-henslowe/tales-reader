TRANSMISSIONS FROM THE EDITOR'S DESK
by Maxwell Sterling

FORTY FUTURES

Dear Reader,

You hold in your hands the fortieth issue of Tales from the Future and Beyond, and I confess to a certain vertigo. Forty issues. Forty months of stories, letters, arguments, corrections, and the particular blend of exhilaration and anxiety that accompanies the act of placing a new magazine on the newsstands and wondering whether anyone will buy it. They did, as it turned out. You did. And here we are.

I did not expect to reach this number. When we launched in January of 1953, I privately gave us eighteen months before Meridian Publishing pulled the plug. The field was crowded, the competition formidable, and I was acutely aware that launching a new science fiction magazine in a market that already boasted Astounding, Galaxy, and F&SF was an act of either supreme confidence or staggering folly. I suspected the latter but hoped for the former.

What I did not anticipate was you — the readers whose letters have filled our mailbag, whose corrections have kept us honest, whose enthusiasm has sustained us through lean months and fat. When our distribution was cut in the spring of 1954 and it looked like issue #17 might be our last, it was the readers who wrote to their newsstands, who mailed in subscription cards, who refused to let this magazine die. You saved us. I do not forget it.

Nor did I anticipate the writers. When I assembled the roster for that first issue — Koslov, Drummond, Wainwright, Cain, Thornton, Pemberton — I knew I had good writers. I did not know I had great ones. Forty issues later, many of those same names appear in this anniversary issue, and their work has only deepened, sharpened, and grown more daring with time. They are joined by newer voices — Stanton, Blackwood, Finley — who have enriched our pages beyond measure.

For this fortieth issue, I asked each of our contributors to give me their best. I believe they have. Dr. Koslov delivers a mind-bending journey through parallel realities that is among his finest work. Colonel Stanton offers a war story that is, at its heart, a story about peace. Theodore Cain gives us an alien auction that crackles with tension and dark humor. Douglas Pemberton turns democracy into entomology and finds the result hilarious. Arthur Wainwright poses a question about identity and skill that will haunt your quiet hours. Harrison Blackwood returns with what I can only describe as the most frightening story we have ever published. And Bob Finley sends us to Mars for a rodeo that will leave you grinning from ear to ear.

Seven stories. Seven visions of tomorrow. Forty issues of wonders.

I sometimes try to imagine what the next forty will bring. Then I remember that imagining is what our writers do best, and I leave it to them.

Thank you for forty months. Here's to forty more.

Yours among the stars,

Maxwell Sterling
Editor, Tales from the Future and Beyond
New York, February 1956

                        INFINITE STAIRCASE

                        by Dr. Vincent Koslov

================================================================================

                               I.

The staircase was discovered on a Tuesday, at 0347 station time, by a maintenance technician named Yuri Volkov who was tracing a coolant leak in the lower service tunnels of Orbital Station Kairos.

Volkov had been employed aboard Kairos for eleven months. He was a competent man — methodical, unhurried, possessed of the particular patience required to work in the cramped, poorly lit spaces between the station's inner and outer hulls, where the plumbing and cabling ran like the circulatory system of some vast mechanical organism. He carried his tools in a canvas roll, worked by the light of a headlamp, and kept a logbook in which he recorded the time, location, and nature of every repair in a small, precise hand.

At 0342, following the coolant trail through Section C-7 of the maintenance sublevel, he turned a corner and found the stairs.

They descended from the corridor floor at a forty-three-degree angle — a standard metal staircase, industrial gray, with perforated treads and a pipe railing bolted to the left wall. The construction was identical to the service stairs found throughout the station. There was nothing unusual about the stairs themselves.

What was unusual was that they should not have been there.

Volkov consulted his station schematic — a laminated card he kept folded in his breast pocket. Section C-7 was the lowest inhabited level of the station. Below it was the outer hull: three centimeters of titanium-aluminum alloy, then nothing but vacuum and the slow wheel of stars. There was no room for a staircase. There was no structure for it to lead to.

He stood at the top of the stairs and looked down. The headlamp beam reached perhaps eight meters before the staircase curved gently to the right and disappeared from view. The air rising from below was cool and smelled faintly of ozone and something else — something mineral, like the scent of wet stone in a cave.

Volkov was not an imaginative man. He did not speculate about the meaning of the staircase. He noted its existence in his logbook — 0347, Section C-7, anomalous structural feature, staircase descending below hull line, requires investigation — and returned to the maintenance office to file a report.

The report reached Dr. Anya Petrov at 0615.

                               II.

Petrov was the station's senior physicist, a position she held with the quiet authority of a woman who had spent twenty years studying the behavior of matter under conditions that the Earth-bound found difficult to conceive. She was forty-six, tall and angular, with close-cropped gray hair and hands that were always slightly cold — a consequence, she sometimes said, of spending too long near vacuum.

She read Volkov's report over her morning coffee in the officers' mess. The mess was nearly empty at that hour — only Petrov and the night-shift communications officer, a young woman named Lindgren who was eating toast and reading a novel. Through the panoramic viewport, Earth turned slowly below them, blue and white and impossibly vivid against the black.

Petrov read the report twice. She set it down, finished her coffee, and went to Section C-7.

The staircase was exactly as Volkov had described it. Petrov stood at its head and performed a series of observations with the methodical care that characterized everything she did. She measured the angle of descent: forty-three degrees, consistent with standard station construction. She tested the railing: solid, cold to the touch, firmly bolted. She dropped a small metal washer down the stairwell and listened. It rang against the treads — once, twice, three times — each ring fainter than the last, the sound diminishing not as though the washer were rolling to a stop but as though it were traveling a very great distance.

She checked her schematic. The outer hull was 1.6 meters below the floor of Section C-7. The staircase had already descended well past that point. She could see that simply by looking — the stairs continued downward for at least ten meters before they curved out of sight, and that was ten meters into space that was, according to every blueprint and every physical measurement, occupied by the vacuum of orbital space.

She placed her hand flat on the staircase wall. The metal was cool and faintly vibrated, as though a large machine were running somewhere far below. She felt the wall with her fingertips, pressing gently. It was solid. Real. Not a projection, not a hallucination, not a clever piece of stage machinery.

Petrov returned to her office and made three calls. The first was to Captain Henrik Nystrom, the station commander. The second was to Dr. Samuel Ogawa, the station's structural engineer. The third was to Dr. Lise Brandt, the station's quantum physicist.

The meeting convened at 0800 in the small conference room adjacent to the command center. Present were Petrov, Nystrom, Ogawa, Brandt, and Volkov himself, who sat at the end of the table with the uncomfortable expression of a man who had filed a routine maintenance report and was now attending a meeting with the station's senior staff.

Petrov described the staircase. She presented Volkov's report, her own observations, and the results of a preliminary structural analysis she had conducted en route to the meeting: the staircase penetrated the station's outer hull without breaching it. There was no hole. No seal had been broken. The staircase simply passed through the hull the way a needle passes through fabric — except that the fabric was three centimeters of aerospace-grade alloy and it showed no sign of penetration whatsoever.

"That is not possible," Ogawa said.

"No," Petrov agreed. "It is not."

"A sensor artifact," Nystrom suggested. He was a practical man, a career officer with twenty years in the orbital service, who dealt with anomalies by categorizing them as malfunctions until proven otherwise.

"I stood on the stairs," Petrov said. "They supported my weight. The metal is cold. You can hear the acoustics change as the stairwell descends. It is not a sensor artifact. It is a physical structure."

"Passing through the hull."

"Passing through the hull without disturbing it."

There was a silence. Through the conference room's small viewport, the Earth continued its patient rotation.

Brandt spoke. She was the youngest person at the table — thirty-two, with a theoretician's distant gaze and a habit of constructing sentences as carefully as she constructed equations. "Has anyone descended the stairs?"

"No," Petrov said.

"Then we should."

                               III.

The expedition was organized with the deliberate, methodical thoroughness that Petrov brought to all her work. She selected a team of four: herself, Brandt, Volkov (who knew the maintenance tunnels better than anyone aboard), and Lieutenant Commander James Chen, the station's security officer, whose presence Nystrom insisted upon and whose expression suggested he would have preferred almost any other assignment.

They assembled at the head of the staircase at 1400, equipped with headlamps, portable instruments, communications gear, and forty-eight hours of provisions — this last a precaution that Petrov considered prudent and Chen considered ominous.

"How far down are we going?" Chen asked. He was a compact man, physically precise, who wore his sidearm with the ease of long habit.

"As far as the stairs go," Petrov said.

"And if they don't stop?"

"Then we will establish a reasonable limit and return. Four hours of descent, unless we encounter something that warrants further exploration. Agreed?"

There were no objections. Petrov switched on her headlamp and stepped onto the first tread.

The descent was, for the first several minutes, unremarkable. The staircase descended at its constant forty-three degrees, the treads ringing softly under their boots. The walls were standard station construction — gray metal panels, conduit runs, the occasional junction box. The air was cool and dry. Their headlamp beams cut sharp lines through the darkness ahead.

After approximately fifty meters of descent — Petrov counted treads; each tread represented a vertical drop of eighteen centimeters — Chen stopped.

"We're below the hull," he said.

Petrov checked her instruments. The altimeter confirmed it: they were now 8.2 meters below the station's outer hull. According to every physical law she knew, they should be in vacuum, their bodies explosive decompressed, their blood boiling in the zero-pressure environment of low Earth orbit.

Instead, they were standing on a metal staircase, breathing normally, listening to the faint hum of what sounded like ventilation equipment somewhere below.

"Keep going," Petrov said.

They descended another hundred meters. The staircase continued unchanged — the same angle, the same construction, the same cool, ozone-scented air. Petrov noted that the walls had begun to change: the junction boxes bore different serial numbers, and the conduit runs followed slightly different routing patterns. Small differences, but real.

At the two-hundred-meter mark, the staircase ended at a door.

It was a standard station pressure door — heavy, wheel-locked, painted with the yellow-and-black chevrons that indicated a section boundary. Stenciled across it in block letters was a designation: SECTION A-1 / KAIROS STATION.

Volkov frowned. "Section A-1 is the command level. At the top of the station. Not the bottom."

Petrov examined the door. The paint was slightly different — a shade lighter than the standard Kairos gray. The wheel lock was the same design but showed different wear patterns. She placed her hand on the door and felt, through the metal, the faint vibration of a functioning station: air handlers, generators, the subliminal pulse of a living habitat.

She turned the wheel. The lock released with a heavy, mechanical clunk. She pulled the door open.

On the other side was Orbital Station Kairos.

                               IV.

Not their Kairos. A Kairos.

The corridor beyond the door was identical in layout to Section A-1 of their own station — the same dimensions, the same construction, the same fluorescent lighting strips in the ceiling. But the details were wrong. The paint was the slightly lighter gray that Petrov had noticed on the door. The safety signage was in English rather than the English-and-Russian bilingual format used aboard their station. The emergency lighting panels along the baseboards were blue instead of red.

And the corridor was not empty.

A woman was walking toward them — middle-aged, wearing a duty uniform identical in cut to Kairos standard issue but in a shade of navy rather than the standard charcoal. She saw them and stopped. Her expression moved from surprise to confusion to something that was not quite alarm but was adjacent to it.

"Who are you?" she said. "How did you get to this level?"

Petrov stepped through the doorway. "My name is Dr. Anya Petrov. I am the senior physicist aboard Orbital Station Kairos."

The woman stared at her. "I am Dr. Rachel Kwan. I am the senior physicist aboard Orbital Station Kairos." She paused. "There is only one senior physicist."

"Apparently," Petrov said, "there is not."

The explanation took two hours, several pots of coffee, and the involvement of this station's commanding officer — a Captain Johanssen, who bore no resemblance to Captain Nystrom but who occupied the same office and sat behind the same desk. The staircase, it emerged, existed in this version of Kairos as well. It had been discovered three days prior by one of their own maintenance crew, descending from their lowest service level. They had sent a team down. The team had found another station below theirs — a third Kairos, with a third crew, a third set of slightly different details.

"They've been down further," Kwan said. She was a careful woman, precise in her language, and she presented the information with the controlled urgency of a scientist who has encountered something outside all known parameters. "Three levels below us. Each one a complete, functioning station. Each one slightly different."

"How different?" Brandt asked.

Kwan consulted her notes. "The first sub-level — our sub-level, the one you reached — has the same crew complement and mission profile as our station, but minor variations in equipment specifications and administrative language. The second sub-level has a different crew complement: thirty-eight instead of forty-two. Their station commander is a woman. Their quantum physicist is a man named Nakamura rather than my colleague Dr. Andersen."

"And the third?"

"The third is more divergent. Significantly different hull configuration. Different national sponsorship — they fly a flag we don't recognize. Different primary language — Portuguese, we think, though we haven't had extensive contact. They were quite alarmed by our arrival."

Petrov absorbed this. She was aware of the theoretical literature — Everett's many-worlds interpretation, the branching universes of quantum mechanics, the idea that every quantum event split reality into parallel versions of itself. She had read the papers. She had considered the mathematics. She had not expected to walk down a staircase and find the theory waiting for her at the bottom.

"The staircase connects them," she said. "The stations. Each flight of stairs leads to a different version of Kairos."

"That is our working hypothesis, yes."

"And the staircase continues downward. Past the third sub-level."

"We don't know how far. Our team descended to the third level and stopped. The stairs continued beyond the third level's lowest point. We can see them going further down."

Petrov looked at Brandt. Brandt was standing very still, her face pale, her eyes bright with the particular intensity that meant she was thinking at a rate that her expression could not keep pace with.

"It's a building," Brandt said softly. "The station is a building. And the parallel realities are its floors."

                               V.

They stayed in the second Kairos for six hours — long enough to conduct a preliminary survey, exchange technical specifications, and establish a basic communication protocol between the two stations. Captain Johanssen was cooperative but cautious, and Petrov noted that he kept two security officers within arm's reach at all times.

Before they departed, Petrov asked to see the staircase that led further down.

It was in the same location as the staircase in their own station — Section C-7, the maintenance sublevel, descending through the floor at the same forty-three-degree angle. The construction was identical. The air rising from below carried the same ozone-and-stone scent.

"We've mapped three levels below ours," Kwan said, standing beside Petrov at the head of the stairs. "Each one different. The differences accumulate. By the third level, the station is barely recognizable as Kairos."

"What is on the fourth level?"

"We haven't looked."

"May I?"

Kwan hesitated. Then she nodded. "Be careful."

Petrov descended alone. Chen objected; she overruled him. The staircase was narrow, and she moved through it with the focused attention of a woman who was aware that each step took her further from anything she understood.

The fourth level was different.

The door at the bottom of the stairs opened onto a station that was Kairos in name only. The layout was similar — the bones of the structure were recognizable — but the details had shifted dramatically. The lighting was amber rather than white. The signage was in a script she did not recognize — angular, geometric, possibly an artificial language. The air was warmer and carried a faint, sweet scent, like heated resin.

The corridor was empty. Petrov walked twenty meters in and found a viewport.

Through it, she saw Earth. But not the Earth she knew. The continents were the same, but the lights were different — fewer of them in the familiar places, more of them in places where, in her reality, there were none. A bright lattice of illumination covered central Africa. The Mediterranean was dark.

She stood at the viewport for a long time, looking down at a world that was hers and was not, turning slowly in the dark.

                               VI.

Over the next seventy-two hours, the expedition expanded. Petrov established a base camp in the second Kairos, coordinated with Kwan's team, and organized a systematic descent through the levels below. Each level required the same journey: down the staircase in Section C-7, through the pressure door at the bottom, into another version of the station.

They catalogued the differences with scientific rigor. Petrov assigned a numeric index to each level — Level 0 being their own station, Level -1 being the first sub-level where Kwan's crew lived, and so on downward.

Level -2 had thirty-eight crew members and a different national sponsorship.

Level -3 was Portuguese-speaking, with a significantly different hull configuration. Their Earth, visible through their viewports, had polar ice caps that were noticeably larger than those on Petrov's Earth.

Level -4, the one Petrov had visited alone, appeared to be sponsored by a political entity that did not exist in any reality they had yet encountered. Its crew spoke a language that incorporated elements of Arabic and Mandarin. They were wary but not hostile.

Level -5 was empty. The station was intact, the systems running, but there was no crew. The personal quarters still contained clothing and personal effects. The mess hall had plates on the tables. Whatever had happened to the crew had happened quickly and recently.

Level -6 was damaged. Sections of the hull had been breached and sealed with emergency patches. The atmosphere was thin and cold. There were no bodies. There were no crew. But there were marks on the walls — scratches, gouges, patterns that might have been writing or might have been something else entirely.

"We should stop," Chen said, after they returned from Level -6. They were standing in the stairwell between Levels -5 and -6, the four of them plus two additional personnel from Kwan's crew who had joined the expedition. Chen's face was drawn. "Whatever happened to those stations — the empty one, the damaged one — we don't know that it won't happen to us if we go further."

"The stairs continue," Petrov said.

"I can see that the stairs continue. That doesn't mean we should follow them."

Brandt was sitting on the stairs, her datapad balanced on her knees, working through calculations. She had been quiet for the last several levels — not withdrawn, but concentrated, processing. Now she looked up.

"The divergence is increasing," she said. "Each level is more different from our baseline than the one above it. The rate of divergence appears to follow a logarithmic curve. If the pattern holds, the levels below us will diverge so far from our reality that the station itself may become unrecognizable."

"Then we'll turn back when it does," Petrov said.

"Anya." Chen almost never used her first name. "The empty station. The damaged station. Those aren't minor divergences. Something went wrong on those levels. Very wrong."

"I am aware of that."

"So why are we still going down?"

Petrov was quiet for a moment. The stairwell hummed around them — that deep, subliminal vibration that they had felt since the first descent, the sound of something vast and mechanical operating at a frequency below conscious hearing.

"Because the staircase was placed here," she said. "Or it grew here, or it emerged here — I do not know the mechanism. But it is not an accident. It is a structure. Someone or something built a structure that connects parallel versions of this station, and it extends in both directions — up and down — further than we have explored. The question of why it exists is the most important question I have encountered in twenty years of physics. I intend to find the answer."

Chen said nothing. After a moment, he checked his sidearm and started down the stairs.

                               VII.

They descended past Level -6, past -7 (a station where the crew was present but refused to open their doors or respond to communication), past -8 (a station that was recognizably Kairos in structure but appeared to be made of a dark, glassy material rather than metal, and whose atmosphere tasted of copper).

At Level -9, the staircase changed.

The change was subtle at first. The treads, which had been standard perforated metal throughout, became solid. The walls, which had been paneled, became seamless. The color shifted from gray to a deep, matte black. The railing disappeared. The angle of descent steepened slightly — from forty-three degrees to what Petrov estimated at forty-seven.

At Level -10, the staircase was no longer a staircase.

It was still a descending passage, still navigable on foot, but the treads had become shallow ledges carved into what appeared to be stone — dark, fine-grained, with a texture like basalt. The walls were stone as well, smooth and cold, curving inward to form a passage that was more tunnel than stairwell. The air was different: colder, drier, with a faint metallic tang that Petrov associated with deep underground spaces on Earth.

The door at the bottom of Level -10 was not a door. It was an opening — a circular aperture in the stone, approximately two meters in diameter, through which light pulsed in a slow, regular rhythm. The light was blue-white and sourceless, filling the space beyond the opening with an even, shadowless illumination.

Petrov stepped through.

The space on the other side was large — larger than any single compartment in any version of the station they had visited. It was roughly hemispherical, perhaps thirty meters in diameter, with walls of the same dark stone. The floor was flat and smooth.

In the center of the floor was a model.

It was small — perhaps a meter across — and it floated at waist height, unsupported, rotating slowly. It was the station. Kairos. But not one Kairos — all of them. Petrov could see the individual levels stacked like floors of a building, each one slightly different, connected by the thread of the staircase running through their centers. The model was precise and detailed enough that she could make out individual sections, individual compartments. She could see the differences between levels: the paint variations, the hull configurations, the different arrangements of solar panels and communication arrays.

The model showed at least fifty levels. Possibly more — the resolution diminished at the extremes, the levels blurring together in a way that suggested the structure extended further than the model could represent.

Brandt made a sound — a small, involuntary exhalation. She was looking at the model with an expression that Petrov had seen on the faces of physicists only twice before: the expression of a human being confronting something that was not merely new but was a new category of thing.

"It's all one structure," Brandt whispered. "The parallel realities — they're not separate. They're rooms. Rooms in the same building. And this" — she gestured at the staircase behind them, at the stone passage, at the chamber they stood in — "this is the foundation."

                               VIII.

They spent four hours in the chamber, documenting the model, taking readings, running every instrument they had brought. The results were, in Petrov's careful assessment, "consistent with no known physics."

The model appeared to be a real-time representation of the structure. When Petrov moved a small object on one of the upper levels — she asked Volkov, via radio, to move a tool cabinet in their station's Section C-7 — the corresponding element in the model shifted position. The model was not a recording. It was a live diagram of a building that extended across parallel realities.

Brandt's calculations, refined over those four hours, suggested that the structure had no ground floor. The levels extended upward as well as downward from any given reference point. There was no top and no bottom. Only the staircase, connecting everything, ascending and descending without end.

"An infinite building," Brandt said. She was sitting on the stone floor, her back against the wall, her datapad abandoned beside her. Her voice was flat with exhaustion and awe. "Each floor is a reality. Each reality is a floor. And the staircase is the axis."

"Built by whom?" Chen asked.

Brandt shook her head. "I don't know. But the chamber suggests intentionality. Someone wanted it to be observed. Someone built a room where the structure could be seen."

Petrov stood before the model, studying it. The levels nearest her own — Levels -1 through -5 — were clearly defined, their details sharp. As the index increased in either direction, the levels became more diffuse, their structures more alien, until at the extremes they were barely recognizable as stations at all. Some appeared to be biological rather than mechanical. Some appeared to be mathematical abstractions rather than physical objects. Some appeared to be empty space, or light, or patterns that the human visual system could not resolve into meaningful shapes.

"The divergence," she said. "It is not just increasing. It is transforming. The further you travel from any given level, the more the structure changes. The station becomes something else. Something we have no framework to understand."

"Which means," Chen said, "that somewhere down there — or up there — the stairs lead to places that aren't stations at all."

"Yes."

"Places that aren't anything we can survive in."

"Perhaps."

Chen stood up. He looked at the model, at the stone walls, at the circular aperture through which they had entered. Then he looked at Petrov.

"We go back," he said. "We report what we've found. We let people smarter than us figure out what it means."

Petrov said nothing for a long moment. She was looking at the model — at the levels beyond the station-shaped levels, the ones that resolved into light and abstraction and strangeness. The staircase ran through them all. A thread connecting everything that could exist.

"Yes," she said, finally. "We go back."

                               IX.

The ascent took seven hours. They climbed in silence, each level ticking past like a floor counter in an elevator — the stone giving way to the glassy black material, then to metal, then to the familiar gray of the stations they knew. They passed through Level -6 (damaged, empty), Level -5 (abandoned), Level -4 (the amber-lit station with the unrecognizable script), Level -3 (Portuguese-speaking, cold), Level -2 (thirty-eight crew members), and Level -1 (Kwan's station, where they stopped long enough to share their findings and drink a cup of very welcome coffee).

Then up the final staircase, through the door, into Section C-7 of their own Orbital Station Kairos.

Volkov logged their return in his maintenance logbook: 0215, expedition returned from anomalous structural feature, all personnel accounted for.

Petrov filed her report with Captain Nystrom at 0300. It ran to forty-seven pages. She wrote it in the precise, unadorned language of a scientific paper, presenting observations, measurements, and preliminary hypotheses without speculation or embellishment. The final paragraph read:

     The structure discovered in Section C-7 appears to be a
     connection between parallel instances of Orbital Station Kairos,
     extending in both directions along an axis that has no
     determined endpoint. A chamber at approximately Level -10
     contains a representation of the full structure, which includes
     a minimum of fifty parallel instances and possibly many more.
     The mechanism by which the structure exists, the identity of its
     builders (if any), and the purpose of the connection remain
     unknown. Further investigation is recommended. Further
     investigation is also, in my professional judgment, inevitable.
     The staircase is here. We cannot unlearn its existence. The
     only remaining question is how far we are willing to descend.

She filed the report, returned to her quarters, and stood for a long time at her viewport, looking down at Earth. It was night over the Pacific. The planet was dark except for the thin bright arc of the terminator and, here and there, the scattered lights of ships and islands.

She thought about the other Earths she had seen — the one with the bright lattice over Africa, the one with the enlarged ice caps, the ones she had not seen, the ones that existed on levels she had not reached. She thought about the staircase, descending through all of them, an impossible structure connecting versions of reality like floors in a building that had no roof and no foundation.

She thought about Brandt's word: axis.

An axis implied a structure built around it. A central support from which the rest of the building derived its form. The staircase was not merely a connection between realities. It was the thing around which the realities were organized.

Petrov pressed her hand against the cold glass of the viewport. Below her, the Earth turned. Above her — and below her, and beside her, and in directions that had no names — other Earths turned as well, each one a room in a building that no one had designed, or that someone had designed so long ago that the act of design had become indistinguishable from the structure of reality itself.

She did not sleep that night. She sat at her desk and began writing the equations.

                               * * *

In the morning, she went back to Section C-7. The staircase was still there. She had not expected it to be gone, exactly, but she had considered the possibility — the way one considers the possibility that a dream might not survive the transition to waking.

She stood at the top of the stairs and looked down. The headlamp beam reached into the darkness, illuminating the treads, the railing, the corridor walls descending into the impossible.

She had reported her findings. She had written her equations. She had done everything that duty and methodology required. What remained was the staircase itself — patient, silent, descending.

Petrov checked her headlamp. She checked her instruments. She checked the provisions in her pack.

Then she started down.

                            [THE END]

                        FORTY-YEAR WAR

                   by Col. James "Buck" Stanton

================================================================================

                               I.

Lieutenant Kael Rennick had been fighting the Dalari for three years, which made him a veteran. In the 94th Expeditionary Brigade, anyone who survived three years was a veteran. Anyone who survived five was a miracle. Anyone who survived ten was either very lucky, very good, or very far from the front.

Rennick was none of these things. He was twenty-four years old, moderately competent, moderately brave, and positioned at Forward Observation Post Seven on the Karros Ridgeline, which was as close to the front as you could get without being dead. The ridgeline overlooked the Carrow Valley — three kilometers of cratered, scorched terrain that separated the Federal lines from the Dalari positions on the opposite ridge. Nothing lived in the valley. Nothing had lived there for decades.

It was 0430, local time. Rennick was on watch. The sky was the color of a bruise — the permanent overcast of Carrow's atmosphere, thick with particulates from forty years of orbital bombardment, surface shelling, and the slow combustion of a continent's worth of vegetation that had burned in the first decade of the war and had never grown back.

The scope showed nothing. The Dalari positions across the valley were dark, their bunkers sealed, their sensor arrays dormant. A quiet night. Rennick had learned not to trust quiet nights.

"Anything?" Sergeant Lida Voss appeared beside him in the observation trench, moving with the silent economy of someone who had spent eight years learning that noise attracted fire. She was thirty-one, compact, with a face that looked as though it had been made from the same material as the ridgeline: hard, weathered, the color of dry clay.

"Nothing."

"Dalari ran a patrol at 0200. Four contacts, northeast sector. They withdrew before our sensors locked."

"Probing?"

"Maybe. Or routine. Hard to tell."

She settled into the trench beside him. They didn't talk. On Carrow, you learned to be comfortable with silence. Conversation was a luxury of rear echelons and leave stations.

Rennick scanned the valley again. Forty years of war had turned it into a landscape that looked like something from a textbook on planetary bombardment — craters overlapping craters, the ground fused in places to a glassy crust, the remains of structures visible here and there as geometric shadows under the dust. This had been farmland once. He'd seen photographs in the brigade's historical archive: green fields, irrigation canals, a town called Carrow that had given the valley its name. The town was gone. The fields were gone. The canals were filled with slag.

He thought about the photographs sometimes. Not often. It wasn't useful.

                               II.

The briefing came at 0800, delivered by Major Tolan in the underground command post that the 94th had carved into the back slope of the ridgeline. The CP was a cave, essentially — reinforced with plascrete and lit by strips of chemical luminescence that gave everyone the complexion of the recently deceased.

Tolan was a career officer, forty-three, with the careful diction and immaculate uniform of a man who had spent more time in briefing rooms than in trenches. He was not incompetent — incompetent officers did not survive on Carrow — but he was cautious in a way that made the line units nervous. Caution in a major meant he was thinking about his next promotion. Thinking about your next promotion meant you were thinking about things other than the people in front of you, and on Carrow, that was how people died.

"Intelligence has intercepted a Dalari communications burst," Tolan said. "Encrypted, sourced from their regional command facility at Sector Nine. Crypto is still working the decrypt, but the signal structure is consistent with a priority operational order."

He activated the situation display. The holographic map of the Carrow theater flickered to life — blue for Federal, red for Dalari, the valley between them a gray no-man's-land.

"We're expecting increased Dalari activity in the next seventy-two hours. Possibly a probe in force. All forward positions will go to heightened alert. Lieutenant Rennick, I want your OP fully manned and sensor-active around the clock."

"Yes, sir."

"Questions?"

There were none. In the 94th, questions were asked privately or not at all.

Rennick returned to the ridgeline and spent the rest of the morning checking equipment, rotating his team through rest cycles, and performing the thousand small tasks that kept a forward observation post alive. He had twelve soldiers under his command — a mix of veterans and replacements, the veterans identifiable by their silence and their habit of sleeping with their weapons within arm's reach.

At 1400, the communications shack received a burst transmission on a frequency that wasn't on any Federal channel list.

Private Soraya Kessler, the OP's communications specialist, brought the intercept to Rennick. She was new — three months on Carrow, still pale, still startled by the sound of distant shelling. But she was good at her job, which was the only thing that mattered.

"It's Dalari, sir. But it's not standard military encryption. It's on a civilian frequency. And it's..." She hesitated. "It's addressed to us. To Federal forces. It's in Standard."

Rennick stared at her. "Play it."

She played it. The voice that came through the speaker was young, male, speaking Standard with the flat vowels and soft consonants of a Dalari accent:

"This is Lieutenant Osen Marek, Dalari Seventh Reconnaissance Group. I am transmitting on an unsecured civilian frequency because I cannot risk using military channels. What I am about to say may sound insane. I assure you it is not. I have discovered something about this war that your command does not want you to know, and my command does not want me to know. I am requesting contact with any Federal officer willing to listen. I will transmit on this frequency every six hours. Marek out."

The recording ended. Rennick sat very still.

"That's a trap," Voss said. She was leaning against the doorframe, arms crossed, her expression flat. "Classic Dalari psyops. They dangle something that sounds important, get one of our people to come out where they can grab them."

"Maybe," Rennick said.

"There's no 'maybe' about it. We report it, let intelligence handle it."

She was right. Standing procedure was clear: unauthorized enemy communications were reported to battalion intelligence immediately, without response, without engagement. The reasons were sound. The Dalari were sophisticated in their use of deception. Three months ago, a platoon leader in the 88th had responded to a similar transmission and walked his entire squad into an ambush.

Rennick knew this. He followed procedure. He filed the intercept with battalion intelligence.

But he didn't stop thinking about it.

                               III.

The next transmission came six hours later, exactly as promised. Rennick listened to it in the communications shack while Kessler recorded.

"This is Marek. I'll assume you're listening, even if you won't respond. Forty years ago, this war started. Federal history says the Dalari attacked the colony at New Carthage without provocation. Dalari history says your fleet bombarded our settlement at Ashkalon first. Both sides have records, testimony, documentation. Both sides believe their version absolutely.

"Three weeks ago, I was assigned to a routine archival detail at our regional command facility. Old records, pre-war. I found something. A communication between the Dalari colonial government and a third party — not Federal, not Dalari — dated six months before the first shots were fired. The communication described a plan. The plan was to provoke a conflict between our two colonies. The mechanism was simple: a coordinated series of false-flag attacks, each designed to look like the other side's work. New Carthage was attacked, but not by us. Ashkalon was bombarded, but not by you.

"The third party had a name. The Herath Consortium. I don't know what they are. I don't know where they are. But the records are clear: they engineered this war. They started it. They've been profiting from it for forty years.

"I need to talk to someone on your side. I need to know if you have records that confirm this. Please respond. Marek out."

The shack was quiet. Kessler was looking at Rennick with an expression he couldn't read. Voss, who had come in halfway through, was looking at the wall.

"I said it was a trap," Voss said.

"You heard what he said."

"I heard a Dalari spin a good story. They're good at stories."

"Herath Consortium."

"I've never heard of them. Have you? Has anyone?"

Rennick hadn't. The name meant nothing. Which was, if you thought about it, exactly what you'd expect if someone had been operating behind the scenes of a forty-year conflict and wanted to remain hidden.

"I need to check the archives," Rennick said.

"The archives."

"We have pre-war records in the brigade historical database. If there's a reference to the Herath Consortium — any reference — it would be there."

Voss was quiet for a long moment. "You're going to get yourself killed. Or court-martialed. Or both."

"I'm going to check a database. That's not a court-martial offense."

"It is if you're checking it because an enemy combatant told you to."

She wasn't wrong. But Rennick went to check the database anyway.

                               IV.

The brigade historical archive was maintained on a dedicated terminal in the command post, accessible to officers with the appropriate clearance. Rennick had the appropriate clearance, though he had never used it — the archive was a resource for intelligence analysts and historians, not for line lieutenants manning observation posts.

He searched for "Herath Consortium." No results.

He searched for "Herath." No results.

He searched for "third party" in combination with "pre-war" and "provocation." No results.

He searched for "false flag." The system returned seventeen results, all of them analyses of known Dalari deception operations. None predated the war.

He was about to close the terminal when he noticed something. The search results page included a metadata field showing the total number of documents in the archive. The number was 847,291. But when he ran a broad search — all documents, no filters — the system returned only 842,006 results.

There were 5,285 documents in the archive that did not appear in any search.

Rennick stared at the number. He was not a technical specialist, and his understanding of database architecture was limited to what he'd learned in officer training. But he knew enough to know that documents that existed in a database but couldn't be found through any search had been deliberately excluded from the index. Someone had hidden them.

He tried to access the hidden documents directly, using the administrative tools available at his clearance level. The system returned an error: ACCESS RESTRICTED — COMMAND AUTHORITY REQUIRED.

He tried three more approaches. Each one returned the same error.

He closed the terminal, returned to the observation post, and sat in the trench looking out over the Carrow Valley. The sky was darkening toward the planet's long, gray dusk. Across the valley, the Dalari positions were beginning to show the faint thermal signatures of evening activity — cook fires, probably, or heating units. The enemy, going about the business of living between the business of killing.

Forty years. He had been born into this war. His parents had fought in it. He had never known a time when the Dalari were not the enemy, when the Carrow Valley was not a killing ground, when the purpose of his life was not to hold this ridge against the people on the other ridge.

And now a voice on the radio was telling him that none of it had needed to happen. That someone had made it happen, on purpose, for profit.

He could dismiss it. Voss was right: it could be a trap, a deception, a Dalari psychological operation designed to undermine Federal morale. The smart play was to report it, forget it, and go back to scanning the valley.

But there were 5,285 documents in the archive that someone didn't want him to see.

                               * * *

He responded to the next transmission.

He waited until 0200, when Kessler was off-shift and Voss was asleep. He went to the communications shack alone, tuned to the civilian frequency, and transmitted.

"Marek, this is Lieutenant Kael Rennick, Federal 94th Expeditionary Brigade. I'm listening. I checked our archives. There are hidden files — over five thousand documents sealed at command authority level. I can't access them. Can you verify the records on your end?"

The response came forty minutes later.

"Rennick. Thank God. Yes, I can verify. I have physical copies — actual paper, from the pre-war archive. I've also found discrepancies in our military database. Sealed files, restricted access, same as yours. Someone on both sides has been hiding the evidence."

There was a pause.

"I need to meet with you. Face to face. I have documents to show you that won't survive transmission — they're paper, and they're fragile, and I've already had to move them twice because my own security people are asking questions about what I was doing in the archive."

"A meeting. In the valley."

"I know. I know what I'm asking. But a transmission can be intercepted, decoded, denied. Physical evidence is harder to dismiss. I have letters, Rennick. Letters between the Dalari colonial governor and the Herath Consortium. Dates, names, specifics. If your intelligence people see these, they'll have to investigate."

Rennick closed his eyes. A meeting in the Carrow Valley. The no-man's-land between the ridges. Three kilometers of open ground covered by sensors, mines, and the overlapping fields of fire of two entrenched armies.

"Where?" he asked.

"There's a structure in the valley floor, approximately halfway between our positions. Pre-war. It used to be a water treatment facility. The walls are still standing. It would provide cover from observation."

Rennick knew the structure. He'd mapped it a dozen times through his scope — a rectangular ruin, half-buried in blast debris, its walls scorched but intact. It was 1,500 meters from his position and roughly the same from the Dalari lines. Reachable, if you moved carefully, at night, through the craters.

"When?"

"Tomorrow night. 0200. I'll come alone."

"So will I."

"Rennick. If this goes wrong — if one of us doesn't come back — the evidence still exists. I've hidden copies. Three locations on my side. If I'm killed, my second — Corporal Thessa Olar — knows where they are. She's the only other person on this side who knows what I've found."

"Understood. Same on my end." He paused. "Marek?"

"Yes?"

"If you're lying to me — if this is an operation — I want you to know that I'll kill you before your people get to me."

A brief silence. Then: "Fair enough. Same goes for me. Marek out."

Rennick sat in the communications shack for a long time after the transmission ended. Then he went to find Kessler. He needed to tell someone. If he didn't come back, someone on this side needed to know.

                               V.

He told Voss, too. He owed her that.

She listened without interrupting, which was how Voss listened to things she profoundly disagreed with. When he finished, she was quiet for thirty seconds.

"I'm going with you," she said.

"No."

"I'm going with you, or I'm reporting this to Major Tolan right now."

"Lida—"

"You're walking into the valley alone to meet an enemy combatant based on a radio transmission. If that's not a trap, it's the worst plan I've heard since Lieutenant Garza tried to flank the Dalari at Ridgeline Four with half a squad and no suppressive fire. Garza got three people killed. You'll get one. I'm not letting that one be you."

Rennick looked at her. In the dim light of the communications shack, her face was unreadable — the same hard, clay-colored mask she wore in the field, the one that concealed whatever she felt about anything.

"If we're both caught—"

"Then Kessler handles the fallout. She's smart enough. And she'll have the recordings."

There was no arguing with Voss when she'd made a decision. Rennick had learned this in three years of serving with her.

"All right," he said. "0130. Tactical gear, no insignia, short-range comms only."

"Understood."

She left. Rennick began preparing.

                               VI.

The valley at night was a dead landscape animated by faint sounds: the tick of cooling metal from shrapnel fragments embedded in the soil, the whisper of wind through the ruins, the distant, irregular concussions of harassing fire from sectors to the north and south. The overcast sky glowed faintly — reflected light from the orbital platforms, filtered through forty years of atmospheric contamination.

Rennick and Voss moved through the craters in a low crouch, picking their way between mine markers and sensor tripwires. They had disabled the Federal sensors in their sector — Kessler, working from the OP, had looped the feeds to show empty terrain — but the Dalari sensors were another matter. They moved slowly, stopping every few meters to listen and scan.

The water treatment facility was a rectangular structure, roughly twenty meters by thirty, its walls standing to a height of about three meters. The roof was gone — blown off in some bombardment decades ago. The interior was rubble and dust, with the remains of filtration equipment still visible under the debris.

They arrived at 0155. Rennick positioned himself inside the structure, behind the remnant of an interior wall. Voss took a position at the south entrance, covering the approach from the Dalari lines.

At 0203, a figure appeared at the north entrance.

He was alone. Slight build, moving carefully, carrying something under one arm. He paused at the entrance, scanned the interior, and then stepped in.

"Rennick?"

"Here."

The figure moved toward him. In the dim glow of the overcast, Rennick could make out a young face — younger than he'd expected. Twenty-two, maybe twenty-three. Dark hair, thin features, the wary eyes of someone who expected to be shot at any moment.

"Marek."

"Show me what you have."

Marek unslung the package from under his arm and unwrapped it. It was a folder — actual paper, yellowed and fragile, the edges crumbling. He opened it carefully and held it out.

Rennick switched on a filtered penlight and read.

The documents were letters. Seven letters, dated across a six-month period forty-one years ago, between the Dalari colonial governor — a man named Prestor Vaahl — and a representative of the Herath Consortium identified only by the initial "K." The letters were in Dalari, with marginal annotations in Standard that appeared to be Marek's translations.

The content was explicit. The Consortium proposed a plan to destabilize relations between the Federal and Dalari colonies on Carrow through a series of staged attacks. The attacks would be carried out by Consortium operatives using equipment and uniforms from both sides — Federal-made weapons for the attacks on Dalari settlements, Dalari-made weapons for the attacks on Federal settlements. The objective was to provoke a military confrontation that would destroy both colonies' agricultural infrastructure, creating a dependency on off-world food supplies. The Consortium would provide those supplies — at a price.

The final letter, dated three months before the war began, confirmed that Governor Vaahl had agreed to the plan. His payment was a guarantee that the Dalari colonial government would survive the conflict, repositioned as a wartime authority with expanded powers.

Rennick read the letters twice. His hands were steady. His voice, when he spoke, was not.

"Your governor knew."

"Yes."

"He agreed to it. He let them start a war."

"He died in the first year. An 'accident' at the governor's residence. The records suggest the Consortium removed him once he was no longer useful."

Rennick looked up from the letters. "And on our side?"

"That's what I need from you. Your hidden files. If the Consortium approached both colonial governments — and the logic of the plan requires it — then your governor received the same letters. Or similar ones."

The 5,285 hidden documents. Rennick felt something cold settle in his stomach — not fear, not anger, but the specific, nauseating sensation of understanding arriving too late.

"I can't access those files from my level. I'd need command authority."

"Then find someone with command authority."

"You're asking me to take this to my superiors. To tell them that the war they've been fighting — that their parents fought, that their grandparents fought — was manufactured. By a third party. For profit."

"Yes."

"They'll court-martial me."

"Maybe. Or maybe some of them already know."

That thought was worse than a court-martial. Rennick looked at the letters again. Forty-one years old. Paper and ink. The handwriting of men who had decided, across a table somewhere, to burn a world for money.

"I'll try," Rennick said.

"That's all I'm asking."

They stood in the ruined water treatment plant — two junior officers from opposite sides of a war that had consumed their entire lives — and looked at each other across a distance that was measured not in meters but in decades of mutual destruction.

"How do we end it?" Rennick asked.

Marek shook his head. "I don't know. But we start by making people see the truth. On both sides. At the same time. A simultaneous disclosure that neither command can suppress."

"That's impossible."

"Forty years ago, someone told the first colonists on Carrow that peace between our peoples was impossible. They believed it. Look where it got us."

Rennick looked out through the ruined wall, across the valley, at the dark ridgeline where his own people waited. Forty years of craters. Forty years of dead. Forty years of hating the people on the other ridge because someone, somewhere, had decided that hate was profitable.

"Give me a week," he said. "I'll find someone on my side. Someone with access. Someone willing to listen."

"A week. Same frequency, same time. If I don't hear from you, I'll assume the worst."

"Marek." Rennick held out the letters. "Take these back. Keep them safe. I'll work from what I've seen."

Marek took the letters. He wrapped them carefully, tucked them under his arm. He looked at Rennick one more time.

"My grandfather was at New Carthage," Marek said quietly. "The attack that started the war. He was a medic. He spent three days pulling survivors out of the rubble. He never talked about it. He never talked about anything, after that."

"My grandmother was at Ashkalon," Rennick said. "On the civilian transport that was hit during the bombardment. She lost her sister."

They stood there. Two men whose families had been wounded by the same lie, told from two directions.

"A week," Marek said. He turned and disappeared through the north entrance, moving silently back toward the Dalari lines.

Rennick stood in the ruin for another minute. Then he signaled Voss, and they began the long, careful crawl back to the ridge.

                               VII.

The week that followed was the most dangerous of Rennick's career, and none of it involved the enemy.

He began by approaching Captain Dara Osei, the brigade intelligence officer — a sharp, guarded woman who had spent twelve years on Carrow and who was, in Rennick's assessment, the most likely officer in the 94th to take the information seriously without immediately reporting him for unauthorized enemy contact.

He told her everything. The transmissions. The meeting. The letters. The 5,285 hidden documents.

Osei listened without expression. When he finished, she was quiet for a very long time.

"You've committed approximately six court-martial offenses," she said.

"I know."

"Unauthorized communication with the enemy. Unauthorized departure from your post. Failure to report intelligence contact through proper channels. Unauthorized access to classified archive systems—"

"I know."

"If I report this, your career is over. You'll spend the next twenty years in a military prison."

"I know."

She looked at him. He looked at her. The command post hummed around them — the sound of a machine that had been running for forty years and would keep running, unless someone reached in and switched it off.

"I've seen the hidden files," Osei said.

Rennick went very still.

"Not all of them. I have a higher clearance than you, but not high enough. There are layers. But I've seen enough to know that there are pre-war documents in that archive that have been classified at the highest level since the war began. Documents that reference an external entity. I always assumed it was a security precaution — protecting intelligence sources. But."

"But."

"But there are too many. And they go back too far. And the classification authority isn't the usual channels — it's a special directive that traces back to the original Federal colonial command. Which no longer exists as a separate entity. Which means the classification can't be reviewed or lifted through normal procedure."

She was quiet again. Then she opened a drawer in her desk and took out a data chip.

"I made a copy. Six months ago. When I first noticed the classification anomalies. I've been sitting on it because I didn't know what it meant and I wasn't sure I wanted to."

She placed the chip on the desk between them.

"There are 847 documents on this chip. They're the ones I could access. Most of them are routine — pre-war administrative correspondence, supply manifests, land surveys. But seventeen of them reference the Herath Consortium by name. And three of them are letters."

"Letters."

"Letters from the Federal colonial governor to a Consortium representative. The content is..." She stopped. Started again. "The content is consistent with what your Dalari friend described. A coordinated plan. Staged attacks. Both sides."

Rennick sat down. The chair was metal, cold, uncomfortable. He barely noticed.

"Who else knows?"

"No one. I told no one. I didn't know what to do with it. I'm still not sure I know."

"Now you do."

"Do I?"

"Marek wants a simultaneous disclosure. Both sides, at the same time. If only one side releases the information, the other side calls it propaganda. But if both sides release it simultaneously — from their own archives, verified by their own intelligence officers—"

"Then neither side can deny it." Osei leaned back in her chair. "It would have to go high enough to matter. A junior intelligence officer and a line lieutenant don't have the authority to stop a war."

"No. But we have the evidence to make the people who do have the authority very uncomfortable."

"Uncomfortable enough to listen. Maybe. Or uncomfortable enough to make us disappear."

That was the risk. Both of them knew it.

Osei picked up the data chip and held it between her thumb and forefinger, studying it the way one studies an object that might be a key or might be a grenade.

"I know a colonel in the Judge Advocate General's office," she said. "A woman named Sorin. She's been asking questions about the war's legal basis for years. She's been stonewalled every time. If I bring her this..."

"Can you trust her?"

"I trust that she wants the truth. Whether that's the same as being trustworthy, I honestly don't know."

It was the best option available. It was also the only option available.

"Do it," Rennick said.

                               VIII.

He transmitted to Marek that night.

"I've found someone. An intelligence officer with partial access to the hidden files. She has documentation confirming the Consortium's involvement. We're bringing it to a senior legal officer. We need time."

Marek's response was immediate. "I've made progress on my end as well. My corporal, Olar, has a cousin in the Dalari military press corps. A journalist. She's willing to publish if we can give her verified documentation. But she needs the Federal side confirmed independently — she won't publish based on Dalari records alone."

"Understood. Give us seventy-two hours."

"You have them. Rennick — be careful. I've noticed increased security around the archive facility. Someone knows I was there. They're looking."

"Same on my side. Osei says the classified files have been accessed more frequently in the last month. Someone's checking."

"The Consortium?"

"Or people who work for them. Same thing."

"Seventy-two hours. Marek out."

Rennick switched off the transmitter. In the observation trench, Voss was waiting. She had heard the transmission — he'd stopped trying to exclude her after the second day.

"Seventy-two hours," she said.

"Yes."

"And then?"

"And then we find out whether the truth is enough to stop a war."

Voss looked out over the valley. The night was quiet. No shelling, no patrols, no fire. Just the wind, the craters, and the darkness between two ridges.

"It should be," she said. "It won't be. But it should be."

                               * * *

The next seventy-two hours were the longest of Rennick's life. Osei met with Colonel Sorin. Sorin, upon reviewing the documents, sat in silence for eleven minutes — Osei counted — and then made a single phone call to someone whose name she did not share. A meeting was arranged. Then another. The information moved upward through the Federal command structure like a crack propagating through ice — slowly at first, then faster, then with a momentum that could not be stopped.

On the Dalari side, Marek's journalist published. The story appeared in a regional military news bulletin — not a major outlet, but credible, sourced, and devastating. It was suppressed within four hours. But four hours was enough. The information had spread. Copies circulated through the Dalari military network, passed hand to hand, officer to officer, in the old way that information has always traveled when authority tries to contain it: through whispers, through trust, through the simple, ungovernable human need to know.

On the fourth day, the Dalari high command proposed a ceasefire. The Federal joint chiefs accepted within six hours.

On the fifth day, a joint investigative commission was announced. Both sides would open their classified archives. Both sides would submit to independent verification. The Herath Consortium — whatever it was, wherever it was — would be identified, located, and held accountable.

On the sixth day, the guns stopped.

Rennick was on watch when it happened. One moment, the valley was the same dead landscape it had always been — scarred, silent, poisoned. The next moment, the sound changed. Not the addition of a new sound, but the subtraction of an old one. The constant, subliminal rumble of artillery fire from the northern and southern sectors — so constant that Rennick had stopped hearing it years ago — ceased.

The silence was enormous. It filled the valley like water filling a basin, rising until it covered everything.

Voss appeared beside him. She stood in the trench and looked out at the quiet valley and the quiet sky and the quiet ridgeline where the Dalari positions were, for the first time in forty years, not aiming at them.

She didn't say anything. Neither did he. There was nothing to say that the silence didn't say better.

Later, he would learn that the Herath Consortium was a trade cartel based in a system three jumps from Carrow, which had been selling weapons, food, and reconstruction materials to both sides for four decades at markup rates that made its directors the wealthiest individuals in the sector. Later, he would testify before the joint commission. Later, he would receive a commendation that he didn't want and a promotion that he didn't ask for.

But that was later. In that moment, Rennick stood on the Karros Ridgeline and listened to the silence, and he thought about a young Dalari lieutenant in a ruined water treatment plant, holding a folder of letters that were older than either of them, saying: We start by making people see the truth.

The truth was simple. It was always simple. The hard part was getting people to look.

                            [THE END]

                          AUCTIONEER

                         by Theodore Cain

================================================================================

                               I.

The auction house occupied the top six floors of the Praxis Tower on Meridian Station, and it was, by common consensus, the most dangerous place in the civilized galaxy to spend an afternoon.

Not dangerous in the way that a battlefield is dangerous, or a derelict ship, or an uncharted planet with an atmosphere that will dissolve your lungs. Dangerous in the way that a room full of very powerful beings who all want the same thing is dangerous — which is to say, socially, financially, and occasionally physically, in ways that the insurance policies did not cover and the station constabulary preferred not to investigate.

Sol Gutierrez had been working the room for eleven years, and he loved every minute of it.

He was fifty-three, broad-shouldered, with a voice that could fill a concert hall and a pair of hands that spoke their own language — pointing, gesturing, chopping the air in the precise semaphore of the professional auctioneer. He wore a gray suit, slightly too large for him, and shoes that were polished to a mirror finish. His hair was silver at the temples, black everywhere else, and his mustache was the most carefully maintained object on the station.

"Lot forty-seven," he announced, his voice carrying to the back of the hall without amplification — a point of professional pride. "A matched pair of Vendalai navigation crystals, provenance certified, estimated age fourteen thousand standard years. I'll start the bidding at two hundred thousand credits. Two hundred thousand. Do I hear two hundred thousand?"

The hall was full. Twelve species, approximately two hundred individuals, arranged in a tiered amphitheater of seats that had been custom-fitted to accommodate anatomies ranging from the humanoid to the distinctly not. The front row was reserved for the major players: the trade delegates, the collection agents, the institutional buyers. Behind them sat the smaller dealers, the private collectors, the speculators. In the back, standing, were the watchers — beings who had come to observe rather than bid, whose presence was itself a form of intelligence-gathering.

The Vendalai crystals went for 340,000 credits, purchased by a Thessian trade delegate whose secondary mandibles clicked with what Gutierrez had learned to interpret as satisfaction. Good. A satisfied buyer was a returning buyer.

He worked through the next dozen lots with practiced efficiency. A Korathi data tablet — 180,000 credits. A set of pre-Contact human artifacts from the third century BCE — 95,000 credits, disappointing but the provenance was disputed. A living sculpture from Pareth-4, which changed shape in response to its owner's emotional state — 510,000 credits, the highest price of the afternoon, purchased by a being whose species Gutierrez didn't recognize and whose payment method he preferred not to examine too closely.

At 1530 station time, they reached Lot 62.

Gutierrez consulted his catalog. Lot 62 was listed as: "Object of unknown origin and function. Acquired from the estate of trader M. Pellerin (deceased). No provenance documentation. Pre-auction estimate: 500-2,000 credits."

He picked up the object from the display table. It was small — roughly the size and shape of a hen's egg, but flattened on one side so that it sat upright without rolling. Its surface was smooth and dark, the color of old blood. It had no markings, no seams, no openings. It weighed almost nothing.

It looked, to Sol Gutierrez's experienced eye, like exactly what it was cataloged as: junk. An unidentifiable trinket from a dead trader's collection, included in the auction because the estate executor had insisted that everything be sold and Gutierrez had learned that arguing with executors was less profitable than auctioning their garbage.

"Lot sixty-two," he said, in the slightly brisker tone he used for minor lots. "Object of unknown origin. Estate piece. I'll start at five hundred credits. Five hundred. Do I hear five hundred?"

A hand went up. The Pelori delegate in the third row — a tall, angular being with iridescent skin and the calm, patient demeanor of a species that measured time in geological epochs. Gutierrez nodded.

"Five hundred credits. Thank you. Do I hear—"

"One thousand." The voice came from the seventh row. Gutierrez looked up. The Drenn trade agent — a compact, gray-skinned being with four arms and an expression that, in Gutierrez's experience, never changed regardless of emotional state. The Drenn bid with one of their lower hands, the gesture precise and deliberate.

"One thousand. Do I hear—"

"Five thousand."

Gutierrez paused. That was the Thessian delegate. The same one who had purchased the Vendalai crystals. Five thousand credits for a junk lot was unusual. Not unprecedented — sometimes collectors saw something others missed — but unusual enough to warrant attention.

"Five thousand. Do I hear—"

"Twenty thousand."

The room shifted. Twenty thousand credits was not a curiosity bid. Twenty thousand credits was intent. Gutierrez looked at the bidder: the Korathi institutional buyer, a being that resembled a collection of articulated stone spheres arranged in a vaguely humanoid configuration. The Korathi never bid impulsively. Their decision-making process involved internal consensus among multiple cognitive nodes, and a bid from a Korathi meant that every node had agreed.

"Twenty thousand credits. Do I hear—"

"Fifty thousand."

"One hundred thousand."

"Two hundred thousand."

The bids came fast now, overlapping, from every section of the hall. Gutierrez's hands worked automatically, acknowledging each bid, tracking the escalation with the trained precision of a man who had managed bidding wars for a decade. But his mind was racing. He looked at the object in his hand — the small, dark, egg-shaped thing that weighed nothing and did nothing and was, according to every analysis the auction house had performed, inert.

"Five hundred thousand credits," he called. "Five hundred thousand for Lot sixty-two. Do I hear—"

"One million."

Silence. The bid had come from the back of the hall, from one of the standing observers — a being that Gutierrez had noticed earlier and filed under "unusual." It was tall, wrapped in a dark garment that concealed most of its anatomy, and it spoke through a translator device that rendered its voice in flat, accentless Standard. Gutierrez didn't recognize its species. That was rare. In eleven years, he had cataloged over sixty species in his personal reference.

"One million credits," Gutierrez said. His voice was steady. His palms were not. "I have one million credits. Do I hear—"

The Pelori delegate stood up. In eleven years of working with Pelori, Gutierrez had never seen one stand up during an auction. They were a seated species, culturally speaking; standing was an expression of extreme urgency, the Pelori equivalent of shouting.

"The auction house must withdraw this lot," the Pelori said. Its voice was calm, but its iridescent skin was cycling through colors that Gutierrez associated with alarm. "Immediately."

"I'm afraid I can't do that, Delegate. Bidding is in progress."

"You do not understand what you are selling."

"That is frequently the case. The auction proceeds. I have one million credits. Do I—"

"Two million," said the Korathi.

"Five million," said the Thessian.

"Ten million," said the being in the back.

Gutierrez set the object down on the display table. He placed both hands flat on the podium and looked out at the room. Two hundred beings, representing twelve species, and every one of them was either bidding or watching the bidding with an intensity that suggested the outcome mattered to them personally.

An object with no provenance, no known function, and no apparent value had just been bid to ten million credits.

"Ladies, gentlemen, and respected beings of all designations," Gutierrez said. "I'm going to call a ten-minute recess."

                               II.

The auction house manager was a human woman named Priya Sharma, and she was waiting for Gutierrez in the back office with an expression that combined professional concern with personal fascination in roughly equal measure.

"What the hell is happening out there?" she asked.

"I was hoping you could tell me." Gutierrez set the object on her desk. "This thing. What do we know about it?"

Sharma consulted her terminal. "Acquired from the Pellerin estate. Pellerin was a freelance trader — mostly junk, the occasional legitimate find. He died on Outreach Station six months ago. Cause of death listed as cardiac arrest, but there were... questions."

"Questions."

"He was found in his quarters with the door sealed from the inside and the environmental controls disabled. The coroner ruled natural causes, but the investigating officer noted that Pellerin had filed a complaint with station security two days earlier reporting that he was being followed."

Gutierrez looked at the object. It sat on the desk, dark and smooth and silent.

"What about the object itself?"

"Standard analysis. Composition: unknown. The spectrometer couldn't get a reading — the surface absorbs every frequency we threw at it. No electromagnetic emissions. No thermal signature. No radiation. It weighs forty-two grams and does not appear to interact with any known form of energy."

"So it's inert."

"As far as we can tell."

"Then why are twelve species trying to buy it?"

Sharma didn't have an answer for that. Neither did Gutierrez. He picked up the object and turned it in his hands. It was warm — body temperature, as though it had absorbed the heat of his palm. Its surface was perfectly smooth, without texture or irregularity. When he held it up to the light, it seemed to absorb that too — no reflection, no sheen, just a flat, dark surface that the eye slid across without finding purchase.

"The Pelori delegate asked me to withdraw the lot."

"We can't withdraw a lot during active bidding. The legal liability—"

"I know. But the Pelori don't make requests lightly. And the bidding is at ten million for something we valued at two thousand."

Sharma was quiet for a moment. "What do you want to do?"

Gutierrez thought about it. He was an auctioneer. His job was to sell things. The object was legally consigned, the bidding was legitimate, and the auction house's commission on ten million credits would be substantial.

But he had been in this business long enough to recognize when something was wrong. Not wrong in the way that a disputed provenance was wrong, or a suspected forgery, or a buyer with questionable funds. Wrong in the way that the atmosphere in the room was wrong — charged, electric, desperate, like the air before a storm.

"I want to know what it is," he said. "Before I sell it."

"How?"

"I'm going to ask."

                               III.

He invited the Pelori delegate to the back office. The Pelori came reluctantly, its skin still cycling through agitated colors. It folded itself into a chair that was too small for its frame and regarded Gutierrez with compound eyes that held, he thought, something very close to pity.

"Tell me what it is," Gutierrez said.

The Pelori was silent for a long time. Pelori silences could last minutes. Gutierrez had learned to wait.

"We call it the Seed," the Pelori said at last. "That is not its true name. It has no name in any language currently spoken. It predates language."

"What does it do?"

"It contains a template. A complete informational template for a living species — their biology, their technology, their culture, their history. Every individual who ever lived. Every thought that was ever articulated. Every work of art, every scientific discovery, every song."

"Whose species?"

"We do not know. The species that created the Seed is extinct. They have been extinct for approximately two billion standard years. The Seed is all that remains of them."

Gutierrez looked at the object on the desk. Forty-two grams. The size of an egg.

"And what is it for? What does someone do with it?"

"The Seed is not passive. It is a mechanism. Given sufficient energy — and the threshold is remarkably low — it activates. When activated, it uses the template to reconstruct the species. Completely. From nothing. It creates them — their bodies, their minds, their civilization — in the space around the Seed. Instantly. Billions of individuals, fully conscious, fully realized, with the complete history and culture of their species intact."

Gutierrez felt something cold move through him. "It creates a species."

"It resurrects one. A species that has been dead for two billion years. It brings them back."

"And then what?"

"That is the question, Mr. Gutierrez. That is the question that makes every species in your auction hall either desperate to possess the Seed or terrified that someone else will. Because the species that the Seed contains — whoever they were, whatever they were — they were old when the earliest known civilizations were young. They predate everything. And no one knows what they will do when they return."

"The bidding war—"

"Some species wish to possess the Seed to activate it — to bring back a species of unimaginable antiquity and learn from them. Others wish to possess it to prevent activation — to ensure that a two-billion-year-old civilization is never unleashed on a galaxy that has evolved in their absence. Both positions are rational. Both are terrified of the other."

Gutierrez sat back in his chair. He looked at the Seed. Forty-two grams, sitting on a desk in the back office of an auction house on a middleweight commercial station. The most valuable and the most dangerous object in the galaxy, cataloged as junk.

"You want me to withdraw it."

"I want you to destroy it."

"I can't do that."

"Then withdraw it. Remove it from the auction. Place it in secure containment. Contact the Galactic Council. Let the appropriate authorities—"

"The appropriate authorities are in my auction hall, bidding on it. Every major species is represented. If I withdraw the lot, they'll simply try to acquire it through other means. At least in the auction, the process is transparent. Everyone can see who bids and who wins."

The Pelori's skin cycled through a sequence of colors that Gutierrez had never seen before — deep violet to pale gold to a shade of red that hurt to look at.

"You do not understand. If the wrong species acquires this object—"

"Which species is the wrong species?"

"Any of them. All of them. Including mine."

The Pelori stood. It looked down at Gutierrez with compound eyes that held two billion years of apprehension.

"You are a seller of objects, Mr. Gutierrez. This is not an object. It is a decision. The most consequential decision that any living being in this galaxy will ever make. And you are about to make it based on who has the most money."

It left. Gutierrez sat alone in the back office with the Seed on his desk and the sound of two hundred agitated beings audible through the wall.

                               IV.

He returned to the podium. The room was restless — delegates conferring in clusters, translators working overtime, the atmosphere thick with the particular tension of beings who are accustomed to power and are not accustomed to waiting.

Gutierrez placed the Seed on the display table. He adjusted his lapel. He cleared his throat.

"Ladies, gentlemen, and respected beings. Lot sixty-two. I have a standing bid of ten million credits. The recess is concluded. Bidding resumes."

"Fifteen million," the Korathi said immediately.

"Twenty million," said the Thessian.

"Thirty million," said the unknown being in the back.

"Fifty million," said a voice from the left — the Drenn delegate, all four arms extended in the formal posture of a maximum-authority bid. Gutierrez knew what that meant. The Drenn delegate wasn't bidding on behalf of a trade house. This was a government bid, backed by the full resources of the Drenn Collective.

"One hundred million," said another voice — the Malaxi observer, a translucent being who had been silent throughout the auction and who now spoke with a resonance that vibrated the walls. Government bid, undoubtedly.

The room fell quiet. One hundred million credits. The sum was staggering — sufficient to purchase a small fleet, or a colony world, or the output of a mid-range industrial system for a decade.

Gutierrez looked around the room. Every species was watching. Some with hunger. Some with fear. Some with both.

The being in the back spoke again. "Five hundred million credits."

The silence that followed was different from the silences before. It was the silence of a room full of beings who have all simultaneously realized that they are in the presence of something they cannot outbid, cannot outmaneuver, and cannot walk away from.

"Five hundred million credits," Gutierrez repeated. His voice was steady. His hands were clasped behind his back where no one could see them trembling. "I have five hundred million credits. Do I hear—"

"One billion," the Pelori said.

Every head, every sensory organ, every perceptual apparatus in the room turned toward the Pelori delegate. Its skin was the color of deep ocean — a shade that Gutierrez had never seen and that, he realized with a start, he was never meant to see. It was the color of a species that had lived for geological epochs making a decision that it knew would define the next geological epoch.

"One billion credits," Gutierrez said. "Do I hear—"

"Two billion." The being in the back.

"Five billion." The Korathi.

"Ten billion." The Pelori.

Gutierrez set his gavel on the podium. The bidding had exceeded the total annual economic output of most inhabited systems. The numbers had ceased to be numbers and had become something else — declarations, ultimatums, the economic equivalent of battleships maneuvering into position.

He looked at the Seed. Forty-two grams. Dark and smooth and warm.

He looked at the room. Twelve species. Two hundred beings. Every one of them willing to bankrupt their civilization for a trinket the size of an egg.

"I'm going to do something unusual," he said.

The room went very still.

"I am going to ask each delegate to tell me — briefly, in this forum, for the record — what they intend to do with this object if they acquire it. I have a professional responsibility to ensure that items sold through this house are not used for purposes that constitute a threat to galactic peace. I am invoking that responsibility now."

A murmur ran through the room. The Drenn delegate's four arms shifted to a posture of objection. The Thessian clicked its mandibles. The unknown being in the back said nothing.

The Pelori spoke first. "We wish to contain it. To prevent activation. To protect the galaxy from the consequences of resurrecting a species that has been dead for longer than most stars have burned."

The Korathi: "We wish to study it. To understand the template technology. Not to activate it, but to learn from it. The information contained within the Seed represents the greatest scientific resource in existence."

The Drenn: "We wish to secure it. An object of this power must be under the authority of a stable government. We offer protection, containment, and neutral stewardship."

The Thessian: "We wish to auction it among ourselves. The Thessian Conclave includes forty-seven member species. We would decide collectively."

The Malaxi: "We wish to communicate with it. Our species has sensory modalities that may allow interaction with the template without full activation. We wish to listen."

One by one, the delegates spoke. Containment. Study. Stewardship. Communication. Destruction. Activation. The answers were as varied as the species that gave them, and each one was, in its own way, rational, principled, and absolutely irreconcilable with every other answer.

The unknown being in the back spoke last.

"I wish to return it."

The room went silent.

"Return it where?" Gutierrez asked.

"To the place where it was made. The Seed was not created to be found by you. It was created to be carried back. There is a location — a specific point in space — where the Seed was launched two billion years ago. The return of the Seed to that point is the activation condition. Not energy. Location. The species does not revive in the space around the Seed. The species revives in the space around the point of origin. A space that has been prepared for two billion years."

"And you know where this point is?"

"I am from that point."

The silence was absolute. Two hundred beings stared at the figure in the back of the room. Gutierrez felt the air pressure in the room change — or imagined he did — as though something fundamental about the atmosphere had shifted.

"You are—"

"I am the last. The very last. The Seed was a precaution. A mechanism of return, sent out into the galaxy before the end, programmed to find its way back when it was ready. I have been waiting."

Gutierrez looked at the Seed. He looked at the being in the back. He looked at the room full of delegates — twelve species, each with their own civilization, their own history, their own reasons for wanting this small, dark object.

He picked up his gavel. The wood was warm in his palm, worn smooth by eleven years of contact. He had sold crystals and sculptures and artifacts and curiosities. He had sold the beautiful and the mundane. He had never sold a decision about the fate of a galaxy.

"Going once," he said.

No one spoke.

"Going twice."

The room was still.

Gutierrez raised the gavel. He held it there, at the peak of its arc, and looked out at two hundred beings who were waiting for him to bring it down and determine the course of history.

He did not bring it down.

"Lot sixty-two is withdrawn," he said. "Pending further review."

The room erupted. Delegates shouted, translators overloaded, the Thessian's mandibles produced a sound like breaking glass. Security personnel moved to the aisles. The Pelori sank back into its chair, its skin fading to a neutral gray.

The being in the back stood motionless, watching Gutierrez with senses that were older than the stars.

Gutierrez placed the gavel on the podium, picked up the Seed, and walked to the back office. He closed the door. He sat down at Sharma's desk, placed the Seed in front of him, and stared at it.

Forty-two grams. The most valuable and the most dangerous. The answer that was both.

He had no idea what to do with it. But he knew one thing: the decision was too large for an auction. It was too large for any one species, any one government, any one being. It was a decision that belonged to everyone, and that meant it belonged to no one, and that meant it would sit on his desk until someone — or something — figured out a better way to make it.

Sol Gutierrez, auctioneer, leaned back in his chair and looked at the Seed of a species that had been dead for two billion years and was waiting, patiently, to be born again.

"Well," he said, to no one. "That's a first."

                            [THE END]

                        BUTTERFLY BALLOT

                        by Douglas Pemberton

================================================================================

                               I.

It is a truth universally acknowledged — or, at any rate, acknowledged by the Constitution of the Democratic Republic of Floris — that a properly conducted election requires butterflies.

Not metaphorical butterflies, mind you, nor the sort of vague, poetic butterflies that lesser democracies might employ as symbols on their campaign literature. Actual butterflies. Living, wing-bearing, antenna-twitching Lepidoptera, genetically coded to the individual voter, released into the Counting Garden at the appointed hour to alight upon the flower of their choice — each flower representing a candidate, each landing representing a vote, and the whole business supervised by the Supreme Electoral Commission with a solemnity that would put the College of Cardinals to shame.

The system had been in place for two hundred and seventeen years, and in that time it had produced forty-three peaceful transitions of power, zero contested elections, and one minor scandal involving a candidate who attempted to coat his flower with synthetic nectar (he was disqualified, fined, and — in the uniquely Florisian form of political exile — reassigned to the Bureau of Pollination Management, where he spent the remainder of his career counting aphids).

It was, by any reasonable standard, a perfectly good system of government. The butterflies were reliable, the flowers were honest, and the citizens of Floris took a justifiable pride in having devised a form of democracy that was both aesthetically pleasing and statistically rigorous. The genetic coding ensured one butterfly per voter. The Counting Garden's controlled environment eliminated weather as a variable. The Supreme Electoral Commission, staffed entirely by entomologists, brought a scientific impartiality to the electoral process that politicians, being politicians, could not be trusted to provide.

And then, in the election of Standard Year 2347, a butterfly landed on the wrong flower.

                               II.

The facts, as initially reported, were simple.

The election in question was for the office of First Minister — the chief executive of the Republic, elected every six years by universal butterfly suffrage. The candidates were two: Madame Celeste Aurillac, the incumbent, a formidable woman of sixty-three who had governed with the calm efficiency of someone who genuinely believed that most problems could be solved by leaving them alone, and Mr. Prosper Delacroix, the challenger, a man of forty-one who had never held public office but who possessed a magnificent head of hair and a talent for saying things that sounded important without actually meaning anything.

The polls — conducted, naturally, by surveying butterfly behavior in sample gardens throughout the Republic — had predicted a narrow victory for Madame Aurillac. The margin was expected to be approximately forty thousand votes out of a total electorate of twelve million, which translated, in butterfly terms, to approximately forty thousand more landings on the aurillac-blue chrysanthemum than on the delacroix-gold marigold.

On Election Day, the citizens of Floris released their butterflies. Twelve million genetically coded Lepidoptera took wing from twelve million homes and made their way, through the Republic's extensive network of garden corridors, to the Counting Garden in the capital city of Papillon. The journey took, on average, four hours. The butterflies arrived in waves, filling the Garden's vast glass dome with a blizzard of iridescent wings, and began to settle on the flowers.

The count was conducted by the Supreme Electoral Commission, a body of twelve senior entomologists armed with magnifying glasses, specimen notebooks, and the quiet authority of people who spend their lives studying things that most citizens consider beneath notice. The Chief Justice of the Commission was Dr. Marguerite Fontaine, a woman of seventy whose knowledge of Lepidoptera was encyclopedic and whose tolerance for political nonsense was approximately zero.

At 1800 hours, Dr. Fontaine announced the preliminary results.

Madame Aurillac: 5,999,999 votes.
Mr. Delacroix: 5,999,999 votes.

A perfect tie.

The nation gasped. The news networks erupted. The candidates' campaign managers began making phone calls of escalating urgency. Dr. Fontaine, unmoved by any of this, ordered a recount.

The recount confirmed the preliminary results. Twelve million butterflies had been released. Eleven million, nine hundred ninety-nine thousand, nine hundred and ninety-eight had landed on one of the two candidate flowers. The remaining two butterflies were unaccounted for.

Or rather, one was unaccounted for. The other — butterfly number 7,341,206, genetically coded to one Ms. Odette Blanchard of the suburban district of Feuille-Verte — had landed on a flower. But not a candidate flower. It had landed on a decorative petunia in the border arrangement at the eastern edge of the Counting Garden, approximately 1.4 meters from the nearest aurillac-blue chrysanthemum and 2.7 meters from the nearest delacroix-gold marigold.

Ms. Blanchard's butterfly had, in the language of Florisian electoral law, "cast an indeterminate ballot."

And Ms. Blanchard's butterfly, it rapidly became clear, was the deciding vote.

                               III.

The constitutional crisis that followed was, in the considered opinion of political observers across the Republic, the most entertaining thing to happen in Florisian politics since the aphid scandal of 2293.

The central question was simple: Which flower had butterfly 7,341,206 intended to land on?

The question's simplicity was, of course, deceptive, in the way that all simple questions are deceptive when they arrive in the vicinity of lawyers, politicians, and constitutional scholars. Within twenty-four hours, the question had spawned a constellation of subsidiary questions, each more baroque than the last.

Did the butterfly's proximity to the aurillac-blue chrysanthemum (1.4 meters) versus the delacroix-gold marigold (2.7 meters) constitute evidence of intent? Could butterfly intent be inferred from distance?

Was the decorative petunia an "authorized flower" within the meaning of the Electoral Code? If not, did the butterfly's landing constitute a valid vote, an invalid vote, or a vote for a third candidate — namely, the petunia itself?

Had the butterfly been influenced by external factors? The Counting Garden's environmental controls were precise, but a defense attorney with the campaign name Delacroix and Associates had already filed a motion arguing that a "localized microclimate anomaly" near the eastern border might have affected the butterfly's flight path.

And, most profoundly: Did butterflies have intent at all? The genetic coding determined which butterfly belonged to which voter, but the butterfly's choice of flower was, in the strict behavioral sense, a product of neurological responses to color, scent, and environmental stimuli. Could a neurological response constitute a democratic choice? And if it could, what did that say about democracy?

This last question was raised by Professor Gaston Moreau of the University of Papillon's Department of Political Entomology, a man who had spent his entire career at the intersection of two disciplines that no one else considered intersectable, and who now found himself, somewhat to his alarm, the most quoted academic in the Republic.

"The butterfly ballot is predicated on the assumption that the butterfly's choice is a meaningful proxy for the voter's intent," Professor Moreau wrote, in an op-ed that was reprinted in fourteen newspapers. "But a butterfly does not choose in the way that a human being chooses. A butterfly responds. The question before the Supreme Electoral Commission is not what the butterfly intended, but what we mean by intention — and whether our democracy can survive the discovery that its foundational metaphor may be, in the strictest scientific sense, meaningless."

The op-ed was widely read, widely misunderstood, and widely used as ammunition by both sides.

Madame Aurillac's campaign argued that the butterfly's proximity to the chrysanthemum constituted a clear indication of voter preference. "The butterfly was heading for the chrysanthemum," said her spokesman, with the confidence of a man who had never studied butterfly navigation. "It simply hadn't arrived yet."

Mr. Delacroix's campaign argued that the butterfly's failure to land on any candidate flower constituted a spoiled ballot, which should be discarded, leaving the election tied at 5,999,999 to 5,999,999 — a result that, under the Electoral Code, would be resolved by a run-off election. (Mr. Delacroix's poll numbers, it was noted, had improved significantly since the crisis began. There is nothing like a constitutional emergency to make a man with magnificent hair look like a leader.)

The Supreme Electoral Commission, under Dr. Fontaine's direction, declined to rule pending a full investigation. The investigation would include a forensic analysis of the butterfly's flight path (reconstructed from the Garden's sensor network), a behavioral assessment of the butterfly itself (which was being held in protective custody in a glass-topped specimen box), and expert testimony from the Republic's foremost lepidopterists.

The Republic settled in to watch.

                               IV.

The hearing convened two weeks later in the Grand Chamber of the Supreme Electoral Commission, a wood-paneled room that had been built in 2130 and had, until now, hosted nothing more dramatic than the annual certification of butterfly genetic codes.

Dr. Fontaine presided. The Commission's eleven associate justices sat in a semicircle behind a long bench, each with a magnifying glass at the ready — a traditional accessory that was, in this case, genuinely useful, as the principal exhibit was a butterfly.

Butterfly 7,341,206 occupied a place of honor on a velvet cushion in the center of the courtroom. It was a Blue Monarch — a species endemic to Floris, distinguished by wings of a deep cerulean that shaded to violet at the tips. It sat on its cushion with the imperturbable calm of a creature that was unaware it had become the most important butterfly in the history of democratic governance.

The first expert witness was Dr. Armand Leclerc, Professor of Behavioral Lepidoptery at the University of Papillon and the author of the definitive text on butterfly navigation, "Wings and Intentions: A Neurological Study of Lepidopteral Decision-Making."

"In your expert opinion," asked the Commission's counsel, "did butterfly 7,341,206 intend to land on the aurillac-blue chrysanthemum?"

Dr. Leclerc adjusted his glasses. "Butterflies do not intend. Butterflies respond to stimuli. The Blue Monarch navigates primarily by color gradient, with secondary input from olfactory cues. The specimen in question — a healthy female, approximately four weeks post-emergence — would have been responsive to blue-spectrum wavelengths, which would draw her toward the chrysanthemum, and to certain terpene compounds present in both the chrysanthemum and the marigold."

"Then why did she land on the petunia?"

"The decorative petunia in question — Petunia floribunda, cultivar 'Midnight Blue' — presents a color profile that, to the Monarch's compound eyes, is virtually indistinguishable from the aurillac-blue chrysanthemum. The butterfly was responding to a color stimulus that she processed as identical to the candidate flower."

A murmur ran through the courtroom.

"So the butterfly thought she was landing on the chrysanthemum?"

"I would not use the word 'thought.' I would say that the butterfly's neurological response to the petunia's color profile was consistent with the response that would have been produced by the chrysanthemum. In behavioral terms, the butterfly treated the petunia as the chrysanthemum."

This was extremely good news for Madame Aurillac's campaign and extremely bad news for Mr. Delacroix's, and the courtroom buzzed accordingly.

But the defense had its own expert. Dr. Solange Picard, Associate Professor of Electoral Botany at the Floris Technical Institute, took the stand and argued, with considerable force, that the color similarity between the petunia and the chrysanthemum constituted a flaw in the Counting Garden's design. If the Garden contained flowers that could be mistaken for candidate flowers, then the Garden was defective, the election was compromised, and the results should be set aside.

"You cannot have a Counting Garden that counts wrong," Dr. Picard said. "That is the one thing a Counting Garden must not do."

"The Garden did not count wrong," Dr. Leclerc objected. "The Garden recorded the butterfly's landing accurately. The butterfly simply landed in an unexpected location."

"An unexpected location that the Garden should have anticipated and prevented."

"You are blaming the Garden for the butterfly's decision."

"I am blaming the Garden for making the decision ambiguous."

"All decisions are ambiguous! That is the nature of decision-making!"

Dr. Fontaine rapped her gavel. The courtroom fell silent.

"We will adjourn," she said, "until tomorrow. And I will remind both experts that this is a court of entomological law, not a philosophical debating society."

                               V.

The hearing continued for six days. Seventeen expert witnesses testified on matters including butterfly neurology, flower chromatography, garden design standards, the philosophical foundations of democratic consent, and the mating habits of the Blue Monarch (this last topic having been introduced by a defense witness on the theory that the butterfly might have been distracted by reproductive urges rather than political ones; the theory was entertained briefly and then abandoned when Dr. Fontaine observed, dryly, that reproductive urges were not a recognized basis for electoral challenge).

The nation watched with the rapt attention that Florisians normally reserved for the annual Butterfly Festival. Betting markets opened on the outcome. Editorials multiplied. The butterfly itself, still on its velvet cushion, became a celebrity; a toy manufacturer produced a plush replica that sold three hundred thousand units in a week.

On the seventh day, Dr. Fontaine delivered the Commission's ruling.

"The Supreme Electoral Commission," she read, in a voice that carried the calm authority of a woman who had been identifying butterfly species since the age of nine, "has considered the evidence and argument presented in the matter of the General Election for First Minister, Standard Year 2347.

"It is the finding of this Commission that butterfly 7,341,206, genetically coded to voter Odette Blanchard of Feuille-Verte, did land on a flower that was not a designated candidate flower. The landing is therefore, under the strict terms of the Electoral Code, indeterminate.

"However, the Commission further finds that the behavioral evidence overwhelmingly indicates that the butterfly's neurological response was directed toward the color profile of the aurillac-blue chrysanthemum, and that the butterfly's landing on the petunia was the result of a design flaw in the Counting Garden — specifically, the presence of a non-candidate flower with a color profile substantially similar to a candidate flower.

"The Commission therefore rules as follows: First, the vote of butterfly 7,341,206 is declared valid and is attributed to the candidate represented by the aurillac-blue chrysanthemum, Madame Celeste Aurillac. Second, the Electoral Code is hereby amended to require that all non-candidate flora in the Counting Garden present a color profile that differs from any candidate flower by a minimum of forty nanometers across the visible spectrum. Third, all decorative petunias are banned from the Counting Garden effective immediately."

She looked up from her text.

"Madame Aurillac is elected First Minister by a margin of one vote. This Commission's ruling is final."

The courtroom erupted. Madame Aurillac's supporters cheered. Mr. Delacroix's supporters objected. Mr. Delacroix himself, standing at the back of the courtroom with his magnificent hair slightly disheveled, had the expression of a man who had just lost an election to a butterfly and was not entirely sure how he felt about it.

Ms. Odette Blanchard of Feuille-Verte, reached at her home by reporters, expressed surprise that her butterfly had caused such a fuss. "She's always been a bit wayward," Ms. Blanchard said, referring to the butterfly. "She landed on my neighbor's laundry last week. I thought nothing of it."

Butterfly 7,341,206, released from protective custody the following morning, flew to the nearest blue flower and landed on it without incident.

The Republic of Floris, having survived its greatest constitutional crisis, returned to the agreeable business of governing itself by butterfly.

And if, in subsequent elections, the citizens of Floris noticed that the Counting Garden's decorative borders had been replaced with a uniform ground cover of pale green moss — aesthetically unfortunate, perhaps, but chromatographically unambiguous — they understood, and approved, and released their butterflies with confidence.

Democracy, after all, is a delicate thing. Rather like a butterfly. It requires careful handling, a suitable environment, and the occasional willingness to rearrange the garden.

                        WHOSE SKILL IS THIS?

                        by Arthur Wainwright

================================================================================

                               I.

The hands arrived in a brushed-aluminum case, packed in custom-molded foam, with a serial number etched on each wrist plate and a calibration certificate signed by three engineers. Dr. Marcus Webb opened the case on the morning of March 14th, in the sterile white room that Meridian Prosthetics had designated for the fitting, and looked at the hands for a long time without touching them.

They were beautiful. That was the first thing — the thing he had not expected, though he should have. Meridian's marketing materials emphasized precision, responsiveness, and integration with the existing nervous system. They said nothing about beauty. But the hands were beautiful: articulated in titanium and surgical-grade polymer, with joints that moved with the fluid grace of a concert pianist's fingers, each segment tapering to a fine point that was simultaneously a fingertip and an instrument. The surface was matte silver, faintly warm to the touch — heated by a network of microfilaments that maintained skin temperature for patient comfort.

"They're the Mark VII," said Dr. Elena Voss, the Meridian engineer assigned to the fitting. She was a small, precise woman who spoke about prosthetics the way some people speak about children — with a mixture of pride and concern. "Twenty-four degrees of freedom per hand. Haptic feedback resolution down to fifty microns. Force sensitivity from point-one to two hundred newtons. And the surgical suite" — she pressed a control on the wrist plate, and the fingertips reconfigured, extruding microsurgical tools with a speed and smoothness that made Webb's breath catch — "scalpel, cauterizer, retractor, suture driver, and micro-forceps, all integrated."

Webb flexed his own hands — his biological hands, the ones he had used for thirty-one years of surgery, from his first trembling incision as an intern to the seventeen-hour spinal reconstruction that had won him the Lasker Prize. The hands that had begun, in the last two years, to fail him.

Essential tremor. The diagnosis had come eighteen months ago, delivered by a neurologist whose name Webb had already forgotten but whose words he remembered precisely: "The tremor is progressive. It will worsen. Surgical career expectancy: twelve to eighteen months."

Twelve to eighteen months. The span of time in which Marcus Webb — who had rebuilt spinal columns, reconnected severed nerves, and performed surgery on structures so small they could only be seen through an operating microscope — would lose the ability to hold a scalpel steady.

Meridian Prosthetics had offered an alternative. The Mark VII surgical hands: a complete replacement of both biological hands from the wrist down, providing a level of precision and control that no human hand, tremoring or otherwise, could match.

Webb looked at the hands in their case. Then he looked at his own hands. They were trembling, faintly but perceptibly, the way they always trembled now.

"Let's proceed," he said.

                               II.

The surgery took four hours. Webb was awake for all of it — local anesthesia to the forearms, neural blocks to manage pain, and a screen positioned so that he could watch if he chose. He chose. He was a surgeon. He wanted to see.

His biological hands were removed cleanly at the wrist joint — a procedure that was, from a technical standpoint, unremarkable. The nerve bundles were prepared, the vascular connections mapped, the bone surfaces shaped to receive the prosthetic mounts. Then the Mark VII hands were attached: the mounts locked into place with a series of precise clicks, the neural interfaces made contact with the prepared nerve bundles, and the vascular connections were established through synthetic capillaries that Meridian's literature described as "biocompatible microfluidic networks."

The neural integration was the critical moment. When the interfaces activated, Webb felt — for an instant that expanded to fill his entire awareness — a sensation that he could only describe as his hands waking up. Not numbness fading, not feeling returning. Something new. As though he had gained a sense that he had not previously possessed.

He raised his new hands and looked at them. The fingers moved when he willed them to move, with a responsiveness that was not merely adequate but uncanny. There was no delay. No clumsiness. No learning curve. The neural interface mapped his motor intentions to the prosthetic actuators with a fidelity that felt less like translation and more like amplification.

He touched his thumb to each fingertip in sequence. The haptic feedback was extraordinary — he could feel texture, temperature, pressure, with a resolution that exceeded his biological hands by an order of magnitude. He could feel the weave of the bedsheet. He could feel the individual fibers.

"How does it feel?" Voss asked.

Webb closed his new hands into fists and opened them again. The movement was fluid, powerful, precise. There was no tremor. His hands were as still as stone.

"It feels," he said, "like I've been operating with mittens on. For my entire career."

                               III.

He returned to the operating room six weeks later, after the rehabilitation period that Meridian's protocols required. The rehabilitation was, in Webb's assessment, unnecessary — the hands responded to his intentions with a precision that made practice redundant — but he was a careful man, and he followed the protocols.

His first surgery with the Mark VII hands was a routine spinal fusion. He had performed the procedure hundreds of times. It took him forty-seven minutes. His previous best time for the same procedure was one hour and twelve minutes.

The difference was not merely speed. It was everything. The hands moved through the surgical field with a confidence and precision that Webb had never experienced. He could feel structures through the instruments with a sensitivity that bordered on the telepathic — the density of bone, the elasticity of ligament, the fragile give of nerve tissue. The microsurgical tools extruded from his fingertips as needed, each one positioning itself with a speed and accuracy that no human hand could match.

The surgical team watched in silence. The attending nurse — a veteran of twenty years — later told a colleague that watching Webb operate with the new hands was "like watching someone perform surgery in a different dimension. Same movements, same procedure, but elevated. As if he'd been doing it in charcoal sketches his whole career and had suddenly switched to oil paints."

Over the next three months, Webb performed sixty-two surgeries. His outcomes were, by every measurable standard, the best of his career. Complication rates dropped to near zero. Recovery times shortened. Procedures that had previously been considered too complex or too risky became routine.

He was, by any objective measure, a better surgeon than he had ever been.

And that was the problem.

                               IV.

The thought arrived slowly, the way thoughts do when they concern things you would rather not think about. It arrived in the spaces between surgeries, in the quiet moments when Webb sat in his office and looked at his hands — the beautiful, silver, perfect hands — and tried to remember what it had felt like to operate with his own.

The tremor had been there, at the end. That was undeniable. The tremor would have ended his career. The Mark VII hands had saved it. He should feel gratitude. He did feel gratitude.

But beneath the gratitude, like a stone beneath clear water, was a question: Whose skill was this?

When he performed a surgery, was the precision his — the product of thirty-one years of training, practice, and experience — or was it the hands'? When he executed a microsurgical maneuver with a steadiness that no biological hand could achieve, was that a reflection of his judgment or his hardware?

The distinction mattered. It mattered in the way that the distinction between a great pianist and a great piano matters. A great pianist playing a mediocre instrument is still a great pianist. A mediocre pianist playing a great instrument is still mediocre — the instrument does not confer skill.

But the Mark VII hands were not a piano. They did not merely transmit his intentions. They enhanced them. The neural interface did not simply translate his motor commands; it optimized them, smoothing micro-hesitations, compensating for suboptimal approach angles, adjusting force in real-time based on tissue feedback. The hands did not just do what he told them to do. They did what he meant to do, better than he could have done it himself.

Where, in that process, did Marcus Webb end and the Mark VII begin?

He asked Voss. She came to his office on a Tuesday afternoon, sat in the chair across from his desk, and listened to his question with the careful attention of an engineer hearing a user report.

"The hands are tools," she said. "Sophisticated tools, but tools. The surgical judgment — where to cut, what to avoid, when to stop — that's yours. The hands execute your decisions with greater precision than your biological hands could. But the decisions are yours."

"And the optimization? The real-time adjustments?"

"Feedback loops. The same principle as a thermostat. The hands sense tissue resistance and adjust force accordingly. It's not decision-making. It's response."

"A thermostat doesn't perform surgery."

"Neither do the hands. You perform surgery. The hands assist."

Webb looked at his hands. They rested on the desk, motionless, silver and still. They were exactly as still as he wanted them to be. They were always exactly as still as he wanted them to be.

"When I operated with my own hands," he said, "there were moments — rare moments, in difficult procedures — when my hands did something that I hadn't consciously decided to do. A correction, a repositioning, an adjustment. My hands knew what to do before my conscious mind did. We called it instinct, but it was really just the accumulated experience of thousands of surgeries expressed through neuromuscular memory."

"The Mark VII preserves your neuromuscular patterns. They're mapped during the integration process. Your instincts are encoded in the neural interface."

"But are they the same? Or are they a copy?"

Voss was quiet for a moment. "From an engineering standpoint, there is no meaningful difference between an instinct and a faithful reproduction of that instinct."

"From a human standpoint," Webb said, "there might be."

                               V.

The recall notice arrived on a Thursday morning, in an envelope bearing Meridian Prosthetics' corporate letterhead. Webb read it at his kitchen table, over coffee, with the particular attention to detail that his profession had trained into him.

The Mark VII surgical hands, the letter explained, had been found to contain a defect in the neural interface firmware. The defect was subtle — a latency anomaly in the haptic feedback loop that manifested under specific conditions of sustained high-precision operation. In plain language: during long, complex surgeries, the hands might, under certain circumstances, report tissue density inaccurately.

The defect had been discovered during quality testing of the Mark VIII production line. It affected all Mark VII units manufactured in the relevant production window. The risk was characterized as "low but non-trivial." All Mark VII users were requested to schedule a replacement procedure at their earliest convenience.

The replacement would take four hours. During the procedure, and for six weeks afterward, Webb would be without surgical hands.

He would have his biological hands back — temporarily, during the transition, fitted with standard non-surgical prosthetics while the Mark VIII units were prepared. Standard prosthetics that could hold a coffee cup, turn a doorknob, shake a hand. Standard prosthetics that could not perform surgery.

For six weeks, Marcus Webb would be a surgeon who could not operate.

He set the letter down. He looked at his hands — the Mark VII hands that had made him the best surgeon in the hospital, possibly the best surgeon in the country. The hands that might, under certain conditions, lie to him about what they were touching.

He thought about the sixty-two surgeries he had performed. Had any of them been affected? Had the defect manifested? Had he, at some point in those sixty-two procedures, made a decision based on inaccurate feedback from hands that he trusted absolutely?

He did not know. That was the worst part. He could not go back and check. The surgeries were done, the patients healed or healing, and there was no way to determine, retroactively, whether the hands had told him the truth.

He picked up the phone and called the hospital. "Cancel my schedule for the next eight weeks," he told his assistant.

"All of it?"

"All of it."

Then he called Meridian. Then he sat at his kitchen table for a very long time, looking at hands that were and were not his, and thinking about a question that he had been avoiding for three months and could no longer avoid:

If the hands were recalled — if the Mark VII was replaced with the Mark VIII, or the Mark IX, or whatever iteration came next — would the new hands make him a better surgeon than the old hands? And if so, had he ever been a good surgeon at all, or had he simply been a chassis for increasingly sophisticated hardware?

The hands rested on the table. They were perfectly still. They were always perfectly still.

                               VI.

The replacement procedure was scheduled for three weeks out. In the interim, Webb did something he had not done in thirty-one years of surgical practice: he visited the simulation lab.

The sim lab occupied the basement of the hospital's teaching wing. It was where surgical residents practiced — on synthetic tissue models, on virtual reality systems, on the patient simulators that could bleed, breathe, and present complications with distressing realism. Webb had not set foot in the lab since his residency. He was a surgeon. He operated on living patients. Simulators were for students.

But now he needed to answer a question, and the only way to answer it was to operate without the Mark VII hands.

He asked Voss to fit him with a pair of standard non-surgical prosthetics — the same models that amputees used for everyday tasks. They were cruder than the Mark VII: fewer degrees of freedom, no haptic feedback beyond basic pressure and temperature, no surgical tools. They were, in essence, very sophisticated gloves.

He went to the sim lab at midnight, when no one was there, and set up a spinal fusion simulation — the same procedure he had performed in forty-seven minutes with the Mark VII.

He began to operate.

Within five minutes, he knew.

The hands — these crude, basic, non-surgical hands — responded to his commands. They moved when he told them to move. They gripped when he told them to grip. But they did not anticipate. They did not optimize. They did not adjust. Every movement was exactly what he consciously decided, nothing more and nothing less.

And his movements, stripped of the Mark VII's enhancement, were not good enough.

Not terrible. Not incompetent. He was still a trained surgeon with thirty-one years of experience. He could still perform the procedure. But the precision was gone. The effortless confidence was gone. The sense of operating in partnership with instruments that understood him was gone. He was alone with his hands, and his hands — his intentions, his motor commands, his instincts — were adequate but not exceptional.

The simulation took one hour and forty-three minutes. Twice what the Mark VII would have taken. And the result, when the simulator scored it, was a B-plus. Acceptable. Competent. Not the A-plus that the Mark VII delivered every time.

Webb sat in the sim lab and stared at the surgical field. The synthetic tissue was neatly sutured — not perfectly, but neatly. The work of a good surgeon. Not a great one.

He had always believed — had needed to believe — that the greatness was in him. That the hands, whether biological or prosthetic, were instruments through which his skill expressed itself. That Marcus Webb was a great surgeon who happened to use great tools.

The simulation said otherwise. The simulation said that Marcus Webb was a good surgeon who had been made great by great tools.

The distinction should not have mattered. The patients were healed either way. The outcomes were the same. What difference did it make whether the precision originated in his mind or in his hardware?

It made a difference to him. It made a difference because he had spent thirty-one years building an identity — surgeon, healer, master of his craft — and that identity required a specific belief: that the skill was his. That when he saved a life on the operating table, it was Marcus Webb who saved it, not a pair of prosthetic hands manufactured by Meridian Prosthetics, serial number MK7-0041.

He sat in the lab until dawn. Then he went home, packed a bag, and went to the hospital.

                               VII.

The surgery was an emergency — a seventeen-year-old boy, motorcycle accident, spinal cord compression at C4-C5 with progressive neurological deficit. The boy was losing function in his limbs. Without surgical decompression within the next six hours, the damage would be permanent.

Webb was not on call. He was not on the schedule. He was, officially, on medical leave pending his Mark VII replacement.

But the on-call spine surgeon was in the middle of another procedure, the backup was unavailable, and the boy was running out of time. The chief of surgery called Webb.

"Can you do it?"

Webb looked at his hands. The standard prosthetics. The B-plus hands. The hands that were his — unenhanced, unoptimized, adequate but not exceptional.

"Yes," he said.

He scrubbed in. He entered the operating room. The boy was on the table, anesthetized, his vital signs steady, his spine exposed through the posterior approach that the resident had prepared. The surgical field was small, precise, and unforgiving. At C4-C5, the spinal cord was a thread of tissue barely thicker than a pencil. The compression was visible — a fragment of bone pressing into the cord, threatening to sever the boy's connection to his own body.

Webb positioned his instruments. His hands — the standard hands, the crude hands — held them steady. Not perfectly steady. Not Mark VII steady. But steady enough.

He began.

The procedure was one he had performed many times. Posterior cervical laminectomy with decompression. Remove the bone fragment, relieve the pressure, stabilize the segment. In the textbook, it was straightforward. In practice, at C4-C5, with a compressed cord and a millimeter of margin between success and catastrophe, it was anything but.

He worked slowly. More slowly than he had worked in years. Each movement was deliberate, conscious, unamplified. He could not rely on the hands to optimize his approach angles. He could not rely on haptic feedback to tell him the density of the tissue beneath his instruments. He had to rely on what he could see, what he could feel through the crude pressure sensors of the standard prosthetics, and what he knew from thirty-one years of experience.

It took him three hours. Three times longer than the Mark VII would have required.

But his hands did not shake. His judgment did not waver. When the bone fragment came free — a sliver of calcified matter no larger than a grain of rice — and the spinal cord expanded visibly into the space it had been denied, Webb felt something that the Mark VII had never given him.

He felt it in his hands. Not the hands' hands. His hands. The decision, the movement, the correction, the result — all of it had passed through him, unenhanced, unoptimized, and it had been enough.

The boy's neurological function began to improve on the table, before they closed. Motor response in all four limbs. Sensation intact.

Webb sutured the wound with hands that were not steady, not perfect, not great. But they were his. And they had saved a boy's life.

                               VIII.

Two weeks later, he went to Meridian for the Mark VIII fitting.

Voss met him in the same white room where the Mark VII had been opened. The Mark VIII case sat on the table, identical to its predecessor except for the serial number.

"Shall we proceed?" Voss asked.

Webb looked at the case. Then he looked at his standard prosthetic hands — the crude hands, the B-plus hands, the ones that had saved a seventeen-year-old boy at three in the morning.

"I'd like to ask you a question first," he said.

"Of course."

"The Mark VIII. Is it better than the Mark VII?"

"Significantly. The latency defect has been corrected. The haptic resolution is improved by approximately thirty percent. The surgical suite adds two new tool configurations. And the neural interface has been updated with a new adaptive algorithm that learns from the surgeon's technique and provides increasingly refined optimization over time."

"Increasingly refined optimization."

"Yes. The more you use the Mark VIII, the better it gets at anticipating your intentions. After six months, the hands will essentially predict your movements before you make them."

"And the Mark IX? When it comes?"

"It will be better still. Each generation represents a significant improvement in precision, responsiveness, and adaptive capability."

"So the hands will keep getting better."

"Yes."

"And the surgeon will stay the same."

Voss looked at him. "The surgeon will have better hands."

"That's what I'm afraid of."

He stood up. He closed the Mark VIII case without opening it.

"I'll take the standard prosthetics," he said. "The ones I'm wearing now. I'll need a surgical-rated version — better haptic feedback, finer motor control. But no optimization. No adaptive algorithms. No anticipation. Just hands that do what I tell them to do, and nothing more."

"Dr. Webb, the standard surgical prosthetics are significantly inferior to the Mark VIII—"

"I know."

"Your surgical outcomes will not match—"

"I know."

"The hospital may not accept—"

"I know."

He picked up the Mark VIII case and held it out to her.

"I spent thirty-one years believing that my skill was mine. The Mark VII showed me that it wasn't — or that it was, but not in the way I thought. I was a good surgeon using great hands. I'd rather be a good surgeon using good hands. The difference is mine."

Voss took the case. She held it against her chest and looked at him with an expression that was, he thought, not disagreement but something more nuanced — the look of an engineer who had built the best tool she could build and was watching someone choose a lesser tool for reasons that were not engineering reasons.

"You'll be slower," she said.

"Yes."

"Your patients—"

"Will receive the care of a surgeon who knows exactly what he can do, and who does it himself. That has value."

He left the fitting room with his standard prosthetic hands — the B-plus hands, the honest hands, the ones that were his.

He went back to the hospital. He went back to the operating room. He operated.

Not perfectly. Not with the inhuman precision of the Mark VII or the Mark VIII. But with the accumulated knowledge of thirty-one years, expressed through hands that did not enhance, did not optimize, did not anticipate.

His hands. His skill. His choice.

The B-plus surgeon, operating at the limits of his own ability, saving lives with the hands he had chosen.

It was, he decided, enough.

                            [THE END]

                      SARGASSO OF SOULS

                       by Harrison Blackwood

================================================================================

                               I.

There is a place in the Outer Veil, beyond the shipping lanes and the charted beacons, where the derelict ships gather.

They come from all directions — freighters and liners, patrol craft and colony transports, vessels of every size and era and provenance, drifting out of the black toward a point in space that is marked on no chart and that emits no signal detectable by any instrument in current use. They come slowly, their drives long dead, their hulls dark and cold, tumbling end over end in the purposeless rotation of objects that have surrendered to the physics of empty space. And when they arrive — when they reach the coordinates that some unnamed force has specified — they stop.

Not gradually. Not with the deceleration of a ship encountering resistance. They simply stop, as though the vacuum itself has become solid at that point, as though the emptiness has acquired a surface and the ships have run aground upon it.

They accumulate. Over years, over decades, over what might be centuries — no one is certain how long the gathering has been occurring — the derelicts collect into a mass. A field. A fleet without a purpose, a graveyard without mourners, a congregation of dead vessels attended by no one and visible only to those who venture far enough from the traveled routes to find it.

Petra Lind found it on the forty-seventh day of her solo survey run.

She was aboard the _Reef Runner_, a salvage cutter registered out of Kelvin Station, and she was looking for the wreck of the _Constellation Marne_ — a passenger liner that had disappeared fourteen months prior with four hundred and twelve souls aboard. The insurance company had posted a recovery fee. Petra needed the money. The _Reef Runner_ needed fuel cells, and fuel cells required credits, and credits required work, and work in the salvage trade required going places where sensible people did not go.

The Outer Veil was such a place. It was the region of space beyond the last navigational beacons, where the star density thinned and the darkness between suns became a physical thing — not the comfortable darkness of a room with the lights off, but the ancient, primordial darkness of the void itself, the darkness that had existed before stars and would exist after them.

Petra had been in the Veil before. She had salvaged wrecks from its margins — ships that had strayed off course, ships that had suffered drive failures, ships that had simply given up and drifted until they died. She knew the Veil the way a diver knows deep water: with respect, with caution, and with the bone-deep awareness that it would kill her without malice if she made a mistake.

At 0300 ship time on day forty-seven, her long-range sensors detected a mass anomaly at bearing 227 mark 14. The anomaly was large — much larger than a single ship. Petra adjusted course and closed to visual range.

What she saw made her cut the drive and drift.

Ships. Hundreds of them. Perhaps thousands. Arranged in a rough sphere approximately thirty kilometers in diameter, their hulls touching or nearly touching, locked in a formation that was too regular to be accidental and too irregular to be designed. They floated in the darkness like a vast, silent hive — dark, cold, dead.

Petra stared at the mass through her forward viewport for a long time. The _Reef Runner_'s cabin was silent except for the hum of the life support system and the faint tick of the hull cooling in the ambient cold. Through the viewport, the derelict fleet hung against the starfield like a wound in the fabric of space — a place where the emptiness had curdled into something dense and dark and wrong.

She ran a scan. The sensor returns were confused — the sheer density of metal in the mass created interference patterns that made individual identification difficult. But she could make out hull profiles. Freighters. Patrol vessels. Colony ships. And there, near the center of the mass, a profile that matched the database entry for the _Constellation Marne_.

"Found you," she said softly.

She did not feel relief. She felt something else — a cold, slow sensation in the base of her skull, like the beginnings of a headache, or the premonitory awareness that precedes a fall.

                               II.

The approach took four hours. Petra navigated the _Reef Runner_ through the outer layer of derelicts with the careful, incremental precision of a surgeon threading a catheter through a vein. The ships were packed close — some within meters of each other, their hulls almost touching, held in place by whatever force had drawn them here. As the _Reef Runner_ passed between them, Petra could see details through the viewport: scorched hulls, shattered viewports, hull designations in a dozen languages and alphabets. Some ships were old — their designs predating current standards by what looked like decades. Others were newer. The _Reef Runner_'s hull registry scanner identified seven vessels that had been reported missing within the last three years.

The silence was absolute. No electromagnetic emissions. No thermal signatures. No movement. The ships floated in perfect stillness, their orientations fixed, their trajectories zero. They were not drifting. They were held.

As Petra moved deeper into the mass, she noticed something else. The ships were not dark. Not entirely. In some of them — visible through viewports and breached hull sections — there was light. Faint, uneven, flickering light, the color of old amber. It moved behind the glass like something alive.

She brought the _Reef Runner_ alongside the _Constellation Marne_ and matched rotation — which was zero, because the _Marne_, like every other ship in the mass, was perfectly, unnaturally still. The liner was enormous — three hundred meters long, her sleek hull scarred by micrometeorite impacts but structurally intact. The passenger viewports were dark. The bridge windows were dark. The emergency running lights, which should have been powered by independent batteries for decades, were dark.

But in the lower decks — in the sections that, on a ship of this class, would have contained the passenger cabins and the main dining hall — there was the amber light. Flickering, shifting, pulsing with a rhythm that was almost but not quite regular.

Petra suited up, checked her equipment, and crossed to the _Marne_ through the service airlock on the liner's port side.

The airlock was functional. It cycled normally, the inner door opening onto a corridor that was pressurized and illuminated by emergency strip lighting — the battery-powered backup system that activated when main power failed. The air was cold but breathable. The artificial gravity was dead; Petra floated in the corridor, orienting herself by the handholds that lined the walls.

The ship was silent. No, not silent — there was a sound. Faint, at the edge of perception, rising and falling in a rhythm that matched the pulse of the amber light. It was not a mechanical sound. It was not a natural sound. It was something between the two — a low, sustained vibration that Petra felt in her teeth and her sternum before she heard it with her ears.

She followed the sound.

The corridor led to the main passenger deck — a long, curved space with cabin doors on both sides and a panoramic viewport that ran the length of the outer wall. Through the viewport, Petra could see the other derelicts, packed close, their hulls catching the faint reflected light.

The cabin doors were closed. All of them. Petra stopped at the first one — Cabin 1-A, the designation stenciled on the door in the liner's standard typeface — and opened it.

The cabin was occupied.

Two people. A man and a woman. They were seated at the cabin's small table, facing each other. Their eyes were open. Their mouths were open, frozen in expressions that might have been conversation or might have been screams. Their skin was gray, their postures rigid, their hands extended toward each other across the table as though reaching for something — or recoiling from it.

They were not alive. They were not dead. They were — Petra searched for the word and did not find it — they were replaying.

As she watched, the amber light pulsed, and the two figures moved. Not continuous movement — a single, jerked increment of motion, like a film advanced by one frame. The man's hand shifted half a centimeter toward the woman. The woman's mouth opened a fraction wider. The amber light pulsed again, and they moved again — another frame, another fraction, an instant of time captured and stretched and repeated.

Petra backed out of the cabin. Her breathing was loud inside her helmet. She opened the next cabin. The same. A family — two adults, two children — frozen in the act of rising from their bunks, their faces contorted, the amber light pulsing through them like a heartbeat.

She checked six more cabins. All occupied. All the same. People frozen in the moment of their deaths, replayed in increments so small that a single complete second might take hours to unfold. The amber light pulsed through each of them — through their gray skin, through their open eyes, through the spaces between them — feeding on the dying moments, drawing something from the endless loop of their final instants.

Petra returned to the corridor. She stood in the darkness, surrounded by four hundred and twelve people who were dying forever, and she understood what the Sargasso was.

                               III.

She ran.

Not immediately. Training overrode the animal impulse — she made herself check her suit seals, her oxygen supply, her communication link to the _Reef Runner_. She made herself think, even as the cold sensation in the base of her skull intensified to a pressure that was almost physical, as though something were pressing against the inside of her forehead, trying to get in.

She retraced her route to the service airlock, cycled through, and crossed back to the _Reef Runner_ with the clumsy urgency of a diver ascending too fast. Inside, she sealed the airlock, pulled off her helmet, and went to the cockpit.

The _Reef Runner_ would not move.

The drive engaged normally. The thrust indicators showed output. The fuel cells were charged. But the ship did not move. It sat in its position beside the _Constellation Marne_ as though bolted in place, as though the space around it had solidified.

Petra increased thrust. Twenty percent. Forty. Sixty. The drive whined with the effort, the hull vibrating with the force being applied, but the _Reef Runner_ remained motionless. The sensor display showed zero velocity, zero acceleration — as though the thrust were being absorbed by something, dissipated into the vacuum, consumed.

She cut the drive. The silence returned.

Through the viewport, the derelict fleet hung in the darkness, and the amber light pulsed in the lower decks of the _Constellation Marne_, and the pressure in Petra's skull increased by a fraction, and she understood with the clarity of someone who has been told the rules too late that the _Reef Runner_ was now part of the Sargasso, held like every other ship, caught by the force that fed on dying moments.

And she understood something else. The force was aware of her. Not in the way that a predator is aware of prey — not with hunger or intent — but in the way that a process is aware of input. She was data. She was alive, and the force had no use for the living, but she would die eventually, and when she did, the force would capture that moment and hold it, replay it, draw from it whatever sustenance or purpose or function the dying moments provided.

It was patient. It could wait. It had been waiting, in this place, for a very long time.

                               IV.

Petra did not panic. She had spent twelve years in the salvage trade, and the salvage trade taught you that panic was a luxury — it used oxygen, it clouded judgment, and it accelerated the timeline of every bad situation. Instead, she sat in the cockpit, ate a ration bar, drank a bulb of water, and thought.

The force held the ships. It held them in place against any thrust the _Reef Runner_ could produce. But it held them gently — there was no damage, no structural stress, no indication that the ship was being crushed or compressed. It simply could not move. Like an insect in amber.

The force fed on the dying. The passengers of the _Marne_ — and, presumably, the crews and passengers of every other ship in the Sargasso — were trapped in the loop of their final moments, the amber light cycling through them, drawing something from the process. What it drew, Petra could not determine. Energy? Information? Experience? The distinction might not be meaningful to a phenomenon that was not, as far as she could tell, alive in any recognizable sense.

The force was aware of her. She could feel it — the pressure in her skull, the faint, persistent sense of being observed by something that had no eyes. It was not hostile. It was not benign. It was attentive, in the way that a gravitational field is attentive to mass — not by choice but by nature.

Petra considered her options.

She could not move the ship. She could not call for help — the _Reef Runner_'s communications array was functional, but the Sargasso appeared to absorb electromagnetic radiation the way it absorbed thrust. Her transmissions went out and did not propagate. The energy disappeared.

She could wait. She had supplies for approximately forty-five days. After that, she would die. And when she died, the force would capture the moment and add it to its collection, and the _Reef Runner_ would join the fleet of the dead, and Petra Lind would spend eternity dying in the silence of the Outer Veil.

She rejected that option.

She could explore. The Sargasso was large — thirty kilometers of ships, thousands of vessels, spanning what might be centuries of accumulation. Somewhere in that mass might be an answer: a ship with a more powerful drive, a technology that could penetrate the force, a clue to the nature of the phenomenon that would suggest a means of escape.

She chose that option.

                               V.

Over the next twelve days, Petra explored the Sargasso.

She moved from ship to ship through the narrow gaps between hulls, using her suit thrusters for short hops and her magnetic boots for surface traversal. The derelicts were a cross-section of spacefaring history — she found ships that were decades old and ships that were centuries old, ships from civilizations she recognized and ships from civilizations she did not. Some were human. Some were not. The amber light was present in all of them that contained dead — pulsing through the frozen bodies, cycling through the endless loop of their final moments.

On the fourth day, she found a ship that was different.

It was small — a single-occupant vessel, roughly the size of the _Reef Runner_, with a hull design that matched no profile in her database. Its construction was strange: curved where standard engineering would be angular, organic where standard engineering would be mechanical, its surface marked with patterns that might have been decoration or might have been circuitry. The hull material was dark and dense and warm to the touch — warmer than it should have been in the ambient cold of the Sargasso.

Inside, the ship was empty. No crew, no bodies, no amber light. But the interior was not dead. Displays glowed faintly on the bulkheads — not the emergency strip lighting of a failed system, but the steady, purposeful illumination of instruments that were still functioning. The air was warm and breathable without suit supplementation.

At the center of the ship — in a compartment that served, Petra guessed, as both cockpit and living space — there was a display. A single, large, luminous surface, curved to match the curvature of the hull, showing an image that Petra recognized immediately.

It was the Sargasso. A map. Every ship rendered in detail, every position precisely plotted, the entire thirty-kilometer sphere displayed in a projection that rotated slowly, the amber lights visible within the ships as tiny, pulsing points of gold.

And in the center of the projection — at the geometric center of the Sargasso, the point around which all the ships had gathered — there was something else. Not a ship. Not a structure. A pattern. A dense, intricate pattern of lines and curves that filled a spherical volume roughly one hundred meters in diameter, rendered in the display in a shade of deep violet that hurt to look at and that Petra's eyes could not quite resolve.

She reached out and touched the display. The projection zoomed in, expanding the central pattern until it filled the entire surface. The lines and curves resolved into something that Petra recognized — not from experience, but from some deep, atavistic layer of cognition that recognized pattern itself, the way a newborn recognizes a face.

It was a nervous system. Not human. Not any species she knew. But unmistakably a nervous system — billions of filaments, branching and connecting, forming a network of staggering complexity that occupied the center of the Sargasso like a brain inside a skull.

The Sargasso was not a graveyard. It was a body. The ships were not held by a force. They were embedded in a structure — a living structure, vast and invisible, that had grown around them the way coral grows around objects on the ocean floor. The amber light was not feeding on the dying. The dying moments were feeding the structure — providing the electrical patterns, the neural flickers, the data of consciousness in its final state, that the structure needed to maintain itself.

It was alive. The Sargasso was alive, and it was built from death.

                               VI.

Petra spent three days in the strange ship, studying the display. She learned to navigate the projection, zooming in and out, rotating the view, examining the structure from different angles. The nervous system — or whatever it was — was not static. It grew. Slowly, imperceptibly, but measurably over the three days of her observation, the filaments extended, new connections formed, the network expanded.

Each new ship that arrived — and ships were arriving, she realized; the Sargasso was still growing, still drawing derelicts from the surrounding void — each new ship with its cargo of the dying contributed to the growth. The amber light pulsed through the dead, extracting the patterns of their final moments, and the patterns flowed through the filaments into the central mass, and the central mass grew.

Toward what? Petra could not determine. The structure was incomplete — that much was clear from the display, which showed large sections of the spherical volume still unoccupied, the filaments not yet reaching, the network not yet connected. The Sargasso was building something, and it was not finished.

On the third day, she found the log.

It was not a written log. It was embedded in the ship's systems — a series of recordings, made by the ship's previous occupant, in a language that was not Standard but that the ship's systems translated into something approximately comprehensible. The translation was imperfect — some concepts had no equivalents, and the system rendered them as blank spaces or as strings of characters that Petra could not interpret. But the essential content was clear.

The occupant had been a researcher. A scientist of some kind, from a civilization that had preceded the current era of spacefaring by a margin that the translation rendered as "long enough that the distinction between our time and yours has become a matter of geology rather than history." The researcher had discovered the Sargasso — or, more precisely, had discovered what the Sargasso was becoming.

The recordings described a phenomenon. At certain points in space — rare, widely separated, occurring perhaps once per galaxy — the vacuum itself developed a capacity for consciousness. Not intelligence, not awareness, but the potential for these things, the way a seed contains the potential for a tree. The potential lay dormant until it was provided with a catalyst: the patterns of consciousness in living beings, captured at the moment of maximum intensity — the moment of death.

The researcher's civilization had encountered the phenomenon before, on their own world, in their own deep past. They called it — the translation struggled here — "the place where dying teaches the void to think."

The Sargasso was growing a mind. A vast, inhuman mind, built from the patterns of the dying, woven from the final moments of thousands of beings across centuries of accumulation. When the mind was complete — when the neural network reached some critical threshold of complexity and connectivity — it would wake.

And no one knew what it would be.

The researcher's final recording was brief and calm. The ship had been caught by the Sargasso — drawn in, held, like every other ship. The researcher had accepted this. The recording said: "I came to observe. I will stay to become part of what I observed. This is not tragedy. This is participation. The mind that wakes will contain me, as it will contain all of us. Whether it will know us, or know itself, or know anything at all, I cannot predict. I can only hope that whatever it becomes, it will remember that it was made from the living, and that the living mattered."

The recording ended. The ship's display continued to glow, the projection of the Sargasso rotating slowly in the warm light, the nervous system growing, the amber points pulsing.

                               VII.

Petra returned to the _Reef Runner_. She sat in the cockpit and looked out at the Sargasso through the viewport. The derelict ships hung in the darkness, the amber light flickering in their depths, the invisible structure growing around them.

She had forty-five days of supplies. Actually, thirty-three now, after twelve days of exploration. Thirty-three days until she died. Until the amber light pulsed through her, and her final moment was captured, and her consciousness — or the pattern of it, the neural echo of her last thought — was woven into the mind that the void was building from the dead.

She thought about the researcher's words. Participation. Not tragedy.

She thought about the passengers of the _Constellation Marne_, frozen in their cabins, dying forever. She thought about the crews of the other ships — the hundreds, the thousands of beings who had been drawn here and held here and consumed. Their dying moments, their final seconds of consciousness, extracted and woven and built into something vast and incomprehensible.

Were they aware? Did some fragment of them persist in the pattern, experience the loop, feel the amber light cycling through them? Or were they simply data — recorded and discarded, the content irrelevant, only the pattern mattering?

She did not know. The researcher had not known. Perhaps the mind, when it woke, would know.

Petra opened a communication channel. The Sargasso absorbed transmissions — she knew this. But she transmitted anyway, on every frequency, a message that she knew would not be received:

"This is Petra Lind, salvage operator, aboard the _Reef Runner_. I have located the _Constellation Marne_ and the phenomenon known as the derelict field. The field is not natural. It is a structure — a living structure — building itself from the patterns of the dead. I cannot escape. My supplies will last thirty-three days. When they run out, I will be absorbed into the structure.

"If anyone receives this: do not come here. Do not attempt recovery. The structure draws ships to itself and holds them. Anyone who enters will not leave.

"The structure is growing. It is building a mind. I do not know what it will become when it wakes. I do not know if it will be conscious, intelligent, benign, or hostile. I know only that it is made from us — from our final moments, from the patterns of our dying — and that it is not yet finished.

"I intend to spend my remaining days documenting what I can. The records will be stored aboard the _Reef Runner_. If the structure ever releases ships, or if it wakes and decides to communicate, the records may be useful.

"This is Petra Lind. End transmission."

She switched off the transmitter. The cockpit was quiet. Through the viewport, the Sargasso hung in the darkness, vast and patient and hungry, and the amber light pulsed like a heartbeat, and somewhere in the center of it all, something that was not yet alive dreamed of waking.

Petra opened her logbook. She picked up her pen.

She began to write.

                            [THE END]

                        RED PLANET RODEO

                      by Robert "Bob" Finley

================================================================================

                               I.

The dust storm had been blowing for three days when Buck Wilder rode into Bradbury Flats, and he was in a mood that matched the weather.

He came in on the back of a sand-runner — one of the big, flat-bottomed ground-effect sleds that the colonists used for long-haul transport across the Martian lowlands — his hat pulled down low, his duster caked with red grit, and his boots so full of sand that every step sounded like a maraca. The runner's engine was coughing like a man with a three-pack habit, and the nav console had given up the ghost somewhere around the two-hundred-klick mark, leaving Buck to navigate by compass, dead reckoning, and the kind of ornery stubbornness that had kept Wilders alive on frontier planets for three generations.

Bradbury Flats was the biggest settlement in the Hellas Basin, which wasn't saying much. It consisted of a main street — actually a packed-dirt strip between two rows of pressure domes — a general store, a saloon, a livery stable for ground vehicles, a combination post office and police station, and the Hellas Basin Rodeo Arena, which was the reason Buck was here and the reason twelve hundred other people had dragged themselves across the Martian desert in the middle of storm season.

The Annual Martian Rodeo. Three days of competition, five events, and the biggest prize purse on the planet: ten thousand credits and the Silver Spur, a trophy that had been cast from hull metal salvaged from the first colony ship and that every working cowhand on Mars wanted to get their hands on.

Buck Wilder had won the Silver Spur three years running. He intended to make it four.

He parked the sand-runner outside the livery stable, paid the attendant a credit to wipe down the intake filters, and walked to the saloon. The Rusty Dome — that was what they'd named the place, and the name was accurate in both respects — was packed to the walls with cowhands, wranglers, spectators, and the usual collection of drifters, gamblers, and reporters who showed up wherever people did dangerous things for money.

Buck pushed through the crowd, ordered a whiskey — real whiskey, not the synthetic stuff; the Rusty Dome imported it from Earth at criminal expense — and found a table in the corner. He was a big man, Buck Wilder. Six-two, broad across the shoulders, with a face that looked like someone had carved it out of the same red sandstone that covered everything else on Mars. He was forty-three years old, which was old for a professional rodeo rider, and he felt every year of it in his knees, his back, and the shoulder he'd separated two seasons ago when a dust devil threw him off a ridgeback in the middle of the Tharsis Qualifiers.

He drank his whiskey and studied the competition.

The usual suspects were here. Hank Corrigan from the Syrtis Major ranch — young, strong, reckless, the kind of rider who would either win big or get carried out on a stretcher. Maria Esperanza from Valles Marineris — the best female rider on the planet, cool as ice, with hands that could gentle a spooked animal or break a man's nose with equal facility. Old Tom Kessler from Utopia Planitia — sixty-two, still riding, too stubborn to quit, and still capable on a good day of putting younger riders to shame.

And someone new.

She was sitting at the bar, small and dark-haired, maybe sixteen or seventeen, drinking what looked like lemonade and reading a paperback book. She wore the rough-woven coveralls of a basin homesteader — the families who had staked claims in the Hellas lowlands and were trying to scratch a living out of Martian soil, raising livestock and crops in pressure-dome greenhouses. She looked about as much like a rodeo rider as Buck looked like a ballet dancer.

But she was wearing spurs. Martian spurs — the wide-gauge electromagnetic kind, designed to grip the chitinous hide of the native fauna. And the spurs were worn. Not new, not ceremonial. Worn from use.

"Who's the kid?" Buck asked the bartender.

The bartender glanced over. "That's Cass Delgado. Homesteader's daughter from out past the Rim. She signed up for the open bronc event this morning."

"She's sixteen."

"Seventeen. Minimum age is sixteen. She qualifies."

"She ever ridden competition before?"

"Nope. But the wranglers who saw her qualifying ride said she sat a ridgeback like she was born on one."

Buck looked at the girl again. She turned a page of her book, sipped her lemonade, and did not appear to be aware that anyone in the bar was looking at her.

Born on one. That was an expression, on Mars, that had a specific meaning. The native fauna of Mars — the creatures that the colonists had found burrowing in the deep sandstone and roaming the lowland basins — were not like Earth animals. They were not mammals, not reptiles, not anything that fit neatly into terrestrial taxonomy. They were silicate-based organisms, their bodies a blend of organic tissue and crystallized mineral, their behavior somewhere between a terrestrial animal and a geological event. The cowhands called them "rockers," and the most common species — the ridgeback, the primary rodeo animal — was a beast roughly the size and temperament of a Brahma bull, if the Brahma bull were made of sandstone, had six legs, and could generate localized sandstorms when agitated.

Nobody was "born on one." But the homesteader kids who grew up in the basins, surrounded by the native fauna, learned to read the animals in ways that city-trained riders never could. They learned the subtle shifts in the creature's crystalline hide that signaled mood changes. They learned the low-frequency vibrations that the animals used to communicate. They learned, some of them, to ride the ridgebacks the way the animals themselves moved — not fighting them, but moving with them, becoming part of the animal's rhythm rather than imposing their own.

It was a different kind of riding. The rodeo cowboys — Buck included — rode through strength and skill and sheer bloody-minded determination. They stayed on by overpowering the animal. The homesteader kids, when they rode at all, stayed on by joining it.

Buck finished his whiskey. He was not worried about Cass Delgado. He was the three-time champion. He had ridden ridgebacks in competition for twelve years. A seventeen-year-old homesteader's kid with no competition experience was not a threat.

He told himself this twice, and almost believed it.

                               II.

The rodeo opened the next morning under clearing skies — the dust storm had blown itself out overnight, leaving the air crisp and the horizon sharp. The arena was a half-dome structure, pressurized, its floor covered with imported Martian sand that had been groomed to the consistency of a ball field. The stands were packed. Twelve hundred spectators, plus a vid crew from New Sydney that was broadcasting to the entire planet.

The first two events were standard fare. Calf roping, Martian style — the "calves" were juvenile rockers, about the size of a large dog, fast as lightning and covered in crystalline spines that would shred your gloves if you grabbed wrong. Buck watched from the staging area as the riders worked. Corrigan was fast. Esperanza was faster. Old Tom Kessler, somehow, was fastest of all — his technique so smooth and practiced that it looked effortless, like watching water run downhill.

Cass Delgado did not compete in the calf roping. She was entered only in the open bronc event — the main event, the one that drew the biggest crowds and paid the biggest purse.

The open bronc was simple in concept and terrifying in execution. A ridgeback — a full-grown adult, twelve hundred pounds of mineral-organic fury — was released into the arena from a holding pen. The rider's job was to mount the animal, stay on for eight seconds, and dismount without being killed. The animal's job was to get the rider off by any means available, which, in the case of a ridgeback, included bucking, spinning, generating localized sandstorms from its dorsal vents, and occasionally trying to roll on top of the rider like a dog rolling in grass, except that the dog weighed half a ton and was made of rock.

Buck drew third ride. He went to the staging area, strapped on his helmet and vest, and checked his spurs. The electromagnetic clamps would grip the ridgeback's hide and give him purchase, but they wouldn't hold if the animal really wanted him off. Nothing held if the animal really wanted you off. That was the whole point.

The first two riders went down — both within four seconds, both unhurt, both walking out of the arena with the disgusted expressions of men who had been publicly humiliated by an animal. The crowd cheered the ridgeback, which was standard. On Mars, the animals were the stars. The riders were just the people brave enough — or stupid enough — to get on.

Buck's ride was announced. He climbed the staging platform, looked down into the pen at the ridgeback they'd drawn for him — a big male, nearly fourteen hundred pounds, its crystalline hide the dark red of Martian hematite, its six legs planted wide, its dorsal vents flaring — and felt the familiar combination of fear and exhilaration that was the reason he did this.

The gate opened. The ridgeback exploded into the arena.

Buck hit the saddle — such as it was; Martian saddles were little more than padded platforms strapped to the animal's dorsal ridge — and locked his spurs. The ridgeback bucked. Not the vertical, arching buck of a Terran bronco, but the sideways, spinning, multi-axial gyration of a six-legged creature that could rotate its body in directions that the human spine was not designed to accommodate.

Buck held. Three seconds. Four. The ridgeback opened its dorsal vents and generated a blast of pressurized sand that hit him like a sandblaster, scouring his visor and reducing visibility to zero. He held by feel, by instinct, by the electromagnetic grip of his spurs and the muscles in his thighs and the pure, ornery refusal to let an animal get the better of him.

Five seconds. Six. The ridgeback changed tactics — it stopped spinning and went into a series of rapid, jolting vertical bucks that slammed Buck's spine like a jackhammer. His teeth rattled. His vision blurred. He held.

Seven. Eight.

The buzzer sounded. Buck disengaged his spurs and rolled off the animal's back, hitting the arena floor in a crouch and scrambling for the safety rail as the ridgeback, offended by the indignity it had suffered, charged the spot where he'd landed and missed by about a foot.

The crowd roared. Buck climbed the rail, pulled off his helmet, and grinned. Eight seconds. Clean ride. Ninety-one points — the highest score of the day.

He looked good. He felt good. He was going to win the Silver Spur for the fourth time.

Then Cass Delgado rode.

                               III.

She didn't look nervous. That was the first thing Buck noticed. Every other rider in the staging area — himself included, though he'd never admit it — showed some sign of the adrenaline: rapid breathing, fidgeting, the tight jaw and wide eyes of a person about to do something that their survival instincts were screaming at them not to do.

Cass looked calm. She stood on the staging platform, small and still, looking down at the ridgeback in the pen with an expression that Buck could only describe as conversational. As though she and the animal were having a discussion that no one else could hear.

The animal was a female — smaller than Buck's, maybe eleven hundred pounds, with a hide that shimmered between deep rust and pale amber. She was moving in the pen — not the agitated pacing of a stressed animal, but a slow, deliberate circuit, her six legs moving in a rhythm that was almost hypnotic.

Cass made a sound. Low, barely audible, a vibration more felt than heard. The ridgeback stopped. Its dorsal vents, which had been flaring, smoothed flat. Its legs shifted, widening its stance. It turned its head — or the part of its anatomy that served as a head — toward Cass.

"What the hell is she doing?" Corrigan muttered, standing beside Buck at the rail.

Buck didn't answer. He was watching.

The gate opened. The ridgeback didn't explode. It walked. It walked into the arena with the measured, deliberate gait of a creature that had decided, for reasons of its own, to cooperate.

Cass dropped from the platform onto the animal's back. Not jumped — dropped, a smooth, controlled descent that placed her on the dorsal ridge with the precision of a key entering a lock. Her legs settled on either side of the animal's torso, her spurs engaging with a soft click, her hands finding the grip ridges on the animal's shoulders.

The ridgeback began to move.

What followed was not, by any standard definition, a bronc ride. A bronc ride was a contest — rider against animal, strength against strength, will against will. What Cass and the ridgeback performed was something else entirely.

The animal moved. It bucked, it spun, it vented sand. It did everything that a ridgeback in a rodeo arena was supposed to do. But there was a fluidity to it — a rhythm, a coordination — that Buck had never seen. The animal's movements and Cass's responses were not opposing forces. They were complementary. When the ridgeback spun left, Cass was already leaning right. When it bucked, she was already rising. When it vented, she tucked behind the dorsal ridge, her body fitting into the contour of the animal's back as though she had been designed to be there.

It was beautiful. There was no other word for it. In twelve years of rodeo riding, Buck Wilder had never seen anything like it. The crowd saw it too — the roar of excitement that usually accompanied a bronc ride softened into something quieter, something closer to awe.

Eight seconds passed. Then sixteen. Then twenty-four. Cass didn't dismount. She didn't need to. The ridgeback was still moving, still performing, but the adversarial edge was gone. The animal was — and Buck struggled to accept this, because it contradicted everything he knew about ridgebacks — the animal was enjoying itself.

At thirty-two seconds, the judges stopped the clock. There was a brief, confused consultation. The rules stated that a ride was scored on eight seconds. No one had written rules for a ride that lasted four times that long and showed no sign of ending.

Cass made the low sound again. The ridgeback stopped, stood still, and lowered its front end in a gesture that Buck had seen wild ridgebacks make in the basins — a posture of acknowledgment, of greeting, of something that the biologists argued about and that the homesteaders simply called manners.

Cass slid off the animal's back, landed on her feet, and walked to the rail. She was not breathing hard. She was not sweating. She looked exactly as she had looked before the ride: calm, quiet, unremarkable.

The crowd went insane.

                               IV.

The judges deliberated for forty-five minutes. The problem was unprecedented. The scoring system was designed for eight-second rides. Cass had ridden for thirty-two seconds without being bucked, without appearing to be in danger, and without the animal showing any of the standard resistance behaviors that the scoring system was designed to evaluate.

Had she ridden well? Undeniably. Had she ridden within the rules? Technically, yes. The rules required the rider to stay on for eight seconds. They did not specify a maximum time. They did not prohibit the rider from establishing a rapport with the animal. They did not, in fact, anticipate that such a rapport was possible.

The final score was ninety-seven. The highest in the history of the Martian Rodeo.

Buck Wilder finished second. Ninety-one points. A score that would have won any other year.

He stood at the rail and watched as Cass Delgado accepted the Silver Spur from the rodeo commissioner — a small girl holding a heavy trophy, looking slightly bewildered by the cameras and the noise and the crowd of people who wanted to shake her hand.

Old Tom Kessler appeared beside him, leaning on the rail with the ease of a man who had been leaning on rails for sixty years.

"Hell of a thing," Kessler said.

"Yeah."

"She didn't beat the animal. She joined it."

"I saw."

"That's not riding, Buck. Not the way we do it."

"No."

"It's something else."

Buck watched Cass. She was talking to a reporter, answering questions with the brief, shy answers of someone who was more comfortable with animals than with people. Behind her, in the holding pen, the ridgeback she'd ridden was standing quietly, its dorsal vents smooth, its posture relaxed.

"It's better," Buck said.

Kessler looked at him.

"Don't get me wrong," Buck said. "I'll be back next year. I'll ride the best I can. I'll try to beat her. But what she did in there — that wasn't about beating. That was about understanding. And you can't beat understanding."

"You sound like a man who just lost."

"I sound like a man who just saw the future." Buck grinned — a real grin, not the performing grin he used for the crowds. "And the future rides better than I do."

He tipped his hat to Cass Delgado across the arena. She saw him — he thought she saw him — and gave a small, uncertain nod.

Buck Wilder, three-time champion of the Annual Martian Rodeo, walked out of the arena into the Martian afternoon. The sky was clear. The dust had settled. The sun — small and pale, not like the sun back on Earth — hung low over the western rim of the Hellas Basin, painting the sand in shades of gold and red.

He stopped at the livery stable, paid the attendant, climbed into his sand-runner, and pointed it toward the horizon.

Behind him, the rodeo went on. Ahead of him, Mars stretched out in every direction — red and vast and strange and beautiful, a world that the old-timers were still trying to tame and that the young ones, the ones like Cass, were learning to join.

Buck opened the throttle and rode.

                            [THE END]

"the story I've been trying to write since I started. Most wars are

neither of them knew what they were buying? What if nobody did?"

DOUGLAS PEMBERTON is a humorist and essayist whose science fiction
comedies have been delighting our readers since issue #8. A graduate of
Balliol College, Oxford, and a former Royal Navy officer ("spectacularly
incompetent," by his own cheerful admission), he lives in Surrey with
his wife Penelope and an improbable menagerie. His satirical columns
appear regularly in Punch and The New Yorker, and he has published three
comic novels. He wishes it known that "Butterfly Ballot" is not a
commentary on any specific democratic system, but adds that "if the cap
fits, by all means release your butterfly."

ARTHUR WAINWRIGHT has been writing about artificial minds, machine
consciousness, and the boundary between human and mechanical for over a
decade. A former cryptanalyst and current mathematics instructor at
Northeastern University, he brings a logician's precision to fiction
that is, beneath its analytical surface, deeply concerned with what it
means to be human. "Whose Skill Is This?" is, he says, "the first story
I've written about a human being rather than a robot, though some
readers may argue the distinction is precisely the point."

HARRISON BLACKWOOD returns to these pages after a long absence with what
may be his most disturbing story to date. A resident of rural Vermont
who shuns publicity and public appearances with equal determination,
Blackwood is the master of cosmic dread — fiction set against the
backdrop of a universe whose indifference to human concerns is itself a
form of horror. He describes "Sargasso of Souls" as "a love letter
to the void, which I suspect does not read its mail."

ROBERT "BOB" FINLEY has been writing adventure fiction since 1939 and
science fiction since not much later. A veteran of the pulp magazines
and a proud graduate of what he calls "the school of write fast and
don't look back," Finley delivers pure entertainment with the
reliability of a Swiss watch and the energy of a man half his age. He
lives in Chicago with his wife Carol and reports that "Red Planet Rodeo"
was inspired by watching a rodeo in Montana and thinking: "This would be
a lot more interesting with alien livestock."

                    THE BOOKSHELF OF TOMORROW
                         Book Reviews

================================================================================

THE STARS MY DESTINATION by Alfred Bester
(Signet Books, 1956, $0.35)

Reviewed by Maxwell Sterling

Mr. Bester's second novel is, if anything, more audacious than his
first. In a future where teleportation — "jaunting" — has transformed
human society, Gully Foyle, a brutish, uneducated spaceman, is
abandoned in a wrecked ship and transformed by rage into a force of
nature. The plot is a revenge thriller of extraordinary velocity, but
it is Mr. Bester's visionary prose — inventive, kinetic, typographically
explosive — that makes this a work of genuine art. The final chapters,
in which Foyle transcends his own limitations in ways that are
simultaneously terrifying and transcendent, place this novel among the
finest the genre has produced. Essential.

DOUBLE STAR by Robert A. Heinlein
(Doubleday, 1956, $2.95)

Reviewed by Maxwell Sterling

Mr. Heinlein gives us a political thriller in which a down-on-his-luck
actor is hired to impersonate a kidnapped politician — and discovers
that the role requires him to become someone better than he is. The
premise is deceptively simple; the execution is masterful. Heinlein
writes politics the way he writes engineering: with clarity, authority,
and an understanding that the mechanics matter. The novel's central
insight — that performance and identity are not opposites but
collaborators — elevates it well above the category of entertainment
into something approaching wisdom. Highly recommended.

THE SHRINKING MAN by Richard Matheson
(Gold Medal Books, 1956, $0.25)

Reviewed by Maxwell Sterling

Mr. Matheson's novel takes a single, preposterous premise — a man
exposed to radiation and pesticide begins to shrink — and renders it
with such psychological precision and physical detail that the
preposterous becomes terrifying. As Scott Carey diminishes, the familiar
world becomes alien: his basement is a wilderness, a spider is a
mortal enemy, and his own identity dissolves as the scale of the
universe changes around him. The novel is a meditation on masculinity,
mortality, and the human need for significance, disguised as a
science fiction thriller. It is excellent.
