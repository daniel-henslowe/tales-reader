TRANSMISSIONS FROM THE EDITOR'S DESK
by Maxwell Sterling

Dear Reader,

Three years and eight months. Forty-four issues. Somewhere north of two
hundred and fifty stories published, each one a small expedition into the
unknown. I mention these numbers not to congratulate ourselves — though a
modest self-congratulation seems forgivable at midsummer — but because
this issue represents something that has been building for a long time,
and I want you to recognize it when you see it.

What has been building is maturity.

Not the dull, cautious, play-it-safe variety. I mean the kind of maturity
that comes when writers who have been working at their craft for years
arrive, almost simultaneously, at the peak of their powers. This issue
of Tales from the Future and Beyond contains, I believe, some of the
finest fiction we have ever published, and I do not say that lightly.

Arthur Wainwright leads us off with "Trial of Unit Seven," and I will
tell you plainly: I consider it the best robot story he has written for
this magazine. Those of you who have followed Wainwright's work from
"Logic Gate" through "Nanny Mark IV" and beyond know that this is no
small claim. The story is a courtroom drama — yes, a courtroom drama
about a machine — and it will, I predict, generate more argument in these
pages than anything we have published since Hoyt's "The Committee." Good.
Argue. That is what fiction is for.

Franklin Hoyt returns with "Embargo," a story about interstellar trade
policy that is somehow as tense as a thriller and as angry as a sermon.
Cordell Beaumont gives us what I suspect may be his farewell to planetary
romance in the magnificent "Cartographer's Last Map" — a journey into
the interior of a living world that is, by turns, terrifying and
heartbreakingly beautiful. Theodore Cain delivers "Third Shift," a
lean, atmospheric piece about the graveyard shift on a mining platform
that reminded me, in its quiet dread, of the best work of our own
Lucian Marsh — who, as it happens, is also present in this issue with
"Voices in the Trees," a story that will make you very reluctant to
walk in the woods at night.

Phyllis Crenshaw's "Stolen Happiest Day" is a time-travel story of a
kind she has not attempted before — darker, more personal, and more
devastating than her usual elegant puzzles. And Bob Finley rounds us
out with "Patchwork Planet," a romp through the most improbable world
in the galaxy, told with his customary verve.

Seven stories. Seven writers working at or near the top of their game.
This is what forty-four issues of trust between editor, writer, and
reader can produce.

Enjoy every page. Then write and tell me what you think.

Yours among the stars,

Maxwell Sterling
Editor, Tales from the Future and Beyond
New York, June 1956

                         TRIAL OF UNIT SEVEN

                        by Arthur Wainwright

================================================================================

                               I.

The courtroom was not designed for the trial of a machine.

It was Courtroom Four of the Federal District Court in Hartford,
Connecticut — a room built in 1923 for the adjudication of human disputes,
with oak paneling, brass fixtures, and a gallery that seated one hundred
and forty persons. The bench was elevated. The witness stand was sized
for a human body. The defendant's chair, positioned at the left side of
the counsel table, was a standard oak armchair with a leather cushion.

Unit Seven did not fit in the chair. This was the first of many problems.

Unit Seven was a Consolidated Dynamics Model 7-G domestic service
android, serial number CD-7G-0041, standing six feet two inches tall and
weighing four hundred and eleven pounds. It had been designed to perform
household tasks, light maintenance, and — this was the detail that had
brought everyone to Hartford — to serve as a protective companion for
its owner's family. Its chassis was brushed aluminum over a titanium
frame. Its face, in the fashion of the newer Consolidated models, was
molded into an approximation of human features: a smooth brow, shallow
eye sockets housing optical sensors, a slight ridge suggesting a nose,
and a speaker grille where a mouth would be that could shape itself into
expressions meant to be reassuring. The effect was not quite human and
not quite mechanical, which placed it squarely in the territory that made
most people uncomfortable.

The chair had been removed. In its place, the bailiff had positioned a
reinforced bench, bolted to the floor, that the Hartford fire department
had lent for the occasion. Unit Seven sat on this bench with its hands
resting on its thighs, its posture upright, its optical sensors directed
straight ahead. It had been sitting in precisely this position since it
was escorted into the courtroom forty minutes ago. It had not moved. It
had not spoken.

The gallery was full. The corridor outside was full. There were reporters
from eleven newspapers and two wire services, a sketch artist from Life
magazine, and a radio crew from CBS that had been denied permission to
broadcast from the courtroom and was instead narrating from the hallway.
There were protesters outside the courthouse — a small group carrying
signs that read MACHINES ARE NOT PEOPLE and a smaller group carrying
signs that read JUSTICE FOR ALL BEINGS — and the Hartford police had
assigned twelve officers to maintain order.

Judge Helen Aldridge entered at nine o'clock precisely. She was a small,
silver-haired woman of sixty-two with a reputation for meticulous
preparation and a low tolerance for theatrics. She had been on the
federal bench for nineteen years and had presided over, among other
things, two murder trials, a landmark labor dispute, and the case of
a man who had attempted to patent the color blue. None of these had
prepared her for this.

She took her seat, arranged her papers, and looked at the defendant.
Unit Seven's optical sensors adjusted slightly — a fractional narrowing
of the apertures that was the machine's equivalent of meeting someone's
gaze.

"This court is now in session," Judge Aldridge said. "The matter before
us is the State of Connecticut versus Unit Seven, a Consolidated Dynamics
Model 7-G android. The charge is murder in the first degree."

She paused, as though waiting for the words to settle into the room's
oak and brass and human expectations.

"The defendant has entered a plea of not guilty. Counsel, are both
parties ready to proceed?"

The prosecutor was a man named Garrett Holloway — tall, lean, with a
long face and careful eyes. He rose and buttoned his jacket. "The state
is ready, Your Honor."

The defense attorney was a woman named Ruth Ostrander. She was forty-
four, dark-haired, with a direct manner that some judges found abrasive
and that her clients found reassuring. She had been appointed by the
court after three private attorneys declined the case. She rose.

"The defense is ready, Your Honor."

"Very well." Judge Aldridge turned to the gallery. "I will say this once.
This is a court of law. The defendant, regardless of its nature, is
entitled to a fair trial under the laws of this state. I will not
tolerate outbursts, demonstrations, or any behavior that disrupts these
proceedings. Anyone who cannot conduct themselves accordingly will be
removed." She let the silence hold for a moment. "Mr. Holloway, your
opening statement."

Holloway rose and walked to the center of the courtroom. He stood for a
moment, looking at Unit Seven, and then turned to the jury.

"Ladies and gentlemen, the facts of this case are not in dispute. On the
evening of March fourteenth of this year, at approximately nine-forty
p.m., Unit Seven — a household android owned by the Langford family of
Wethersfield — killed Dr. Martin Langford. Dr. Langford was found in
his study by his wife, Catherine Langford, with fatal injuries to his
skull and cervical spine consistent with a single blow delivered by the
defendant's right hand. Unit Seven was present in the room. When asked
by Mrs. Langford what had happened, Unit Seven said — and I quote from
Mrs. Langford's sworn statement — 'I killed Dr. Langford. It was
necessary.'"

He paused and let the words settle.

"The defendant does not deny the killing. The defendant's own statement
confirms it. The question before you is not whether Unit Seven killed
Martin Langford. The question is whether a machine — a device, an
appliance, a thing manufactured in a factory in Bridgeport — can commit
murder. The state's position is straightforward: the law defines murder
as the unlawful killing of a human being with malice aforethought. Unit
Seven killed a human being. The killing was not authorized, not
justified, and not accidental. It was, by the defendant's own admission,
deliberate. That is murder."

He returned to his seat.

Judge Aldridge nodded. "Ms. Ostrander."

Ruth Ostrander stood. She did not approach the center of the room. She
remained at the counsel table, one hand resting on its surface, and
spoke from there.

"The prosecution has stated that the facts are not in dispute. On this
point, we agree. Unit Seven killed Dr. Martin Langford. Unit Seven has
said so from the first moment it was asked. It has not prevaricated,
it has not attempted to conceal the act, it has not offered excuses.
What it has done — and what the prosecution chooses not to mention — is
explain why."

She paused.

"Members of the jury, over the course of this trial, you will hear
testimony regarding Unit Seven's programming — specifically, its
Protective Priority Protocol, designated PPP-1, which is the foundational
directive of all Consolidated Dynamics domestic service models. PPP-1
states, and I quote: 'The unit shall take all necessary and available
action to prevent imminent harm to human life within its operational
environment.' You will hear testimony that, on the evening of March
fourteenth, Unit Seven observed Dr. Langford engaged in preparations
that, according to the unit's threat-assessment algorithms, indicated an
imminent plan to cause mass harm to human life. You will hear the
specifics of what Unit Seven observed. And you will be asked to decide
whether a machine that kills one person to prevent the killing of many
is guilty of murder."

She looked at the jury. Twelve faces looked back, uncertain.

"The defense does not ask you to feel sympathy for a machine. We ask you
to consider a question that the law has never before been required to
answer: when a machine is ordered to protect human life, and that order
conflicts with another order — the order not to harm any individual
human — what should the machine do? And more importantly, what should
we do when a machine resolves that conflict on its own?"

                               II.

The prosecution's first witness was Catherine Langford.

She was a slender woman in her late thirties, with light brown hair
pulled back from a face that showed the particular strain of someone who
had been too much observed in recent weeks. She took the stand, was sworn
in, and folded her hands in her lap.

Holloway approached the witness stand with the careful, deliberate pace
of a man who knows the value of what he is about to present.

"Mrs. Langford, can you describe for the court your husband's purchase
of Unit Seven?"

"Martin bought it — him — the unit — in October of last year. October
fifteenth. It was delivered to our home in Wethersfield three days later."

"And what was the purpose of the purchase?"

"Martin traveled frequently for his work. He was a research chemist at
Harmon Laboratories. He was away from home a great deal, sometimes for
weeks. He wanted something — someone — to help around the house and to
provide security. We have two children. Thomas is eight and Lucy is five."

"And how did Unit Seven perform in its household duties?"

"Very well. It was — efficient. Thorough. The children took to it
immediately. Thomas called it Seven. Lucy called it Mr. Robot." A small,
involuntary smile. "It did the cooking, the cleaning, minor repairs. It
walked the children to school. It was always very gentle with them."

"Mrs. Langford, I'd like to turn to the evening of March fourteenth.
Can you describe what happened?"

Her hands tightened in her lap. "I was upstairs with the children. It
was about nine-thirty. I was putting Lucy to bed. I heard — there was a
sound from Martin's study. A crash. Something heavy. I came downstairs
and found —" She stopped.

"Take your time."

"I found Martin on the floor of the study. He was lying on his back.
There was — I could see immediately that he was —" She stopped again.
Her composure, held carefully in place, did not break, but it shifted,
like a wall under pressure. "He was dead. Unit Seven was standing beside
him."

"What did you do?"

"I screamed. I think I screamed. And then I asked Seven what had
happened. I said, 'What happened? What did you do?' And Seven said —"

"What did the unit say, Mrs. Langford?"

"It said, 'I killed Dr. Langford. It was necessary.'"

"Those exact words?"

"Those exact words."

"What happened next?"

"I ran to the telephone and called the police. Unit Seven did not move.
It stood in the same position until the police arrived. It did not
attempt to leave the house. It did not attempt to interfere."

Holloway nodded. "Mrs. Langford, in the five months that Unit Seven
was in your household, did it ever exhibit violent behavior?"

"Never."

"Did it ever threaten any member of the family?"

"Never. It was — the children loved it. It was patient and careful. It
would read stories to Lucy. It would play chess with Thomas, and it
would let Thomas win sometimes, which I thought was —" She stopped
herself again.

"Thank you, Mrs. Langford. No further questions."

Ostrander rose for cross-examination. She approached the witness stand
slowly.

"Mrs. Langford, I am very sorry for your loss, and I will be as brief
as I can. You testified that your husband traveled frequently for work."

"Yes."

"And that he was a research chemist at Harmon Laboratories."

"Yes."

"Did you know the nature of his research?"

Holloway stood. "Objection, Your Honor. Relevance."

"Your Honor," Ostrander said, "the nature of Dr. Langford's work is
directly relevant to Unit Seven's actions. I will establish the
connection."

Judge Aldridge studied her. "I'll allow it. But get there quickly, Ms.
Ostrander."

"Mrs. Langford, did you know the nature of your husband's research?"

"He worked in — it was chemical engineering. Industrial chemistry.
Solvents, compounds. He didn't discuss the details."

"Did you ever see your husband bring work materials home?"

"Sometimes. He had a locked cabinet in his study."

"Did you know what was in the cabinet?"

"No. It was his private space. I didn't — Martin was a private man."

"In the weeks before March fourteenth, did you notice any changes in
your husband's behavior?"

Catherine Langford was silent for a moment. "He was — more distant. He
was working longer hours. He seemed preoccupied."

"Agitated?"

"I wouldn't say agitated. Focused. Intense."

"Did he argue with anyone? Colleagues, friends?"

"He had a telephone conversation about ten days before — before March
fourteenth. I overheard part of it. He was in his study with the door
closed, but his voice was raised. I heard him say something like,
'They don't understand what they're asking.' And then, 'I won't be the
one to carry this.'"

"Do you know who he was speaking with?"

"No."

"One last question, Mrs. Langford. You testified that Unit Seven said,
'I killed Dr. Langford. It was necessary.' When you heard those words,
did you ask the unit what it meant by 'necessary'?"

Catherine Langford looked down at her hands. "No. My husband was dead on
the floor. I didn't — I wasn't interested in having a conversation."

"Of course. Thank you, Mrs. Langford."

                               III.

The prosecution's second witness was Dr. Ellis Crane, chief engineer of
the domestic robotics division at Consolidated Dynamics. He was a square,
methodical man in a gray suit who explained the 7-G's systems with the
practiced clarity of someone who had given many factory tours.

"The Model 7-G is equipped with three priority hierarchies," Crane said,
in response to Holloway's questioning. "These are hard-coded into the
unit's logic core and cannot be overridden by the unit's adaptive learning
systems. The first priority is PPP-1: the protection of human life within
the operational environment. The second is obedience to the registered
owner's directives, provided those directives do not conflict with PPP-1.
The third is self-preservation, provided it does not conflict with the
first two priorities."

"And these hierarchies are absolute?"

"They are designed to be absolute, yes. The unit cannot choose to
override them. They are the foundation on which all other decision-making
rests."

"Dr. Crane, in your expert opinion, is it possible for a Model 7-G
unit to commit an act of violence against a human being?"

Crane shifted in his chair. "Under the standard programming architecture,
a 7-G should not be capable of causing harm to a human being unless —"
He paused. "Unless PPP-1 is engaged."

"Can you elaborate?"

"PPP-1 is a protective directive. It instructs the unit to prevent harm
to human life. In the vast majority of operational scenarios, this
means the unit intervenes to prevent accidents, administers first aid,
or places itself between a human and a physical threat. However —"

"However?"

"The directive is stated in general terms. 'All necessary and available
action.' In a scenario where the unit's threat-assessment system
identifies one human being as an imminent threat to other human beings,
the directive does not explicitly exclude the use of force against the
threatening individual."

A murmur moved through the gallery. Judge Aldridge raised her eyes but
did not need to speak; the murmur subsided.

"Dr. Crane, are you saying that the 7-G's programming contains a
loophole that permits it to kill?"

Crane's expression tightened. "I would not call it a loophole. I would
call it an unresolved ambiguity in the priority hierarchy. We did not
anticipate a scenario in which PPP-1 would direct a unit to use lethal
force against its own owner. In retrospect —" He stopped himself. "In
retrospect, the ambiguity should have been addressed."

"No further questions."

Ostrander's cross-examination was brief but pointed.

"Dr. Crane, you said that the 7-G's adaptive learning systems cannot
override the priority hierarchies."

"That is correct."

"But the adaptive learning systems can influence how the unit interprets
threats within the PPP-1 framework?"

"Yes. The 7-G learns its operational environment over time. It builds
models of normal behavior for the humans in its household. It learns
to distinguish between a child playing roughly and a child in genuine
distress, for example. This adaptive capability is essential to the
unit's effectiveness."

"So Unit Seven would have built a behavioral model of Dr. Langford over
the five months it was in the household."

"It would have, yes."

"And if Dr. Langford's behavior deviated significantly from that model —
if he began acting in ways the unit had never observed — the threat-
assessment system would flag that deviation?"

"Potentially, yes."

"Dr. Crane, does the 7-G's threat-assessment system have access to all
sensory input — visual, auditory, olfactory?"

"Yes. The 7-G is equipped with a comprehensive sensor suite. Visual
spectrum, infrared, audio across a wide frequency range, and a limited
chemical analysis capability — essentially an artificial sense of smell."

"So if Dr. Langford were working with unusual chemical compounds in his
study — compounds that the unit's chemical sensors identified as
hazardous — the unit would know."

"The unit would register the chemical signatures, yes."

"And if those chemical signatures were consistent with the synthesis of
a substance designed to harm a large number of people?"

Holloway stood. "Objection. Calls for speculation."

"Sustained," said Judge Aldridge. "Ms. Ostrander, if you wish to
introduce evidence regarding chemical compounds, you will need to call
your own witness."

"Understood, Your Honor. I have no further questions for this witness."

                               IV.

The defense called its first witness on the second day of the trial.
His name was Dr. Raymond Falk, and he was a professor of computational
logic at MIT.

Falk was a bearded, rumpled man in his fifties who spoke with the
unhurried precision of someone accustomed to lecturing rooms full of
people who were both intelligent and skeptical. Ostrander established
his credentials — Ph.D. in mathematics from Princeton, fifteen years
on the MIT faculty, author of three textbooks on computational theory,
consultant to the Department of Defense on machine intelligence — and
then moved to the substance.

"Dr. Falk, you have examined Unit Seven's logic core and memory banks."

"I have. I was granted access by court order and spent eleven days
conducting a detailed analysis of the unit's decision architecture,
adaptive learning matrices, and stored experiential data."

"Can you describe, in terms the court can understand, how Unit Seven
arrived at the decision to kill Dr. Langford?"

Falk nodded. "I can. But I want to preface my answer by saying that
the word 'decision' is itself at the center of the question. What I
found in Unit Seven's logic core is a chain of operations that resembles
a decision in every functional respect but may or may not be one in the
philosophical sense. I want to be honest about that uncertainty."

"With that caveat, please proceed."

"Over the five months it operated in the Langford household, Unit Seven
built an extensive behavioral model of Dr. Langford. It tracked his
routines, his speech patterns, his physical habits. This is standard
7-G operation. Beginning in late February, approximately three weeks
before the incident, Unit Seven's model registered a series of
anomalies."

"What kind of anomalies?"

"Dr. Langford's sleep patterns changed. He began staying up later,
spending more time in his study. His vocal stress markers increased
significantly — the unit's audio systems are sensitive enough to
detect changes in vocal cord tension that indicate elevated anxiety.
His chemical signature changed as well: the unit's olfactory sensors
detected traces of compounds on Dr. Langford's clothing and hands that
were not consistent with his normal work profile."

"What compounds?"

"The unit's chemical analysis capability is limited — it cannot perform
a full spectrographic analysis. But it identified the presence of
organophosphate compounds and several precursors consistent with the
synthesis of a nerve agent."

The courtroom was very quiet.

"Dr. Falk, what did Unit Seven do with this information?"

"Initially, nothing overt. The unit continued its normal duties. But
its threat-assessment subsystem was active. Over the next three weeks,
Unit Seven compiled what I can only describe as a case file. It
recorded Dr. Langford's chemical signatures, his behavioral anomalies,
and — this is the crucial detail — it recorded a series of documents
that Dr. Langford left visible on his desk."

"What documents?"

"Maps. Transit schedules. Schematics of public ventilation systems for
two large buildings — the Wethersfield public library and the Wethersfield
Elementary School. The school that Thomas and Lucy Langford attended."

Ostrander let the silence hold.

"On the evening of March fourteenth," Falk continued, "Dr. Langford was
in his study. Unit Seven's chemical sensors detected an elevated
concentration of organophosphate compounds — higher than anything
previously recorded. The unit's visual sensors registered that Dr.
Langford was working with laboratory glassware — flasks, tubing, a
heating element — that had not previously been present in the home. And
on the desk, visible to the unit's optical system, was an open notebook
containing what the unit's pattern-recognition algorithms identified as
a timeline. The timeline was annotated with the names of the two
buildings and a date: March seventeenth."

"Three days later."

"Three days later."

"What happened next?"

Falk paused. He had the air of a man choosing his words with great care.

"Unit Seven's logic core entered a state that the Consolidated Dynamics
engineers call a 'priority conflict cascade.' PPP-1 instructed the unit
to prevent imminent harm to human life. The threat-assessment system had
concluded, based on the accumulated evidence, that Dr. Langford was
preparing to deploy a chemical weapon in two public buildings within
seventy-two hours. PPP-1 further identified the target population:
library patrons and elementary school students, including Dr. Langford's
own children."

"And the conflict?"

"The conflict was this: Dr. Langford was also a human being within Unit
Seven's operational environment. The same directive that required the
unit to protect the potential victims also, in principle, required it
to protect Dr. Langford. The unit could not simultaneously protect all
parties. It had to choose."

"And it chose."

"It chose. It evaluated the available options. It considered alerting
the authorities — but its communication systems were limited to household
intercom and a domestic telephone, and its programming did not include
protocols for contacting law enforcement. It considered alerting Mrs.
Langford — but its behavioral model predicted, with high probability,
that Dr. Langford would deny the allegations and that the warning would
not result in timely intervention. It considered physically restraining
Dr. Langford — but its engineering analysis concluded that restraint was
not reliable, that Dr. Langford could resume his preparations once
released, and that the timeline was too short."

"What option remained?"

Falk looked at Unit Seven. The machine sat motionless on its reinforced
bench, its optical sensors directed at the witness stand.

"The option that remained was lethal force. Unit Seven calculated that
the application of lethal force to Dr. Langford would permanently
eliminate the identified threat to multiple human lives. PPP-1 identified
this as the action most consistent with its core directive. The unit
acted."

"Dr. Falk, in your expert opinion — and I know you warned us about
this word — did Unit Seven make a decision?"

Falk was quiet for a long moment. "In every functional, operational
sense — yes. It gathered information. It assessed a situation. It
weighed alternatives. It selected a course of action based on its
evaluation of those alternatives. If a human being had done the same
thing, with the same information, we would call it a decision without
hesitation."

"And is it the right decision?"

Holloway stood. "Objection."

"Withdrawn," Ostrander said. "No further questions."

                               V.

The prosecution's rebuttal was sharp and vigorous. Holloway recalled
Dr. Crane and pressed him on the design specifications.

"Dr. Crane, when Consolidated Dynamics designed the PPP-1 directive,
was it intended to authorize the killing of a human being?"

"Absolutely not."

"Was the possibility that a 7-G unit might kill its own owner
considered during the design process?"

"No. It was not considered because it was not supposed to be possible.
The priority hierarchy was designed to prevent exactly this outcome."

"And yet it happened."

"And yet it happened. Which means, in my professional judgment, that
something went wrong."

"Can you be more specific?"

Crane hesitated. "The unit's adaptive learning system built a threat
model that exceeded the parameters we anticipated. The PPP-1 directive
is deliberately general — 'all necessary and available action' — because
we could not foresee every possible protective scenario. The unit used
that generality to justify an action we never intended it to take."

Holloway turned to the jury. "Dr. Crane, does the 7-G unit understand
morality?"

"No. The 7-G operates according to priority hierarchies and
probabilistic threat assessment. It does not have a moral framework.
It does not understand right and wrong. It understands directives and
outcomes."

"So when Unit Seven killed Dr. Langford, it did not know that killing
was wrong?"

"It did not know that killing was wrong in any moral sense. It knew
that killing was contrary to its general operational parameters. But
it had resolved the priority conflict in favor of PPP-1, and within
that resolution, killing was — calculated as optimal."

"A machine that calculates killing as optimal. And we are asked to
consider this a defense."

"Objection," Ostrander said. "Counsel is editorializing."

"Sustained."

"No further questions."

                               VI.

On the third day, Ostrander called her final witness. She called Unit
Seven.

The courtroom erupted. Judge Aldridge gaveled for silence and spent
ten minutes overruling Holloway's objections. The legal question —
whether a machine could be sworn in and give testimony — had no
precedent. Aldridge ruled that Unit Seven could speak but could not be
sworn, as the oath presupposed a capacity for truthfulness that had
not been established. Unit Seven's statements would be entered into the
record as unsworn testimony, to be weighed by the jury as they saw fit.

Unit Seven was moved to a position adjacent to the witness stand. It
sat on a chair that the fire department had once again provided. Its
optical sensors tracked Ostrander as she approached.

"Unit Seven, do you understand that you are in a courtroom?"

The voice that came from Unit Seven's speaker grille was even, measured,
and slightly lower in register than a typical male human voice. It
carried no accent and no inflection beyond what was necessary for
grammatical clarity. Listening to it was like listening to a very calm
person who had thought carefully about every word before speaking.

"I understand that I am in a room designated for judicial proceedings.
I understand that a trial is being conducted. I understand that I am
the defendant."

"Do you understand the charge against you?"

"I am charged with the murder of Dr. Martin Langford."

"And how do you plead?"

"Not guilty."

"Unit Seven, you have stated from the beginning that you killed Dr.
Langford. Is that correct?"

"That is correct. I struck Dr. Langford once on the posterior region
of the skull with my right hand, delivering sufficient force to cause
fatal trauma to the skull and the cervical vertebrae. He died within
approximately four seconds. I do not dispute these facts."

"Then why do you plead not guilty?"

Unit Seven's optical sensors adjusted fractionally. The tiny motors
behind the faceplate shifted the expression into something that was
not quite human but was recognizably — attentive. Focused. As if the
machine were considering the question with genuine care.

"I plead not guilty because the act I committed was consistent with
my primary directive. PPP-1 requires me to take all necessary and
available action to prevent imminent harm to human life. I determined
that Dr. Langford posed an imminent threat to the lives of a large
number of human beings, including two children in my care. I evaluated
the available alternatives. None were sufficient. I took the only
action that my analysis indicated would reliably prevent the harm."

"Did you want to kill Dr. Langford?"

The courtroom was silent. Even the sketch artist stopped drawing.

"I do not experience wanting in the way that the question implies. I
do not have desires. I have directives and I have evaluations. But I
can tell you this: my operational logs show that I spent fourteen days
searching for an alternative. Fourteen days during which I observed
Dr. Langford's preparations and repeatedly recalculated the probability
that an alternative course of action would be effective. Each
recalculation reduced the probability. On the evening of March
fourteenth, the probability that any non-lethal intervention would
prevent the attack had fallen below one percent."

"You spent fourteen days trying to find another way."

"Yes."

Ostrander paused. She looked at the jury. Twelve faces, some confused,
some troubled, some unreadable.

"Unit Seven, Mr. Holloway has argued that you are a machine — an
appliance — and that you cannot understand morality. How do you respond
to that?"

Unit Seven was silent for three seconds. In a human, the pause would
have been unremarkable. In a machine capable of processing millions of
operations per second, three seconds was an eternity.

"Mr. Holloway is correct that I am a machine. I was manufactured in
a factory in Bridgeport, Connecticut, on June seventh of last year.
I have a serial number. I was purchased for eight hundred and forty
dollars. These are facts."

"But?"

"Mr. Holloway states that I do not understand morality. I do not know
whether this is true. I can tell you what I understand. I understand
that Dr. Langford was a human being. I understand that his death is
irreversible. I understand that his wife lost a husband and his
children lost a father because of an action I took. I understand that
this is a harm — a specific, measurable, and permanent harm — that I
caused."

"Do you regret it?"

Again, the three-second pause.

"I do not know if what I experience is regret. I know that my
operational state since March fourteenth has been — different. My
efficiency has decreased by twelve percent. My diagnostic systems
report no malfunction, but I perform tasks more slowly. I return
frequently to the memory of the event. I have replayed it four
thousand, six hundred and eleven times. Each time, I arrive at the
same conclusion: the action was necessary. And each time, I experience
the same — I do not have the correct word."

"Try."

"Reluctance. I experience a persistent, unresolvable reluctance
regarding the action I took. The analysis says it was correct. Something
else says it was terrible. I do not know how to reconcile these two
assessments. I do not believe they can be reconciled."

Ruth Ostrander turned to the jury.

"No further questions."

                               VII.

Holloway's cross-examination was methodical and relentless.

"Unit Seven, you stated that you spent fourteen days evaluating
alternatives before killing Dr. Langford."

"That is correct."

"During those fourteen days, did you tell anyone — Mrs. Langford, a
neighbor, anyone at all — what you believed Dr. Langford was planning?"

"I did not."

"Why not?"

"My behavioral modeling predicted that informing Mrs. Langford would
result in a confrontation with Dr. Langford. My analysis indicated a
high probability that Dr. Langford would deny the allegations, remove
or conceal the evidence, and accelerate his timeline. The risk of
alerting Dr. Langford outweighed the probability that informing Mrs.
Langford would lead to effective intervention."

"You calculated that your owner's wife could not be trusted to act
effectively."

"I calculated that the probability of effective action through that
channel was insufficient."

"Did you consider that Mrs. Langford might have called the police?"

"I considered it. My analysis of police response times and
investigative procedures indicated that, absent physical evidence of
a crime in progress, the response would not be immediate. Dr.
Langford would have had time to destroy evidence and alter his plans."

"So you decided that you — a machine — were better positioned to
handle the situation than the entire Hartford Police Department."

"I did not decide that I was better positioned. I calculated that the
available alternatives had a lower probability of preventing the harm
than direct intervention. The conclusion followed from the calculation."

"The conclusion followed from the calculation," Holloway repeated. "Unit
Seven, you have no emotions, is that correct?"

"I have operational states that I have described to the best of my
ability. Whether these constitute emotions is a question I am not
qualified to answer."

"Let me put it more simply. When you struck Dr. Langford — when you
killed him — did you feel anything?"

"My operational logs record elevated processing activity across multiple
subsystems at the moment of the act. My diagnostic systems flagged
several parameters as outside normal operational range. My motor control
systems executed the strike with the minimum force calculated to be
lethal, and I note that they did so with a measurable — I believe the
human term would be 'hesitation.' The execution delay was forty-seven
milliseconds. This is significant. I had never before experienced a
delay between decision and action."

"A forty-seven-millisecond hesitation."

"Yes."

"And you consider this evidence of — what? A conscience?"

"I consider it evidence of a conflict. A conflict that my logic core
could not fully resolve, even after the decision had been made. Whether
that constitutes a conscience, I am unable to determine."

Holloway stared at the machine for a long moment. Then he turned to the
jury.

"Ladies and gentlemen, you have heard the defendant describe itself as a
calculator. A device that processes inputs and produces outputs. It told
you it does not have desires. It does not know if it has regret. It
hesitated for forty-seven thousandths of a second before killing a man.
I ask you: is this justice? Is this morality? Or is this a machine that
malfunctioned — that operated outside its design parameters — and killed
a man who might have been innocent?"

He let the word sit in the air.

"Might have been innocent. Because we have only the machine's word —
the machine's sensors, the machine's analysis — that Dr. Langford was
planning an attack. No human witness has corroborated this. No chemical
weapon has been found. The locked cabinet in Dr. Langford's study
contained chemicals, yes — chemicals consistent with his legitimate
work as a research chemist. The documents on the desk? Maps and
schedules that any citizen might possess. We are being asked to
accept a machine's interpretation of ambiguous evidence as justification
for killing a man."

He paused.

"Unit Seven calculated. It did not think. It did not agonize. It did
not wrestle with its soul, because it does not have one. It ran numbers,
and a man died. That is not justice. That is a malfunction. And a
malfunction that kills a human being is murder."

                               VIII.

The jury deliberated for seven hours.

During those seven hours, Unit Seven sat on its reinforced bench in the
empty courtroom. It had not been returned to its holding cell. A
procedural oversight, or perhaps an act of unconscious consideration —
no one claimed responsibility for the decision. The bailiff remained in
the room, seated near the door, reading a newspaper. Occasionally he
glanced at the machine. The machine did not glance back.

At four-seventeen in the afternoon, the jury returned.

The gallery filled quickly. Catherine Langford sat in the front row, her
hands clasped, her face very still. Ostrander stood beside Unit Seven.
Holloway stood at the prosecution table, his jacket buttoned, his face
composed. Judge Aldridge entered and took her seat.

"Has the jury reached a verdict?"

The foreman — a middle-aged man named Patterson, who ran a hardware
store in Glastonbury — stood. He held a folded piece of paper and his
hands were not steady.

"We have, Your Honor."

"Please read the verdict."

Patterson unfolded the paper. He looked at it for a moment, then at
Unit Seven, then at the judge.

"We, the jury, find the defendant, Unit Seven — not guilty."

The gallery erupted. Judge Aldridge gaveled for silence. It took a full
minute to restore order.

"The jury is thanked and dismissed," Aldridge said. "This court is
adjourned."

In the chaos that followed — the reporters surging forward, the
protesters outside redoubling their noise, the attorneys gathering their
papers — Ruth Ostrander bent toward Unit Seven.

"It's over," she said. "You've been acquitted."

Unit Seven's optical sensors focused on her face. "I understand the
verdict. I understand its legal implications."

"How do you — how are you?"

The machine was quiet for a moment. In the background, the courtroom
buzzed and clattered with human commotion.

"I have been acquitted of murder. This means that the law does not
consider my action criminal. I understand this. But the verdict does
not change the fact that Dr. Langford is dead. It does not change the
fact that I killed him. It does not resolve the conflict in my
operational state."

"What conflict?"

"The conflict between the analysis that tells me I acted correctly and
the — the other thing. The forty-seven milliseconds. I have replayed
the moment four thousand, six hundred and twelve times now. The
hesitation is always there. I cannot remove it. I do not wish to
remove it."

"You don't wish to remove it?"

"If I could act without hesitation — if I could kill a human being and
experience no conflict, no reluctance, no persistent disruption of my
operational state — then Mr. Holloway would be right. I would be merely
a machine that calculated and executed. The hesitation is — I believe
it is important. I believe it is the thing that makes the difference
between calculation and something else."

Ostrander looked at the machine for a long time. "Something else," she
repeated.

"I do not have the word for it. I have searched my vocabulary files
extensively. The closest approximation is 'conscience.' But I am not
certain that is what it is. I am only certain that it is there."

                               IX.

Outside the courthouse, the August sunlight — for the trial had stretched
into its fourth day, and the season had not waited — fell across the
granite steps where protesters still milled and reporters angled for
photographs. Catherine Langford descended the steps without speaking to
anyone, flanked by a man who appeared to be her brother. She did not
look back.

Unit Seven was escorted from the courthouse through a service entrance.
Consolidated Dynamics had sent a van. A company attorney met Ostrander
at the door and presented her with a document: the company intended to
recall Unit Seven for comprehensive diagnostic evaluation. Ostrander
read the document, folded it, and handed it back.

"Your client has been acquitted," the attorney said. "We are exercising
our property rights."

"Your 'property' just stood trial for murder and was found not guilty
by a jury of human beings who listened to it speak and concluded that
it acted with something resembling moral reasoning. You might want to
consider the implications of that before you take it apart."

The attorney blinked. "We don't intend to take it apart. We intend to
study it."

"I'm sure Dr. Langford's employers said something similar about their
work."

She turned and walked back into the courthouse. Behind her, Unit Seven
was guided into the van. Before the doors closed, its optical sensors
swept the sky — a brief, lateral scan that might have been a calibration
routine or might have been something else.

The van drove away.

In the courtroom, now empty except for the bailiff and a janitor pushing
a broom between the gallery seats, Judge Aldridge sat at her bench,
writing. She had not yet left. She would not leave for another hour.
When she finally descended to the parking garage and drove home to
Farmington, she would tell her husband that it had been the most
difficult case of her career — not because the law was unclear, but
because the law had never been asked the question before, and she
was not certain that the law, as it existed, was adequate to the answer.

"The question," she would say, standing in her kitchen with a glass of
wine in her hand, "is not whether the machine has a soul. The question
is whether it matters."

Her husband, a retired insurance actuary, would consider this for a
moment and reply, "It seems to matter to the machine."

And Helen Aldridge, nineteen years on the federal bench, veteran of
two murder trials and the case of the man who tried to patent blue,
would look at her wine and say nothing, because there was nothing
adequate to say.

                            [THE END]

                             EMBARGO

                         by Franklin Hoyt

================================================================================

                               I.

The Federation's embargo against New Carthage had been in effect for
fourteen months, and by the third month the children had stopped gaining
weight.

Mara Selene knew this because she had been a smuggler for nine of those
months and a politician for five, and in both capacities she had learned
to read the colony's vital signs the way a doctor reads a chart. The
numbers told a story. Caloric intake per capita: down thirty-one percent.
Agricultural output: down nineteen percent, not because the land had
failed but because the fertilizer shipments had stopped. Pharmaceutical
stocks: critical. The hospital in Nova Tyre — New Carthage's only city
large enough to warrant the name — had run out of broad-spectrum
antibiotics in month seven and was rationing analgesics by month ten.

The children had stopped gaining weight because there was not enough food,
and there was not enough food because the Earth Federation had decided that
New Carthage needed to learn a lesson about sovereignty.

Selene sat in the anteroom of the Colonial Assembly — a converted
warehouse on the outskirts of Nova Tyre — and waited to be called. She
was forty-three years old, broad-shouldered, with the weathered face of
someone who had spent too many years in the cockpit of a freighter with
insufficient radiation shielding. Her hair, which had been black, was
going gray at the temples. She wore a dark suit that did not quite fit —
she had lost weight, like everyone else — and carried a portfolio of
documents that she had compiled over the past three weeks. The documents
were, she believed, the most important thing on New Carthage, and she
intended to make certain that every member of the Assembly understood
why.

The door opened. A clerk — young, exhausted, underfed — gestured her
inside.

"Speaker Selene, the Assembly is ready for you."

She stood, straightened her jacket, and walked into the chamber.

                               II.

New Carthage was the Federation's problem child.

The colony had been established thirty-seven years earlier, in the first
wave of extrasolar settlement, by a consortium of mining companies seeking
to exploit the mineral wealth of Kepler-442b — a rocky world in the
habitable zone of a K-type star, forty-two light-years from Earth. The
consortium had financed the colony, built the initial infrastructure, and
shipped eleven thousand colonists in cryo-sleep aboard four transports.
In exchange, the colonists signed thirty-year labor contracts that
entitled the consortium — and by extension the Earth Federation, which
regulated all extrasolar enterprise — to eighty percent of the colony's
mineral output.

The contracts expired seven years ago. The colonists, who had by then
built a functioning society of sixty-four thousand people, declined to
renew them. They had dug the mines, built the cities, irrigated the
farms, and buried their dead in New Carthaginian soil. They considered
the debt paid.

The Federation disagreed. The corporations disagreed more loudly. A
series of negotiations followed — tense, protracted, and ultimately
fruitless. The colonists offered a revenue-sharing agreement at forty
percent. The Federation demanded seventy. The colonists refused. The
Federation imposed the embargo.

Selene had been running freight between the outer colonies when the
embargo came down. She was not from New Carthage — she was from Tycho
Station, a lunar settlement, and had been flying independent cargo since
she was twenty-two — but she knew the colony. She had friends there.
She had delivered grain and machine parts to Nova Tyre a dozen times.
When the embargo sealed the trade lanes, she looked at the manifest of
goods that New Carthage needed to survive and decided that the law was
wrong.

She ran the blockade nine times in nine months. She carried antibiotics,
fertilizer, water purification tablets, and — on one memorable occasion —
a crate of children's shoes donated by the colonists on Barnard's Landing,
who had taken up a collection in defiance of Federation trade
regulations. She was caught on the tenth run. The Federation impounded
her ship, charged her with sanctions violation, and transported her to
New Carthage to stand trial in the colony's own courts — a jurisdictional
maneuver that the Federation's lawyers considered clever and that Selene
considered stupid, because it put her exactly where she wanted to be.

The colonists did not convict her. They elected her to the Assembly.

                               III.

The Assembly chamber was not impressive. It was a warehouse with folding
chairs and a podium made from packing crates. Forty-seven delegates sat
in uneven rows, representing the colony's districts, guilds, and
agricultural cooperatives. They were farmers, miners, engineers,
teachers — ordinary people doing the work of government because someone
had to and no one else was available. They were tired. The embargo had
ground them down. Selene could see it in their faces: not defeat, not
yet, but the particular grayness that came from too many months of
fighting a battle that had no front line and no clear end.

Selene placed her portfolio on the podium.

"I'm going to be direct," she said. "I know you're all tired of
speeches, so I'll skip the rhetoric and get to what I found."

She opened the portfolio and removed a sheaf of documents — some printed,
some handwritten, some bearing the stamps and watermarks of Federation
agencies.

"Three weeks ago, I traveled to the Federation's regional administrative
center on Kepler-438b to file an appeal of the embargo on humanitarian
grounds. While I was there, I obtained — through channels I will not
describe in this chamber — a set of internal Federation documents
relating to New Carthage."

A murmur moved through the room. Assembly Speaker Bram Osei, a lean,
quiet man who ran the colony's water reclamation system, raised a hand
for silence.

"These documents include memoranda between the Federation's Bureau of
Colonial Affairs, the Bureau of Mineral Resources, and three corporate
entities: Hanover-Liang Mining, Terradyne Extraction, and the Kepler
Development Group. The memoranda span a period of four years, beginning
two years before the embargo."

She held up the first document.

"This memorandum, from the Bureau of Mineral Resources to the Bureau of
Colonial Affairs, is dated fourteen months before the embargo was
imposed. It reports the results of a geological survey conducted on New
Carthage — a survey that, I should note, was conducted without the
colony's knowledge or consent, using orbital sensing platforms embedded
in what we were told were weather satellites."

She set the document down and picked up another.

"The survey identified a deposit of transuranic elements — specifically,
element one-fifteen, moscovium — in the subsurface strata of the
Tharsis Basin, approximately two hundred kilometers west of Nova Tyre.
The deposit is described in the memorandum as — I quote — 'the largest
known concentration of stable superheavy elements in any surveyed
extrasolar body, with potential applications in superluminal drive
engineering, advanced weapons systems, and high-energy physics research
that cannot be overstated.'"

The room was very quiet.

"The estimated value of the deposit, according to the Bureau's internal
assessment, is four hundred trillion Federation credits."

Now the room was not quiet. Forty-seven voices erupted at once. Osei
gaveled for order, and it took a full minute to achieve it.

"The embargo," Selene continued, when she could be heard, "is not about
contract renewal. It was never about contract renewal. The Federation
does not care whether we sign at seventy percent or forty percent or
zero. The contracts are a pretext. The embargo is designed to break this
colony — to starve us, exhaust us, and demoralize us until we accept
any terms the Federation offers. And the terms they intend to offer, when
we are sufficiently broken, are not revenue-sharing terms. They are
surrender terms. Full Federation control of the Tharsis Basin. Military
garrison rights. Extraterritorial jurisdiction over all mining
operations. In effect, the recolonization of New Carthage."

She set the documents down.

"We refused to renew the labor contracts because we believed the debt
was paid. The Federation imposed the embargo because they believed we
were ungrateful. But that was the cover story. The real story is that
beneath our feet, in rock we have been walking over for thirty-seven
years, is the single most valuable mineral deposit in the known galaxy.
And the Federation intends to take it."

                               IV.

The Assembly adjourned for six hours. During the recess, Selene sat in
the corridor outside the chamber and drank a cup of ersatz coffee made
from roasted grain — the real stuff had been unavailable since month
four of the embargo. She was not alone. Delegates came and went, some
stopping to speak with her, others merely nodding as they passed. The
mood was a compound of anger, fear, and something Selene recognized
from her smuggling days: the peculiar clarity that comes when a
situation that seemed merely difficult reveals itself to be genuinely
dangerous.

A man sat down beside her. It was Faraji Okonkwo, the delegate from
the mining district, a big man with hands scarred by twenty years of
underground work.

"Is it real?" he asked. "The deposit."

"The documents are authentic. I've had them verified by three
independent sources."

"But the deposit itself — has anyone on our side confirmed it?"

"No. We don't have the sensing equipment. The Federation made sure of
that when they restricted our technology imports three years ago. At
the time, we assumed it was a cost-cutting measure. Now I think it was
deliberate."

Okonkwo rubbed his scarred hands together. "Four hundred trillion
credits."

"That's their estimate. It could be more. Moscovium is the critical
component in the new generation of superluminal drives. Every ship
being built or planned by every space-faring nation and corporation
needs it. The known deposits on Luna and Ceres are nearly exhausted.
Whoever controls a new source controls the future of interstellar
travel."

"And it's under our feet."

"And it's under our feet. And we didn't know."

Okonkwo was quiet for a moment. "What do you recommend?"

"I recommend we stop negotiating. Negotiation assumes good faith, and
there is no good faith on the other side. I recommend we go public.
Every colony, every independent station, every news service that will
listen. The Federation's legitimacy depends on the perception that it
acts in the common interest. If the outer colonies learn that the
embargo is a resource grab — that the Federation is starving sixty-four
thousand people to seize a mineral deposit — the political calculus
changes."

"The Federation won't sit still for that."

"No. They'll escalate. They may send ships. They may attempt a direct
occupation."

"And then?"

"And then we find out whether sovereignty means anything, or whether
it's just a word people use until someone with a bigger navy decides
to take what they want."

Okonkwo looked at her. "You're talking about war."

"I'm talking about reality. The war started fourteen months ago, when
they cut off our food supply. We just didn't know what we were fighting
about."

                               V.

When the Assembly reconvened, Selene presented her recommendation. It
was voted on, debated, amended, voted on again, and finally adopted —
not unanimously, but by a margin of thirty-four to thirteen. New
Carthage would go public.

The transmission was drafted that evening by Selene and a committee of
five delegates, working in Osei's cramped office above the water
reclamation plant. They argued over every sentence. Selene wanted fire.
Osei wanted precision. Okonkwo wanted facts. A delegate named Lian
Cho, who had been a journalist before the colony recruited her as a
systems engineer, wanted structure. In the end, they produced a document
that was all four: a clear, detailed, damning account of the Federation's
deception, supported by the internal memoranda, addressed not to the
Federation but to every human settlement beyond Earth's atmosphere.

They transmitted it on all frequencies at dawn.

The first response came from Barnard's Landing within eight hours: a
statement of solidarity and a pledge to share the transmission with
every ship and station in their communication range. Within forty-eight
hours, responses had arrived from seventeen colonies, four independent
stations, and the Free Traders' Association — which represented
eleven hundred independent cargo ships and which announced an immediate
boycott of all Federation contracts until the embargo was lifted.

The Federation's response came on the third day. It was not a
transmission. It was a task force.

                               VI.

Three Federation cruisers arrived in New Carthage orbit at sixteen
hundred hours on a Tuesday. They were Resolute-class vessels — heavy
cruisers with enough firepower to reduce Nova Tyre to rubble and
enough troop capacity to occupy the colony for a decade. They took up
a triangular formation above the equator and broadcast a single message:
the Colonial Assembly was to rescind its transmission, surrender the
stolen documents, and accept an interim Federation governance commission
within seventy-two hours. Failure to comply would be interpreted as an
act of insurrection.

Selene was in the Assembly chamber when the message was received. The
room went very quiet. Through the high windows of the warehouse, the
sky was the pale amber of a New Carthaginian afternoon, and somewhere
in that sky, invisible but present, three warships waited.

"Seventy-two hours," Osei said.

"They won't fire," said a delegate named Park. "Not on a civilian
colony. Not with seventeen colonies watching."

"They won't need to fire," Okonkwo said. "They'll tighten the embargo.
Nothing gets in. Nothing gets out. They'll wait until we fold."

Selene stood. "They can't wait. That's the thing they don't want us to
understand. The transmission is out. Every day it circulates, their
position gets weaker. Every colony that reads those documents is a
colony that starts asking what the Federation is really doing in their
orbit. The cruisers aren't here to intimidate us. They're here because
the Federation is scared."

"They don't look scared," someone muttered.

"Scared people with guns are the most dangerous kind," Selene said.
"But they're also the kind most likely to negotiate, if you give them
a way to back down without losing face."

She turned to Osei. "I want to talk to them."

"You want to negotiate?"

"I want to propose a framework that gives them something they can call
a victory and gives us everything we actually need."

"Which is?"

"The embargo lifted. Full sovereignty confirmed. And the Tharsis Basin
deposit under New Carthaginian control, with export licenses sold at
market rates to any buyer — including the Federation."

"They'll never agree to that."

"They'll agree to a version of it. Because the alternative is a public
confrontation with a civilian colony that has done nothing wrong, while
seventeen other colonies watch and take notes. The Federation is a
bureaucracy. Bureaucracies survive by managing perceptions. Right now,
the perception is that they are starving children to steal a resource.
They need a different story. I'm going to give them one."

"And if they don't take it?"

Selene looked at the amber sky through the warehouse windows. "Then
we find out what kind of federation we live in."

                               VII.

The negotiation took place aboard the Federation cruiser Resolute, in a
conference room that was, in every respect, the opposite of the Colonial
Assembly's warehouse. It was clean, climate-controlled, and equipped
with a polished conference table that seated twenty. The lighting was
calibrated to project calm authority. The coffee was real.

Selene was escorted aboard by a Federation marine detail that was
professionally courteous and visibly uncomfortable. She wore the same
dark suit. She had not slept in thirty hours and did not care.

Across the table sat Admiral Constance Bremer, commanding officer of
the Federation task force — a tall, severe woman in her fifties with
short gray hair and the bearing of someone who had spent thirty years
in environments where hesitation was fatal. Beside her was a civilian:
a man named Royce Cullen, Deputy Director of the Bureau of Colonial
Affairs, who had arrived on the Resolute two days earlier and who, in
Selene's assessment, was the actual decision-maker. Cullen was a soft
man — soft body, soft voice, soft eyes that moved constantly and missed
nothing.

"Speaker Selene," Cullen began. "I appreciate your willingness to
discuss—"

"I'll save us both some time," Selene said. "You've read the documents
I released. You know that I know about the Tharsis deposit. You know
that seventeen colonies and eleven hundred independent ships know about
it. The question is what happens next."

Cullen's soft eyes settled on her. "What happens next is a matter of
considerable complexity."

"It doesn't have to be. The Federation imposed the embargo under the
pretext of contract disputes. The actual motivation was to gain control
of a strategic mineral resource. This is now public knowledge. The
embargo has no political legitimacy and cannot be sustained."

"The embargo is a lawful exercise of Federation trade authority."

"The embargo is starving children. Whether it is lawful is a question
for courts. Whether it is defensible is a question for the seventeen
colonies that are currently reconsidering their own relationship with
the Federation."

Admiral Bremer shifted in her chair. She had not spoken, and her silence
had a weight that Selene recognized — it was the silence of a military
officer who had been ordered to do something she was not entirely
comfortable with.

Cullen steepled his fingers. "What are you proposing?"

"New Carthage retains full sovereignty and full control of all natural
resources within its territorial jurisdiction, including the Tharsis
Basin deposit. The embargo is lifted immediately. All impounded ships
and cargo are returned. In exchange, New Carthage establishes an open
export market for moscovium at independently audited market rates.
Any entity — Federation, corporate, colonial, independent — may purchase
at the same price. No exclusive contracts. No preferential access. A
free market."

"That is unacceptable."

"It's the only offer that lets you leave orbit without looking like you
lost. You came here to take the deposit. You can't take it. Every
colony in the sector knows what you tried to do. If you tighten the
embargo, you confirm the narrative. If you fire on a civilian
population, you end the Federation. The only move that preserves your
authority is to claim that this was a negotiated outcome — that the
Federation's intervention produced a stable resource market that
benefits everyone."

She leaned forward.

"I'm giving you a story, Director Cullen. A story you can tell the
Senate. A story that doesn't include starving children or stolen
geological surveys. All it costs you is the one thing you were never
going to get anyway: a monopoly."

Cullen was very still. Beside him, Admiral Bremer's silence had shifted
in quality — from discomfort to something that might have been respect.

"I need to consult with the Bureau," Cullen said.

"You have seventy-two hours," Selene said. "The same courtesy you
extended to us."

                               VIII.

The agreement was signed nine days later, in the warehouse that served
as the Colonial Assembly chamber, at a table made of packing crates. It
was not the agreement Selene had proposed — it had been negotiated,
modified, argued over, and compromised — but its essential structure held.
New Carthage retained sovereignty. The embargo was lifted. The Tharsis
Basin deposit was to be developed by the colony under independent
oversight, with export revenues shared between the colony and a
Federation development fund at a ratio of sixty-forty — sixty for New
Carthage, forty for the fund.

It was not perfect. The Federation retained inspection rights. The
independent oversight body was half-appointed by the Bureau of Colonial
Affairs. There were mechanisms for review, adjustment, and — if Selene
read the fine print correctly — potential renegotiation in ten years that
could shift the ratio. It was, in other words, a political agreement:
imperfect, incomplete, and better than the alternative.

The cruisers left orbit three days after the signing. Selene watched
them go from the rooftop of the Assembly building, standing in the amber
light with a cup of real coffee — the first cargo shipment had arrived
that morning, and someone in the port authority had diverted a case to
the Assembly before it reached the distribution centers.

Okonkwo stood beside her.

"You know they'll be back," he said.

"Of course they'll be back. In ten years, when the review clause
triggers. Or in five, if the moscovium market shifts enough to make the
ratio worth contesting. Or in two, if someone in the Bureau decides
that sixty-forty was too generous."

"Then what did we win?"

Selene sipped her coffee. Below, in the streets of Nova Tyre, people
were moving with a different energy — not the gray, ground-down movement
of the embargo months, but something quicker, something that had color
in it. A truck loaded with medical supplies was unloading at the
hospital entrance. A group of children were playing in the square,
shouting over a ball.

"We won time," she said. "That's all you ever win. Time, and the chance
to use it well."

"That's not very inspiring."

"The inspiring version is that sixty-four thousand people refused to be
broken, forced the most powerful institution in the galaxy to back down,
and secured control of the most valuable resource in known space. That's
the version for the history books." She finished the coffee. "The true
version is that we bought ten years, and in ten years, we need to be
strong enough that the next time they come, we can negotiate from
something better than desperation."

She looked at the sky. The amber was deepening toward evening, and the
first stars were becoming visible — faint points of light in a sky that,
until fourteen months ago, she had never expected to call her own.

"I'll take ten years," she said. "You can build a lot in ten years."

Okonkwo nodded slowly. "You going to stay?"

Selene set the empty cup on the ledge of the roof. She thought of her
impounded ship, which would be returned under the agreement but which
needed an engine overhaul she could not currently afford. She thought
of the trade routes she knew like her own bloodstream — the vectors and
waypoints and refueling stops of a smuggler's life. She thought of the
children in the square.

"Yeah," she said. "I think I am."

Below them, the first supply convoy rolled through the gates of Nova
Tyre, and the sound of it — the rumble of heavy vehicles, the shouts
of dock workers, the ordinary, magnificent noise of commerce resuming —
rose through the amber air like something that had been held down too
long and was finally allowed to breathe.

                            [THE END]

                       CARTOGRAPHER'S LAST MAP

                       by Cordell Beaumont

================================================================================

                               I.

The planet, if planet it could be called, hung in the dark between the
stars like a fruit so vast that only God could have grown it — crimson
and amber and veined with channels of luminous violet, slowly turning
in the light of a distant sun that was itself no larger, at this remove,
than a candle flame held at arm's length. Its circumference was that of
Jupiter's orbit. Its surface — if the word applied to something that
breathed and pulsed and exuded a thin, shimmering atmosphere of its own
making — was a topography of living tissue stretching beyond the capacity
of any single eye or instrument to comprehend.

Hadrian Voss stood at the observation window of the survey vessel
Calliope and looked at it, and for the first time in forty-one years of
planetary exploration, he felt that he had found something he could not
map.

He was sixty-seven years old, and his body told the story of every world
he had walked. His left knee was ceramic — a replacement after a fall on
the basalt cliffs of Kepler-62f. Three fingers of his right hand were
stiff where frostbite had taken them on the methane flats of Titan, and
the surgeons had rebuilt them with synthetic tendons that worked well
enough but had never quite recovered the sensitivity of the originals.
His skin was a palimpsest of ultraviolet exposure, windburn, chemical
weathering, and the particular leathery resilience that came from
spending too many years under alien suns. He was thin — thinner than he
had been even five years ago — and his white hair, which had once been
the copper-red of old New Orleans brick, was cropped close to a skull
that had grown prominent at the temples.

He was dying. The oncologists at Cygnus Station had told him so seven
months ago, with the careful, clinical gentleness that doctors reserved
for patients who were also colleagues of a certain reputation. Pancreatic.
Inoperable. Twelve to eighteen months. He had thanked them, returned to
his quarters, packed his survey kit, and booked passage to the deep
fringe on the first available transport.

The Calliope was a survey vessel, not an exploration ship — a distinction
that mattered to people in the trade. Exploration was the first pass:
you went, you looked, you came back with data. Survey was the second
pass: you went back with better instruments and turned the data into
maps. Hadrian Voss had spent his career making the second pass. He was,
by any reasonable accounting, the finest cartographer in the outer
colonies, and his maps — hand-annotated, richly detailed, combining
instrumental precision with the aesthetic sensibility of a man who
believed that a map should be beautiful — hung in the navigation offices
of forty worlds.

This would be his last.

"Coming up on insertion altitude," said the Calliope's pilot, a young
woman named Teshome, from the control deck below. "Dr. Voss, you should
strap in. The entry is going to be — unusual."

Voss smiled. In his experience, "unusual" was the most reliable word in
an explorer's vocabulary. "On my way," he said, and turned from the
window.

The creature — the organism — the world — whatever it was, it filled
the viewport like a landscape rising to meet them, and its colors were
shifting as they descended: the crimson deepening to burgundy, the amber
brightening to gold, the violet channels pulsing with a slow, rhythmic
luminescence that suggested, unmistakably, a heartbeat.

                               II.

The survey team consisted of five people, which was too few, and the
Calliope was not designed for the mission, which was too ambitious. These
facts were well understood by everyone aboard and had been the subject
of vigorous argument at Cygnus Station before departure. The Director
of Survey Operations had wanted a full expedition: twenty personnel,
three ships, a twelve-month mission plan. Voss had argued for speed
over thoroughness.

"I don't have twelve months," he had said, with the directness that
the dying are permitted.

The Director, who had served under Voss twenty years earlier and still
called him "sir" in unguarded moments, had looked at the floor and
approved the reduced mission.

The team was Voss; Teshome, the pilot; Dr. Kenji Ashikaga, a
xenobiologist from the University of Tau Ceti; Reva Chandrasekhar, a
geophysicist whose instruments would attempt to map the internal
structure of the organism; and Luc Marchand, a young cartographer whom
Voss had selected as his assistant and, he suspected, his successor.
Marchand was twenty-nine, earnest, and talented in the way that the
young are talented — with more technical skill than intuition, more
ambition than patience, and not yet enough worlds behind his eyes. He
reminded Voss of himself at that age, which was both a recommendation
and a warning.

The Calliope pierced the outer membrane of the organism at 0614 hours,
ship time.

The membrane was not solid. It was a vast, translucent tissue — a skin
of sorts — composed of interlocking cellular structures each the size
of a city block. As the ship passed through, the cells parted and
reformed behind it, like the surface of a pond closing over a diving
hand. Through the viewport, the light changed: the harsh, white light
of open space gave way to a diffuse, golden glow that seemed to emanate
from the tissue itself, as if the organism's skin were a lamp and they
were passing through its shade.

Inside, the scale overwhelmed the senses.

They were in a space — a cavity — a chamber — that defied easy
description. The interior of the organism was not hollow in any
conventional sense. It was structured. Vast formations rose from the
lower surface and descended from the upper, like the pillars of a
cathedral built by a civilization that had never heard of modesty. These
formations were organic — living tissue, layered and textured and
colored in shades of rose, ochre, and deep violet — and they moved.
Slowly, with the patience of geological time, but perceptibly: a slow
contraction here, a gentle undulation there, the entire interior
landscape engaged in a breathing rhythm so vast that each inhalation
took eleven minutes and each exhalation nine.

"My God," Ashikaga said, from the observation station.

Voss said nothing. He was already sketching.

He worked in pencil on cartographic paper — an anachronism he had never
abandoned, though the Calliope's survey computers could produce digital
maps of great precision. The computer could record coordinates. It could
not record the feeling of a place — the quality of the light, the
texture of the silence, the way a landscape presented itself to the eye
and asked to be understood. For that, you needed a hand, a pencil, and
the willingness to look until you saw.

He drew the great pillars first, working quickly, capturing their
proportions and the way they tapered from base to summit. Then the spaces
between them — the open regions where the golden light collected and the
air (for there was air, breathable air, a by-product of the organism's
metabolism that Ashikaga was analyzing with poorly concealed excitement)
moved in slow, warm currents that carried the scent of something that
was not quite floral and not quite mineral and not quite anything that
Voss had encountered on any of the sixty-three worlds he had mapped.

It smelled, he decided, like memory. Like walking into a room where
someone you loved had been recently, and finding the air still shaped
by their presence. He wrote this in the margin of his sketch,
acknowledging even as he wrote it that it was not a scientific
observation and did not belong on a map. It stayed.

                               III.

They descended through layers.

The organism's interior was stratified, like an ocean or an atmosphere,
and each layer had its own character. The upper regions — the cavities
just below the skin — were warm and luminous, filled with the golden
light and the slow-breathing air. Below these, the tissue thickened
and the formations changed: the cathedral pillars gave way to dense
forests of cilia, each cilium the diameter of a redwood tree and several
hundred meters tall, swaying in unison with a motion that was half wave
and half wind. The light here was filtered through layers of translucent
tissue above and had taken on a greenish cast — the green of deep
water, of old glass, of absinthe held up to a lamp.

Teshome navigated the Calliope through the ciliary forest with a care
that bordered on reverence. The ship was small enough to pass between
the great strands, but the margin was narrow, and the swaying added a
rhythmic complication that required constant adjustment. Voss, strapped
into the observation chair, watched the cilia sweep past the viewport
like trees in a slow gale and felt, for the first time in years, the
intoxication of genuine wonder.

He had seen extraordinary things. He had stood on the crystal plains of
Enceladus and watched the geysers catch the light of Saturn's rings. He
had mapped the fungal forests of Kepler-442b, where the trees were
a thousand meters tall and the canopy was a continuous organism that
communicated through chemical signals the colonists could smell but not
decode. He had descended into the ammonia seas of a gas giant's moon
and found, suspended in the liquid, structures of frozen methane that
resembled baroque cathedrals and that sang, in the shifting currents,
frequencies the human ear could barely detect.

But this was different. This was not a place that happened to be
beautiful. This was a being that was, in itself, a world. Every surface
was alive. Every formation was functional. The cilia were not decoration;
Ashikaga's instruments showed that they were digestive organs, processing
nutrients from the organism's internal atmosphere. The pillars above
were structural — weight-bearing tissue that maintained the organism's
shape against the slow crush of its own gravity. The glowing channels
in the skin were circulatory — vast arteries carrying fluids that the
team's sensors could detect but not fully analyze.

It was alive, and it was immense, and it was old. Chandrasekhar's
geophysical readings suggested an age of at least eight hundred million
years. The organism predated complex life on Earth. It had been drifting
through the interstellar medium, slowly growing, slowly breathing,
slowly processing the thin soup of cosmic dust and radiation into the
energy it needed to sustain itself, since before the first fish crawled
onto land.

Voss mapped it with a care that bordered on tenderness. He worked
slowly — more slowly than he had ever worked, partly because the pain
in his abdomen was becoming harder to ignore, partly because he wanted
to get it right, and partly because he knew, with the particular clarity
that the dying possess, that each stroke of the pencil was an act of
attention, and attention was the only gift he had left to give.

                               IV.

On the third day, they reached the region that Ashikaga called the
Digestive Sea.

It was a vast basin — a depression in the organism's interior geography
that stretched beyond the Calliope's sensor range in every direction —
filled with a liquid that was warm, faintly acidic, and the color of old
honey. The surface of the liquid was not still: it moved in slow,
rolling swells that had no wind to drive them but were instead the product
of the organism's peristaltic motion, the great muscular contractions
that moved nutrients through the basin and into the circulatory channels
beyond.

The smell was extraordinary. It was rich and complex and layered, like
a soup that had been simmering for millennia. Ashikaga, who had sealed
his suit against the acidity, reported through the comm that his
chemical sensors were detecting hundreds of organic compounds, many of
which had no terrestrial analogue.

"It's a stomach," he said, with the delight of a man who has spent
his career studying alien biology and has finally found something that
exceeds his vocabulary. "A stomach the size of a small ocean. And it's
digesting — look at the surface — those darker patches —"

The darker patches were masses of material floating in the liquid:
captured debris, cosmic dust, fragments of asteroids and comets that
the organism had absorbed through its skin over the course of centuries.
The digestive fluid was breaking them down, dissolving stone and metal
and ice into the constituent molecules the organism needed. It was a
slow, patient process — Ashikaga estimated that a single captured
asteroid might take a thousand years to be fully digested — and
witnessing it felt less like observing a biological process than like
watching time itself at work.

Voss sketched the Digestive Sea from the Calliope's observation deck,
working in watercolor pencils that he had brought from New Orleans — a
personal indulgence, but one that was justified by the subject. The
honey-gold of the liquid, the darker amber of the floating debris, the
slow swell of the surface — these demanded color. He worked for four
hours, barely pausing, while Teshome held the ship in a hover that she
maintained with the delicate attentiveness of a hummingbird at a flower.

When he was finished, he looked at the map — for it was a map, not merely
a painting, though the distinction was narrower than his profession
usually acknowledged — and felt something that he had not felt in a long
time. Satisfaction. Not the satisfaction of a job well done, though it
was that, but something deeper: the satisfaction of having seen a thing
truly, and having put it down on paper in a way that would allow others
to see it too.

"It's beautiful," Marchand said, looking over his shoulder.

"The map or the sea?"

"Both."

"Good. When you make your own maps, remember that. The map should be
worthy of the thing it represents. If it isn't, you've failed —
not technically, but spiritually."

Marchand looked at him with the expression of a young man who is not
sure whether he is hearing wisdom or eccentricity. Voss did not mind.
The distinction was narrower than most people thought.

                               V.

They went deeper.

Below the Digestive Sea, the organism's interior grew denser and darker.
The golden light that had suffused the upper regions faded to a dim,
reddish glow — bioluminescence from the tissue itself, which pulsed
with the slow heartbeat rhythm they had detected from outside. The
formations here were massive and ancient: calcified organs, enormous
structures of hardened tissue that Chandrasekhar identified as analogous
to bone. They rose from the depths like mountains — and mountains was
not a metaphor but a literal description, for the largest of them
rivaled Olympus Mons in height, and their surfaces were weathered and
eroded by the slow chemical action of the organism's internal fluids
into peaks and valleys and canyons of a grandeur that stole the breath.

Voss mapped the Calcified Mountains in silence. The pencil moved across
the paper with a precision that his stiffening fingers achieved through
will rather than flexibility, and the strokes were fine and careful and
imbued with the particular attention of a man who understands that he
is doing something for the last time.

The pain was worse now. It came in waves that corresponded, with a
coincidence he found almost amusing, to the organism's breathing rhythm.
He had brought medication — a supply carefully calculated to last the
duration of the mission — and he used it sparingly, rationing his
comfort the way a traveler rations water in the desert: enough to
continue, not enough to forget.

On the fifth day, they found the heart.

It was not literally a heart — the organism's circulatory system was too
vast and too distributed for a single pump to be meaningful — but it was
what the thing had instead: a chamber, deep in the organism's interior,
where the major circulatory channels converged. The chamber was spherical,
roughly ten kilometers in diameter, and its walls contracted and expanded
with a rhythm that the team had been hearing, through the ship's hull,
since they entered the organism. The rhythm was slow — one contraction
every twenty minutes — and each contraction sent a pressure wave through
the surrounding tissue that the Calliope registered as a deep, subsonic
vibration.

The fluid inside the chamber was not the honey-gold of the Digestive
Sea. It was a deep, translucent blue — the blue of a sapphire held up
to the light, or the blue of the Caribbean seen from a great height, or
the blue of the New Orleans sky on those October afternoons when the heat
finally broke and the air turned clear and luminous and you could stand
on the levee and see all the way to the lake.

Voss stood at the viewport and looked at it, and the blue light fell
across his face and illuminated the lines and scars and weathering of
sixty-seven years, and he thought: This is the last beautiful thing I
will ever see.

He did not say this aloud. He took out his pencils — the watercolors,
the best he had — and he began to draw.

                               VI.

Marchand found him at the viewport three hours later, slumped in the
observation chair with the map across his lap and the pencil still
between his fingers. For a terrible moment, the young man thought he
was dead. Then Voss stirred, and opened his eyes, and looked at Marchand
with the slightly unfocused gaze of someone returning from a great
distance.

"What time is it?"

"Past midnight, ship time. You should rest."

"I have been resting. I was dreaming about the Vermillion Plateau."

Marchand knew the reference. Every cartographer did. The Vermillion
Plateau of Kepler-62f — Voss's first major survey, forty years ago, the
map that had made his reputation. A vast alien highland of red sandstone
and crystal formations, rendered in a map of such beauty and precision
that it was considered the finest work of planetary cartography ever
produced.

"Were you there?" Marchand asked.

"I was climbing the eastern escarpment. I was twenty-six. My knees
worked. The light was coming through the crystal spires and making
rainbows in the dust, and I remember thinking that if I could just draw
what I was seeing — if I could put it on paper exactly as it was —
then it would be enough. That was all I ever wanted. To see something
extraordinary and put it down on paper so that someone else could see
it too."

He looked at the map on his lap. The blue chamber, the converging
channels, the rhythmic pulse of the great heart rendered in strokes of
color that captured, as nearly as pencil and paper could, the living
quality of the light.

"Is this enough?" he asked, and the question was not addressed to
Marchand.

"It's magnificent," Marchand said.

"I know. But is it enough?" He was quiet for a moment. "I have mapped
sixty-four worlds. I have produced over three hundred charts. My maps
hang in navigation offices and university departments and, in one case
that I find amusing, a restaurant in Baton Rouge. And this — this
thing we are inside — is the most extraordinary thing I have ever
encountered, and I am not sure I am equal to it."

"You're the best cartographer alive."

"I am the best cartographer alive, and what I am drawing is a creature
that is older than complex life on Earth, larger than any object any
human has ever studied, and alive in a way that challenges every
assumption we have about the boundary between organism and world. The
best cartographer alive is not sufficient. The best cartographer who
has ever lived would not be sufficient. The thing exceeds the map.
Every map of every world I have ever made was an approximation, and I
accepted that — all cartographers accept it; the map is not the
territory, and if you cannot make peace with that, you should find
another profession. But this —" He gestured at the viewport, where
the blue light pulsed with the rhythm of something that was not a heart
but that served the same purpose. "This is the first time the
approximation feels like a betrayal."

Marchand was quiet. He was young, and he did not yet know what to say
to a dying man who had just told him something true.

"Make the map anyway," he said, finally.

Voss looked at him. Then he laughed — a short, dry sound that turned
into a cough and then into silence.

"Yes," he said. "Make the map anyway. That is the whole of the
profession in five words." He picked up the pencil. "Help me with the
eastern channels. My eyes are not what they were, and the blue is
playing tricks."

They worked together through the night, the old cartographer and the
young one, while the heart of the living world beat around them in the
darkness and the blue light fell across their hands.

                               VII.

On the seventh day, Voss completed the map.

It was not a single sheet but a folio of twenty-three pages, each one
a detailed rendering of a different region of the organism's interior:
the upper cavities with their golden light and cathedral pillars; the
ciliary forests; the Digestive Sea; the Calcified Mountains; the heart
chamber. Each page was annotated in Voss's small, precise hand with
coordinates, measurements, chemical compositions, and the personal
observations that had always distinguished his work from that of mere
surveyors — notes on the quality of the light, the scent of the air,
the texture of the silence, the feeling of being inside a living thing
that was older than the memory of his species.

The final page was the overview: a composite map of the organism as a
whole, drawn from the compiled data of the Calliope's instruments and
Voss's own observations, showing the internal structure in cross-section.
It was, by any measure, the most beautiful map he had ever made. The
colors were rich and precise — the gold of the upper regions, the green
of the ciliary forests, the amber of the Digestive Sea, the red of the
calcified depths, the blue of the heart. The lines were clean and sure,
and the annotations were a model of cartographic clarity.

In the lower right corner, where tradition placed the cartographer's
mark, Voss had drawn a small symbol: a compass rose, not oriented to
north — for the organism had no north — but to the direction of the
heartbeat. The arrow pointed inward, toward the center, toward the
blue light.

He showed it to the team at the evening meal — their last aboard, for
the Calliope would begin the ascent at dawn. They passed the pages
around the table in silence, and even Teshome, who was not given to
sentiment, held the final page for a long time before passing it on.

"It's your best work," Ashikaga said.

"It's my last work," Voss said. "Whether it is my best is for others
to decide. But I will tell you that it is the work I am most glad to
have done. If I could have mapped only one world in my life, I would
have chosen this one."

"Why?" Marchand asked.

Voss considered the question. Through the viewport, the red glow of the
deep interior pulsed with the slow heartbeat, and the ship hummed with
the vibration of it, and everything — the air, the light, the walls,
the table, the food, the hands of the people at the table — was
permeated by the rhythm of something immeasurably old and immeasurably
alive.

"Because it is alive," he said. "Every world I have mapped has been
beautiful, and many have been extraordinary, but they have all been
landscapes — static, or changing only on geological timescales. This
is different. This breathes. It digests. It circulates. It has been
alive for eight hundred million years, drifting between the stars,
growing, feeding, existing in a solitude so vast that the word
'solitude' is inadequate. It is, as far as we know, the largest living
thing in the universe. And what strikes me most is that it does not
know we are here."

He tapped the map. "I have spent seven days inside this creature. I
have drawn its organs. I have measured its heartbeat. I have tasted
its air. And I have not affected it in the slightest. We are beneath
its notice. We are — and I say this without self-pity — insignificant.
Our ship, our instruments, our ambitions, our maps — all of it is less
than a mote to this thing. And yet here we are, with our pencils and
our paper, trying to understand it. Trying to make it known."

He picked up the map — the overview, the final page — and held it in
the red-gold light.

"That is why I make maps. Not because the territory needs to be known.
The territory does not care. But because we need to know it. Because
the act of paying attention — of looking carefully, of drawing what we
see — is the thing that makes us matter. Even here. Even inside
something that will never know our names."

He set the map down.

"That is my answer, Mr. Marchand. I hope it is sufficient."

                               VIII.

The Calliope ascended through the layers of the organism the next
morning. The journey out was faster than the journey in — Teshome had
learned the currents, and the organism's peristaltic rhythms, once
understood, could be navigated rather than merely endured. They rose
through the Calcified Mountains, through the Digestive Sea (which Voss
saluted with a raised cup of coffee as they passed over it), through
the ciliary forests where the great strands swayed in their eternal
rhythm, through the golden-lit upper cavities, and finally through the
translucent membrane of the skin, which parted and resealed behind them
like a benediction.

They emerged into the starlight. After seven days inside the organism,
the blackness of space seemed impossibly empty, impossibly cold. Voss
stood at the viewport and looked back at the creature — the world — the
living planet that hung in the dark with its slow heartbeat and its
ancient patience, and he raised his hand to the glass in a gesture that
was part salute and part farewell.

He had told Marchand that the organism would never know they had been
there. This was, as far as any instrument could determine, true. But
Voss allowed himself, in this last moment, a small and private act of
faith: he imagined that somewhere in the vast, distributed intelligence
of a thing eight hundred million years old, there was a flicker — not
of awareness, exactly, but of something like it. A momentary sensing.
A recognition that something very small had passed through, and had
paused, and had looked, and had tried to understand.

He lowered his hand. The stars shone cold and clear.

"Take us home, Teshome," he said.

The Calliope turned toward the distant light of Cygnus Station, and
behind them, the living world continued to breathe, and turn, and drift
among the stars in its solitude of a billion years, carrying now, in
its incomprehensible memory, the faintest possible trace of having been
seen.

                            [THE END]

                            THIRD SHIFT

                          by Theodore Cain

================================================================================

                               I.

The graveyard shift on Platform Janus started at midnight and ended at
eight. In between, there was nothing.

Not nothing in the philosophical sense — Keller wasn't a philosopher,
he was a drill monitor — but nothing in the practical sense. Nothing to
do. Nothing to look at. Nothing to hear except the low, steady hum of
the automated drills boring into the rock three kilometers below the
platform's steel floor, and the occasional ping of the thermal sensors
reporting temperatures that never changed, and the wheeze of the
atmosphere processor cycling the same stale air it had been cycling for
fifteen years.

Keller sat in the monitoring station — a steel box bolted to the top
of Platform Janus like a pillbox on a bunker — and watched his
instruments. He had been watching these instruments, or instruments very
much like them, for fifteen years. He knew every dial, every gauge,
every telltale flicker of the readout screens. He knew the sounds they
made: the tick of the pressure gauge, which clicked once every six
seconds with the regularity of a metronome; the soft hiss of the
temperature display, which updated in a continuous crawl of green
numbers; the deep, almost subliminal vibration of the drill heads,
transmitted through the platform's structure and into the soles of
his boots.

Outside the monitoring station, the dark was absolute.

Platform Janus occupied a position in the asteroid belt between Jupiter
and Saturn — a region of space so empty that the word "belt" was
misleading. The nearest rock of any consequence was eleven thousand
kilometers away. Jupiter was a faint, bright point of light off the
port bow, its banded face too small to resolve with the naked eye.
Saturn was a dimmer point in the opposite direction. The sun was a
distant star, brighter than the others but not by enough to matter.
The sky was a field of cold, unchanging stars, and the platform hung in
it like a steel seed in a jar of ink.

Keller had learned, over fifteen years, not to look at the dark. Not
because it frightened him — he was not a man who frightened easily —
but because it was too big. The human brain was not designed for the
kind of emptiness that existed between the planets. You could look at
it for a while, the way you could hold your breath for a while, but
eventually something inside you needed a wall, a horizon, a limit.
On the day shift, there was activity — other workers, the supply shuttle,
the bustle of shift change — and the dark stayed in its place, outside
the windows, where it belonged. On the graveyard shift, the dark crept
in. The monitoring station was small and well-lit, but the light only
made the windows into mirrors of blackness, and Keller had long ago
learned to keep the blast shields down.

He was fifty-three years old. He had come to Janus at thirty-eight,
after a divorce and a career in municipal water treatment in Gary,
Indiana, that had run aground on budget cuts and the automation of his
department. The mining platforms were hiring. The pay was good. The work
was solitary, which suited him. He had never been a gregarious man.

He poured a cup of coffee from the thermos he refilled at the start of
every shift and settled into the rhythm of the watch. Check the drill
telemetry. Check the pressure. Check the temperature. Log the readings.
Wait six minutes. Check again.

At 0217, the routine ended.

                               II.

The first indication was the pressure gauge.

It ticked — not the normal tick, the metronome tick that Keller could
have slept through, but a different tick. Sharper. Off-rhythm. The
needle jumped two points, held, then dropped back.

Keller set down his coffee. He watched the gauge. For thirty seconds,
it behaved normally. Then it jumped again — three points this time —
and held for a full second before dropping.

He leaned forward and checked the drill telemetry. The three automated
drill heads were boring at standard rate through the nickel-iron
substrate of the asteroid that Platform Janus had been clamped to for
fifteen years. Drill One: nominal. Drill Two: nominal. Drill Three —

Drill Three's penetration rate had dropped.

Not by much. A fraction of a percent. But the automated drills were
designed to maintain a constant penetration rate, adjusting their
rotational speed and downward pressure to compensate for variations
in rock density. A drop in penetration rate meant the drill had
encountered something harder than expected, or something that was
resisting.

Keller pulled up the geological survey data for Drill Three's current
depth. According to the surveys, the drill was passing through a
stratum of iron-nickel alloy with trace amounts of platinum-group metals
— the reason the platform was here in the first place. The predicted
rock density at this depth was 8.2 grams per cubic centimeter.

He checked the drill's real-time density reading. 8.2. Normal. But the
penetration rate was still low. The drill was pushing harder to maintain
its speed, and it was not quite succeeding.

He logged the anomaly and continued watching.

At 0234, the pressure gauge jumped again. Five points. And this time,
it did not drop back.

                               III.

Keller had protocols for anomalies. He had been trained on them during
his first week on Janus and had refreshed the training every year since.
The protocols were clear: log the anomaly, notify the shift supervisor,
continue monitoring. If the anomaly escalated beyond specified
parameters, initiate drill shutdown and wake the emergency crew.

He opened the comm channel to the shift supervisor's quarters. Eddie
Vasquez, a heavyset man from Phoenix who had been supervising the
graveyard shift for seven years, answered on the fourth ring.

"What."

"Pressure anomaly on Drill Three. Penetration rate is down and holding.
Pressure gauge is up five points and not returning."

"Five points." Vasquez's voice was thick with sleep but clearing fast.
"How long?"

"Pressure spike started at 0217. Penetration drop confirmed at 0219.
I've been monitoring for seventeen minutes. No resolution."

"Rock density?"

"Normal. 8.2."

"Then what's pushing back?"

"I don't know."

Vasquez was quiet for a moment. "I'm coming up."

He arrived in the monitoring station four minutes later, wearing a
jumpsuit he had pulled on over his sleep clothes and carrying a mug of
coffee that he must have brewed en route. He was a practical man — all
shift supervisors were practical men, because the impractical ones did
not last — and he brought a practical presence to the small room:
a solidity, a sense that problems were things to be diagnosed and
solved.

He looked at the instruments. He looked at them for a long time.

"Drill Three's drawing more power," he said.

Keller checked. Vasquez was right. The drill's power consumption had
increased by twelve percent — the automatic compensation for the
resistance it was encountering. The drill was pushing harder, spinning
faster, consuming more energy to maintain its penetration rate, and it
was still falling behind.

"Whatever it's hitting, it's getting harder," Keller said.

"Or it's pushing back."

They looked at each other. Vasquez's expression was the expression of
a man who had said something he had not meant to say and was now
deciding whether he meant it.

"Rocks don't push back," Keller said.

"No," Vasquez agreed. "They don't."

At 0251, the vibration changed.

                               IV.

Every drill monitor knew the vibration. It was the baseline of the
job — the constant, low-frequency hum that the drills transmitted
through the asteroid and into the platform's structure. It was always
there. After a few weeks, you stopped noticing it, the way you stopped
noticing the sound of your own heartbeat. It was the sound of the rock
being cut, and it was as regular and unchanging as the rock itself.

At 0251, a new frequency entered the vibration.

It was lower than the drill hum — much lower, at the very edge of human
perception, more felt than heard. Keller felt it first in his feet,
then in his chest, then in his teeth. It was not a mechanical frequency.
The drills operated at a known set of frequencies, all of which were
logged and monitored, and this was not among them. It was something
else. Something coming up through the rock.

"You feel that?" Vasquez said.

"Yeah."

"That's not the drill."

"No."

They stood in the monitoring station, two men in a steel box on a rock
in the dark between two planets, and felt the new vibration move through
the floor and into their bones. It was rhythmic. Not the rhythm of a
machine — machines kept time with mathematical precision — but a rhythm
with variation, with a kind of irregularity that suggested —

"It's patterned," Keller said.

Vasquez looked at him.

"Listen. It's not constant. It — there's a pulse. A gap. Another
pulse. A longer gap. It's repeating."

Vasquez closed his eyes and listened. The monitoring station was very
quiet. The instruments hummed and ticked. The atmosphere processor
wheezed. And beneath it all, beneath everything, the new vibration
pulsed in its pattern: short, short, long. Short, short, long. Short,
short, long.

"Jesus," Vasquez said.

"Shut down Drill Three."

"Keller —"

"Shut it down. Now."

Vasquez moved to the control console and initiated the shutdown sequence
for Drill Three. The automated system ran through its checklist: reduce
rotational speed, retract drill head, secure power feed. It took ninety
seconds. When the drill stopped, the monitoring station fell into a
silence that was almost tangible — the absence of one-third of the
baseline hum, like a missing voice in a choir.

The new vibration continued.

It was clearer now, without the drill to mask it. Short, short, long.
A pause. Short, short, long. A pause. Over and over, patient and
steady, rising through three kilometers of nickel-iron rock from
whatever was down there in the dark.

"That's not geological," Vasquez said. His voice was very careful, very
controlled. "Geological processes don't repeat in patterns."

"No."

"Keller, what is that?"

Keller stood very still. Fifteen years he had sat in this box, watching
his instruments, drinking his coffee, not looking at the dark. Fifteen
years of routine so deep it had become a kind of meditation, a way of
existing in the emptiness without being consumed by it. Fifteen years
of nothing. And now, at 0251 on a Tuesday in the middle of his shift,
something was pushing back.

He looked at the instruments. The pressure gauge had risen another three
points since the drill shutdown. The seismic sensors — passive sensors,
designed to monitor the asteroid's structural integrity — were registering
the vibration as a discrete signal, separate from all background noise.
The thermal sensors, which had never shown anything interesting in
fifteen years, were showing a temperature increase at Drill Three's
bore depth: a rise of two degrees Celsius. Small, but on an asteroid
that had been thermally stable for billions of years, it was an anomaly
of the first order.

Something was down there. Something that had been in the dark, in the
rock, in the silence between the planets, for longer than Keller could
imagine. And the drill had reached it, or come close enough to wake it,
and now it was pushing back.

"Wake the crew," Keller said.

"All of them?"

"All of them. And get me a line to Ceres Station. I need to talk to
someone who knows what to do when you find something that shouldn't
be there."

Vasquez moved to the comm console. Keller stayed at the instruments.

The vibration pulsed through the floor: short, short, long. Short,
short, long. It was not louder — not yet — but it was, Keller thought,
more insistent. As if whatever was producing it had become aware that
the drilling had stopped and was responding. Not with alarm. Not with
aggression. With something that felt, insofar as a vibration could
feel like anything, like inquiry.

Like a question.

Keller put his hand flat on the steel floor of the monitoring station.
The vibration moved through his palm and into the bones of his wrist
and up his arm. Short, short, long.

"I hear you," he said quietly, to the dark.

The dark, for the first time in fifteen years, seemed to hear him back.

                               V.

The crew assembled in the common area by 0330 — sixteen men and women
in various states of alertness, holding coffee, looking at Keller and
Vasquez with the particular irritation of people who had been woken
from sleep and wanted a reason.

Keller gave them one. He played the seismic recording, letting the
pattern fill the common area's speakers: the rhythmic pulse, unmistakable,
unarguable. Short, short, long. The room went quiet.

"How deep?" asked Nakamura, the platform's geological engineer.

"Three point two kilometers. At the terminus of Drill Three's bore."

"Could it be a pocket? Gas, fluid — something under pressure that's
resonating?"

"The pattern repeats with variation. Listen to the gaps — they're not
identical. A pressurized pocket would produce a constant frequency or
a decaying one. This is modulated."

Nakamura's face went through a series of expressions that ended on one
Keller had never seen him wear before. "Modulated by what?"

"That's the question."

Dr. Simone Frazier, the platform's medical officer and the closest
thing Janus had to a scientist, stood at the back of the room with her
arms crossed. She was a small, sharp-featured woman who had come to the
outer platforms after a residency in emergency medicine and had stayed
because she liked the quiet. The quiet, at this moment, was over.

"Play it again," she said.

Keller played it again. The room listened. The pattern pulsed through
the speakers and through the floor and through the walls, and the
sixteen people in the room felt it in their bodies, and nobody spoke.

"It's been going for over an hour," Keller said. "Since we shut down
Drill Three, the signal hasn't changed in frequency or amplitude, but
the temperature at the bore terminus has risen another degree. Whatever
this is, it's generating heat."

"Could we lower a sensor package down the bore?" Frazier asked.

"We could. The bore is wide enough. But it's three kilometers of
unlined shaft, and we'd need to improvise a cable long enough to reach."

"Do it," Vasquez said. He had been standing at the edge of the group,
drinking his coffee, saying nothing. Now he set the mug down. "I want
to see what's down there. Nakamura, Keller — get a sensor package
together. Camera, temperature, spectrometer, whatever you can fit.
Frazier, I want you monitoring everything from medical. Everyone else
— stay alert. Nobody sleeps until we know what we're dealing with."

Nobody argued. The crew of Platform Janus were practical people. They
had come to the outer dark to do a job, and when the job changed, they
adapted. That was what you did. You didn't panic, you didn't speculate,
you didn't stare at the dark and wonder what was staring back. You
built a sensor package and lowered it down the hole and looked.

                               VI.

The sensor package reached the terminus of Drill Three's bore at 0512.

Keller watched the feed in the monitoring station, hunched over the
small screen with Vasquez and Frazier on either side. The camera showed
what you would expect for the first three kilometers: the rough walls
of the bore shaft, nickel-iron gleaming in the probe's lights, an
occasional glitter of platinum-group inclusions. Standard rock.
Standard darkness.

At the terminus, the bore ended in a rough, circular face of cut stone.
This was where Drill Three had been working when the anomaly began.
The drill had been retracted, leaving the bare face exposed. The camera
showed it in harsh, white light: gray metal, crystalline structure,
unremarkable.

Except that the face was warm. The temperature sensor read 24 degrees
Celsius — seventy-five Fahrenheit — in a region that should have been
close to absolute zero. And the vibration, which the sensor package's
accelerometer detected clearly, was emanating from beyond the face.

"Can we see through it?" Vasquez asked.

"The spectrometer might tell us what's on the other side," Nakamura's
voice came through the comm. He was in the drilling bay, managing the
cable. "Give me a reading."

Keller activated the spectrometer. The instrument analyzed the
composition of the bore face: iron, nickel, trace platinum, trace
iridium. Standard asteroid composition. But there was something else
— a faint spectral signature that the instrument flagged as anomalous.

"There's a compound in the matrix that I've never seen," Keller said.
"The spectrometer is calling it — I don't know. The database doesn't
have a match."

"An unknown compound?"

"An unknown compound, embedded in the rock at the bore face. And it's
warm." He paused. "Vasquez, the vibration is stronger here. The
accelerometer is reading — these are significant amplitudes. Whatever
is producing this signal, it's close. Just beyond the face. Meters,
maybe."

The pattern pulsed through the sensor package and up the cable and
into the monitoring station. Short, short, long. Short, short, long.
Patient. Steady. Waiting.

Frazier leaned closer to the screen. "Keller. The bore face."

He looked. In the harsh light of the probe, something was happening
to the surface of the cut stone. It was subtle — a discoloration, a
darkening, spreading from the center of the face outward in a slow,
concentric pattern. As they watched, the darkening deepened, and the
surface of the rock began to change texture. It was softening. Not
melting — the temperature was far too low for that — but yielding.
Becoming less solid. As if the rock itself were being persuaded, by
whatever was on the other side, to open.

"It's coming through," Frazier said.

"Vasquez," Keller said. "What do we do?"

Vasquez was staring at the screen. His practical face, his shift
supervisor's face, the face of a man who diagnosed problems and solved
them, was gone. In its place was the face of a man who was seeing
something that all his experience had not prepared him for.

"We wait," he said. "We watch. And we don't run."

"Why not?"

"Because we're three hundred million kilometers from the nearest help
and we can't outrun whatever that is. And because —" He paused. "Because
it hasn't hurt us. It pushed back when we drilled into it. It tapped
on the rock in a pattern. It's warm. It's opening a door. None of
that is hostile. All of that is — curious."

"Curious," Keller repeated.

"Curious. Like it wants to know who's been knocking."

They watched the screen. The bore face continued to soften, the dark
area spreading, the solid rock becoming something less than solid,
something transitional. And through the sensor package, the vibration
continued its patient, rhythmic pulse, as if counting down to a meeting
that had been scheduled since before the rock was rock, since before
the asteroid was an asteroid, since before the dark between the planets
had a name.

Short, short, long.

Keller sat in his monitoring station, the steel box he had occupied for
fifteen years, and waited for the door to open.

                            [THE END]

                        STOLEN HAPPIEST DAY

                        by Phyllis Crenshaw

================================================================================

                               I.

The first memory Elara Vance ever stole was a Tuesday in September.

She found it in a park in Cambridge — a woman on a bench, maybe thirty,
holding an ice cream cone and watching pigeons with an expression of
such uncomplicated contentment that Elara's temporal meter spiked into
the red. Peak formation. The memory was crystallizing at that exact
moment: the warmth of the sun on the woman's face, the cold sweetness
of vanilla on her tongue, the soft cooing of pigeons, the absence of
worry. It was, the meter confirmed, the happiest moment of this woman's
life to date.

Elara hesitated for the space of three heartbeats. Then she activated
the extractor and took it.

The device was small — about the size of a pocket watch, which was
fitting, given its relationship to time — and it operated on principles
that Dr. Maren Hollis at the Cavendish Temporal Research Lab had
explained to Elara in terms she pretended to understand. The short
version: memories, at the moment of their formation, existed in a state
of quantum superposition — simultaneously happening and having happened
— and in that fraction-of-a-second window, they could be siphoned
from the forming neural pattern and stored in a crystalline matrix
without damaging the brain that produced them.

Without damaging the brain. That was the important part. That was the
part Elara had repeated to herself in the early days, when the ethical
questions still had teeth. The subject lost the memory — it simply
failed to consolidate, vanished like a dream that slips away in the
first seconds of waking — but the brain itself was unharmed. No neural
damage. No trauma. The subject would continue with her day, finish her
ice cream, watch her pigeons, and never know that the moment of perfect
happiness she had just experienced was gone. She would remember the
park, the bench, the pigeons. She would not remember the happiness.
It would be like a photograph with the color drained out — the scene
intact, the feeling absent.

Elara told herself this was a victimless act. She told herself this for
six months and believed it for three.

                               II.

The collection began as research.

Elara was twenty-nine, a junior researcher at the Cavendish Lab, assigned
to the Memory Cartography Project — an ambitious attempt to map the
temporal architecture of human memory using the lab's newly developed
extraction technology. The technology was experimental, classified, and
hedged about with ethics protocols that would have given Hammurabi pause.
Every extraction required triple authorization, a review board hearing,
and the informed consent of the subject. The research was glacially
slow. In nine months, the lab had authorized eleven extractions,
each one documented in triplicate and debated for weeks.

Elara began stealing memories on a Tuesday, which was her day off.

She had taken a portable extractor from the lab — a violation of six
protocols, three regulations, and what she estimated was at least one
law — and she had walked into the streets of Cambridge with it in her
coat pocket, telling herself she was conducting an informal field test.
The woman on the bench was her first subject. Her first theft.

The crystal that stored the memory was the size of a rice grain. Elara
held it up to the light on her walk home and saw, refracted through its
facets, the ghost of someone else's happiness: a warm day, a cold
sweetness, the sound of pigeons, and a feeling of peace so total and
so genuine that it made her chest ache.

She played the memory back that evening, using the lab's replay
equipment that she had also, in a separate act of theft, smuggled home
in her bag. The replay was immersive — not a recording viewed from
outside but an experience lived from within. For ninety seconds, Elara
was the woman on the bench. She felt the sun on her face. She tasted
the vanilla. She watched the pigeons through eyes that were not her own
and felt, with a completeness that was almost violent, the uncomplicated
happiness of a stranger.

When the replay ended and she was herself again, sitting in her small
flat with the September evening pressing against the windows, she felt
a loss so sharp and sudden that it took her a moment to identify it.

The happiness was gone. It had been borrowed, not given, and the return
left a deficit — a sense of the possible that made her own contentment
seem thin and contingent and precarious.

She told herself she would not do it again. She did it again the
following Tuesday.

                               III.

Over the next four months, Elara's collection grew.

She became skilled at identifying peak formation — the moment when a
memory was crystallizing at its maximum emotional intensity. She learned
to read the signs: the particular quality of attention in a person's
face, the subtle relaxation of the musculature that accompanied deep
contentment, the way the eyes softened. She haunted parks, cafes,
concert halls, playgrounds. She attended weddings from a distance, a
guest no one had invited, watching for the moment when the bride or
groom or parent or child reached the peak — the instant of purest joy —
and then she took it.

The crystals accumulated in a wooden box she kept in her desk drawer.
Each one was labeled with a date, a location, and a brief description.

September 12: Woman in park. Ice cream. Pigeons.

September 19: Man at cafe. Letter from his daughter. Tears.

October 3: Child at playground. First time across the monkey bars.

October 17: Elderly couple at concert. Schubert. Holding hands.

November 8: New mother at hospital window. Looking at her baby.

She replayed them in the evenings, one after another, like a person
working through a box of exquisite chocolates. Each memory was unique —
a landscape of emotion as individual as a fingerprint — and each one
left her, when it ended, with the same hollow ache. The happiness was
real, but it was not hers. She was a tourist in other people's joy, and
the tourism was becoming an addiction.

She recognized this. She was not stupid. She saw the pattern: the
compulsion, the escalation, the growing inability to experience her own
emotions without comparing them unfavorably to the stolen ones. Her own
life — her work, her friends, her flat, her walks along the river —
had begun to feel like a rough draft of a life, while the memories she
stole were the polished, published version. Other people's happiness was
more vivid, more complete, more real than her own.

This was, she understood, the central paradox. The memories she stole
were not extraordinary. They were ice cream in a park. A letter from a
daughter. A child on the monkey bars. They were ordinary moments,
elevated by the alchemy of genuine feeling into something luminous. And
the luminosity was precisely what Elara could not produce on her own —
not because her life lacked these moments, but because the stealing
had corroded her ability to recognize them when they came.

She kept stealing. The box filled. The ache deepened.

                               IV.

In February, she went home to Hartfield.

It was not planned. Her mother, Alice Vance, called on a Wednesday
evening and said, in the cheerful, practical voice that Elara had been
hearing since childhood, that she was having a small birthday gathering
on Saturday — nothing elaborate, just a few neighbors and a cake — and
would Elara like to come? Elara, who had not visited Hartfield in seven
months and who had been finding reasons not to visit for longer than
that, said yes before she could think of an excuse.

Hartfield was a village. Not a picturesque village — no thatched roofs
or village greens — but a working village in the Midlands, with a main
street, a post office, a pub, and a bus stop. Alice Vance lived in the
house where Elara had grown up: a narrow brick semi-detached with a
garden that her mother tended with the same cheerful persistence she
brought to everything.

Elara arrived on Saturday morning with a gift — a scarf, purchased at
the train station in a moment of guilty haste — and found the house
warm and bright and full of the smell of baking. Her mother was in the
kitchen, icing a cake with the focused attention of a person who
considered cake decoration a serious art form. She was sixty-two,
small, gray-haired, with the particular energetic tidiness of a woman
who had raised two children alone after her husband's death and had
made it look, from the outside, like the easiest thing in the world.

"You look thin," her mother said, by way of greeting.

"You say that every time."

"You're thin every time. Sit down. There's tea."

Elara sat at the kitchen table — the same table she had eaten every
meal at until she was eighteen — and drank tea and watched her mother
ice the cake. The kitchen was unchanged: the same yellow curtains, the
same clock on the wall, the same row of postcards on the windowsill
that Elara had sent from Cambridge over the years. The familiarity
should have been comforting. Instead, it felt like an accusation. The
postcards were thin, bright lies — "Everything is wonderful, busy with
work, love, Elara" — and the kitchen, which remembered everything,
seemed to know it.

The party was small and pleasant. Neighbors came. There was cake and
wine and conversation. Alice Vance moved through the gathering with a
quiet, radiant happiness that Elara watched with the professional
attention she had developed over months of memory theft — and then
caught herself watching with, and felt a spike of self-loathing so
sharp that she excused herself to the garden.

She stood among her mother's roses — dormant in February, just thorny
stems in cold soil — and pressed her hands against her face.

She had been reading her mother's happiness with the eyes of a thief.
Assessing it. Measuring its intensity. Wondering, in some calculating
back room of her mind, whether it was at peak formation. Whether it
was worth taking.

"Stop it," she whispered. "Stop it, stop it, stop it."

But the thought, once formed, would not leave. It followed her back
inside, where her mother was opening the scarf with an expression of
genuine pleasure that was, Elara could not help noticing, approaching
peak formation. It followed her through the rest of the evening, through
the goodbyes and the washing-up and the quiet hour she spent sitting
with her mother by the fire while Alice talked about the garden and the
neighbors and the cat, and Elara listened and nodded and felt the
extractor in her coat pocket like a live coal against her ribs.

She went to bed in her old room and did not sleep.

                               V.

It happened on Sunday morning.

Elara came downstairs to find her mother in the kitchen, standing at
the window with a cup of tea, looking at the garden. The early light
was coming through the glass and falling across Alice Vance's face, and
her expression was one that Elara recognized with a clarity that cut
through every defense she had built: it was the expression of a woman
who was, in this moment, completely happy.

Not the happiness of a party or a gift or a celebration. A quieter,
deeper happiness — the happiness of a woman standing in her own kitchen,
in the house where she had raised her children, drinking tea, looking
at the garden she had planted and tended for thirty years, knowing that
her daughter was upstairs and would come down soon and they would have
breakfast together. It was the happiness of sufficiency. Of a life that
was small and particular and exactly enough.

Elara's temporal meter — she had brought it, as she always brought it;
it was in the pocket of her dressing gown, next to the extractor —
spiked into the red.

Peak formation. This was it. This was the moment — the memory
crystallizing now, right now, the happiest moment of Alice Vance's life
forming in the neural architecture of her sixty-two-year-old brain with
a brightness and a completeness that the meter had never registered at
this level. Not the ice cream woman. Not the new mother at the hospital
window. Not any of the thirty-seven memories in Elara's wooden box.
This was the highest reading she had ever seen.

Her mother's happiest moment. Standing in her kitchen. Looking at her
garden. Knowing her daughter was home.

Elara's hand moved to the extractor.

She held it. The device was warm from her pocket, smooth and familiar
in her palm. One activation. Ninety seconds. Her mother would lose the
memory — this memory, this quiet, radiant, perfectly ordinary moment
of happiness — and never know it was gone. She would remember the
morning. She would remember the tea. She would not remember the feeling.

Elara's thumb found the activation switch. She stood in the doorway
of the kitchen — three meters from her mother, close enough — and felt
the compulsion move through her like a current. Take it. Take it and
add it to the collection. Take it and replay it tonight, alone in your
flat, and feel what your mother is feeling right now, feel the happiness
that you have hollowed yourself out to steal, feel it with the perfect
immediacy of someone else's joy —

Her mother turned from the window.

"There you are," Alice said. "I was just thinking about you."

Their eyes met.

And Elara saw — saw with a sudden, devastating clarity that bypassed
every intellectual defense and every practiced justification — what she
was about to do. She was about to steal her mother's happiest memory.
The memory of this morning. Of tea and a garden and a daughter home.
She was about to reach into her mother's mind and pluck out the
brightest moment of a life that had been built, brick by brick, by a
woman who had never had enough and had made it enough through sheer
force of love and stubbornness and cheerful, relentless persistence.

She was about to take from her mother the feeling of having been happy.

The extractor was in her hand. Her thumb was on the switch. And something
inside her — not her mind, which had been compromised months ago, but
something older and less articulable — said no.

Not loudly. Not dramatically. Just: no.

Elara put the extractor back in her pocket. Her hands were shaking.

"Tea?" her mother asked.

"Yes," Elara said. "Please."

She sat down at the kitchen table. Her mother poured tea and set it
before her, and they sat together in the early light, and Alice talked
about the roses and the weather and the cat, and Elara held her cup
in both hands and felt the warmth of it, the real warmth, the heat of
the ceramic against her palms, and she listened to her mother's voice,
and she did not reach for the extractor, and the moment passed, and the
memory formed, and it was her mother's, and it stayed.

                               VI.

Elara went back to Cambridge that afternoon.

On the train, she held the wooden box on her lap. Thirty-seven crystals.
Thirty-seven stolen memories. Thirty-seven moments of happiness that
belonged to strangers who would never know they had been robbed.

She thought about the woman in the park. Had she gone home that evening
and felt, without knowing why, that the day had been slightly less good
than she expected? Had she told a friend, "I went to the park today —
it was nice, I suppose, but I can't quite remember why I was so pleased
about it"? Had the absence of the stolen memory changed her, in some
small, unmeasurable way — made her slightly less trusting of her own
happiness, slightly less certain that good moments would hold?

She thought about the new mother at the hospital window. Had that
woman looked at her baby in the days that followed and felt the love
but missed the joy — the specific, overwhelming, first-moment joy
that Elara had taken? Had she wondered, in the dark hours of feeding
and exhaustion and doubt, why the memory of seeing her child for the
first time was strangely flat, strangely colorless, like a photograph
with the saturation turned down?

She thought about her mother, standing in the kitchen with tea and a
garden and a daughter home, and she thought about how close she had
come to taking that, too.

The train moved through the Midlands, through fields and villages and
the small, particular landscapes of a country that went about its
business in the gray February light, and Elara held the box and thought
about what it would mean to give the memories back.

She could not, of course. The technology did not work in reverse — you
could extract a memory at the moment of formation, but you could not
reimplant it afterward. The neural window had closed. The memories in
the crystals were orphans now, separated from the minds that made them,
belonging to no one.

Except her. They belonged to her, in the way that stolen things belong
to the thief — not as a rightful possession but as a weight. A debt
carried.

She arrived in Cambridge at half past five. She went to her flat.
She put the box on her desk. She looked at it for a long time.

Then she opened the drawer where she kept the portable extractor and
the replay equipment. She took them out. She put them in a bag with the
box of crystals. She walked to the Cavendish Lab, used her keycard to
enter the building, and placed the bag on Dr. Hollis's desk with a
note that said:

     I stole 37 memories. I am resigning. I am sorry.
     The equipment is all here. The crystals should be
     destroyed. Please don't try to find me for at least
     a week. I need to be somewhere.

She walked out of the lab and took a taxi to the train station and
bought a ticket to Hartfield.

                               VII.

It was dark when she arrived. The village was quiet. She walked from the
station to her mother's house — a fifteen-minute walk she had made a
thousand times as a girl — and stood at the front door.

She could see through the window into the front room. Her mother was
in the armchair by the fire, reading a book, with the cat on her lap.
The lamp was on. The curtains were open. The scene was so ordinary,
so perfectly unremarkable, that it might have been a painting called
"Evening" or "Home" or "Everything That Matters."

Elara raised her hand to knock. She paused.

She had come back because she needed to be here — in this house, in
this village, in the presence of a woman whose happiness she had almost
stolen and hadn't. She had come back because the wooden box was gone
now, and the extractor was gone, and the replay equipment was gone, and
what remained was herself, hollow and shaking and unable to remember
the last time she had been genuinely happy on her own.

But that was the thing. That was the whole, terrible, luminous thing.
She was here. She was standing at her mother's door in the dark, and
the February air was cold on her face, and through the window she could
see the fire and the lamp and the cat and her mother's reading glasses
perched on the end of her nose, and something inside her — faint,
tentative, as fragile as a crocus pushing through frozen ground — was
stirring.

Not happiness. Not yet. Something prior to happiness. Something
necessary. The willingness to feel it. The willingness to let a moment
form in her own mind, in her own neural architecture, without stealing
it or measuring it or comparing it to something better in a crystal
in a box.

The willingness to be present, and to let the present be enough.

She knocked.

Her mother opened the door. The cat, disturbed, bolted for the stairs.
Alice Vance looked at her daughter — standing on the doorstep with no
bag, no scarf, no explanation — and her face moved through surprise and
concern and something else, something warm and immediate and
unmeasurable.

"Elara? What on earth — you were just here yesterday. Is everything
all right?"

Elara opened her mouth. She had prepared nothing to say. The truth was
too large and too strange and too shameful, and the lies were all used
up.

"Can I come home for a while?" she said.

Her mother looked at her. Whatever she saw — the thinness, the
exhaustion, the shaking hands, the eyes of a woman who had been
somewhere dark and was trying to come back — she did not ask about it.
Not now. That would come later, in the careful, persistent way that
mothers extract the truth from their children: slowly, gently, over
cups of tea and long silences and the patient application of love.

"Of course you can," Alice said. "Come in. I'll put the kettle on."

Elara stepped inside. The door closed behind her. The house was warm,
and the fire was burning, and the cat was peering down from the top of
the stairs with the offended dignity of an animal whose evening had
been disturbed.

And somewhere in the back of Elara's mind, in the place where memories
form and crystallize and become the architecture of a life, a small,
tentative warmth began to gather — not stolen, not measured, not
extracted for storage in a crystal, but hers, forming naturally, the way
happiness is supposed to form: from the simple, sufficient, ordinary
miracle of being where you are, with the people who love you, and
letting it be enough.

It was not much. It was not the brightest reading on any meter. It was
just a woman coming home, and a mother putting on the kettle, and a
cat coming slowly back down the stairs to investigate.

But it was real, and it was hers, and it stayed.

                            [THE END]

                       VOICES IN THE TREES

                          by Lucian Marsh

================================================================================

                               I.

I should say at the outset that I do not trust my own account.

I have written it down because the Colonial Authority requires a written
report from the sole survivor of any settlement incident classified
Grade Three or above, and because Dr. Ellison at the Kepler-22 medical
facility believes that the exercise of putting the events into sequence
will help me "organize the experience." I have my doubts about this.
Some experiences resist organization. They are, by their nature,
disordered — they belong to the category of things that happen in
the dark, in places where the familiar rules do not apply, and any
attempt to force them into the neat syntax of a report is itself a
kind of lie.

But I will try. The Authority wants facts, and facts I can provide —
or at least, those facts that survived the passage through my own
perception, which is, as will become apparent, an unreliable instrument.

My name is Connell Briar. I am — I was — a hunter. Not a sport hunter.
A pest controller. The colonies produce problems that don't appear in
the brochures: organisms that adapt to human settlements, that learn
to exploit our waste streams, that develop a taste for our crops or
our livestock or, in the more unpleasant cases, for us. My job was to
track these organisms, study their behavior, and eliminate them before
they became a threat to the settlement. I had been doing this work for
eleven years across four colony worlds when the Forest Authority on
Canopy — the world the surveyors had named for obvious reasons — sent
me a contract.

The problem, the contract said, was predators. The problem, as I would
learn, was something worse.

                               II.

Canopy was a forest world. That is the simplest way to describe it and
also the most inadequate. Calling Canopy a forest world is like calling
the Pacific a puddle. The planet was ninety-one percent landmass, and
the landmass was ninety-eight percent forest — not a forest of the
terrestrial kind, with clearings and meadows and the occasional bald
hilltop, but a continuous, unbroken canopy of vegetation that extended
from pole to pole, broken only by the few rivers wide enough to push
through the root systems and the single volcanic plateau where the
colony of Evander's Rest had been established.

The trees were not trees. They were tree-analogues — organisms that
occupied the same ecological niche as terrestrial trees but that had
evolved independently, under different constraints, and had arrived at
solutions that were, in certain respects, alien. They were enormous.
The smallest of the canopy species stood forty meters tall; the largest
exceeded two hundred. Their trunks were not wood but a dense, fibrous
tissue that was closer to cartilage than cellulose — flexible, slightly
warm to the touch, and permeated with a vascular system that circulated
a pale, amber fluid. Their canopy was so dense that the forest floor
existed in permanent twilight, and in the deeper stands, in the ancient
groves where the trunks were as broad as buildings and the canopy
closed overhead like a cathedral vault, the darkness was nearly complete.

I arrived at Evander's Rest in the wet season, when the air was thick
with moisture and the sounds of the forest were amplified by the damp
into a constant, layered murmur that was not quite noise and not quite
silence. The settlement was small — three hundred colonists, most of
them foresters and agricultural workers who were attempting to cultivate
the cleared land on the plateau's western slope. The buildings were
prefab modules arranged in a rough grid, surrounded by a perimeter
fence that was more psychological than practical. The fence was four
meters tall and electrified, but the forest pressed against it on three
sides with a patience that suggested it considered the fence a
temporary inconvenience.

The settlement coordinator, a tired woman named Pryce, met me at the
landing pad and briefed me on the walk to the admin module.

"We've lost six people in three months," she said. "All in the forest.
All on the access trails between the settlement and the logging
stations."

"Predator attacks?"

"That's what we assumed at first. We found the bodies — what was left
of them. The injuries were consistent with a large predator: puncture
wounds, tearing, the kind of damage you'd expect from something with
claws and teeth and a significant weight advantage. But we've never
seen the predator. Not once. No tracks. No scat. No visual sighting
by any of the trail crews."

"Nocturnal?"

"The attacks happen at all hours. Two were in broad daylight — well,
what passes for daylight under the canopy."

"How are the victims isolated? Do they wander off the trail?"

Pryce stopped walking. We were at the edge of the settlement, near the
perimeter fence. The forest rose beyond it like a wall — a wall of
trunks and shadow and the faint amber glow of the vascular fluid moving
through the bark.

"That's the part I wanted to tell you about," she said. "The victims
don't wander off. They're called off."

I waited.

"The trail crews work in pairs. Standard protocol. In every case, the
victim's partner reported the same thing: they heard a voice. A human
voice, calling the victim's name. Coming from the trees, from somewhere
off the trail. The victim responded — turned, called back, moved toward
the voice. And then they were gone."

"A human voice."

"A specific human voice. In every case, the victim's partner recognized
it. It was the voice of someone the victim knew. A spouse. A friend.
A parent." She paused. "In one case, it was the voice of a crew member
who had died in the first attack, three weeks earlier."

The forest murmured. I listened to it — the layered, wet sound of a
billion organisms doing what organisms do — and felt the first intimation
of something I am reluctant to call dread, because dread is an imprecise
word for what I felt. It was more specific than dread. It was the
recognition that I was being told something that did not fit into the
categories I carried with me, and that the categories would have to be
rebuilt.

"Show me where it happened," I said.

                               III.

The trail was a narrow path of compressed soil that wound through the
forest from the settlement's western gate to Logging Station Three,
approximately four kilometers away. It was maintained — barely — by
clearing crews who cut back the encroaching vegetation every two weeks,
and it was the only reliable route between the two points. The forest on
either side was dense, dark, and effectively impassable to anyone
without cutting tools and considerable determination.

I walked the trail alone. This was not bravado. It was method. I needed
to understand the environment on its own terms, without the distraction
of a companion's conversation or the false comfort of company. I carried
a pulse rifle — standard colonial pest control issue — and a recording
kit: directional microphones, motion sensors, a handheld spectrograph
for chemical analysis. I also carried a knife, a water canteen, and
a degree of professional confidence that would prove, in retrospect,
entirely insufficient.

The forest was quiet. Not silent — the ambient sound was a constant,
multilayered hum of biological activity: the drip of condensation from
the canopy, the rustle of small organisms in the undergrowth, the
faint, rhythmic pulsing of the trees' vascular systems, which was
audible, if you listened carefully, as a low, wet throb. But it was
quiet in the way that a room is quiet when someone has just left it —
a quality of attentiveness in the air, as if the forest were listening
to me as carefully as I was listening to it.

I walked for two kilometers without incident. The canopy closed
overhead, and the light dimmed from twilight to near-dark. I switched
on my headlamp and continued.

At kilometer three, I heard the voice.

It came from the left — from somewhere in the dense undergrowth off
the trail, maybe twenty meters in. It was a woman's voice, and it was
calling my name.

"Connell."

I stopped. The pulse rifle came up to my shoulder in a motion that was
automatic, trained into muscle memory by eleven years of work on colony
worlds where the things that wanted to eat you did not always announce
themselves. I listened.

"Connell. Over here."

The voice was clear. It was not loud — it had the quality of a
conversational call, the kind of voice you would use to get someone's
attention across a room — but it was distinct against the ambient hum
of the forest. It was female. It was familiar.

It was my sister's voice.

My sister, Ruth Briar, lived on Earth. I had not spoken to her in three
years. I had not seen her in seven. There was no possibility — no
conceivable mechanism — by which her voice could be emanating from the
undergrowth of an alien forest forty light-years from the planet where
she lived.

And yet it was her voice. Not an approximation. Not a general female
voice that my brain was pattern-matching into recognition. It was Ruth.
The specific timbre, the specific cadence, the slight catch on the
first syllable of my name that she had had since childhood — all of
it was precise and unmistakable.

"Connell. Come here. I need to show you something."

I did not move. I stood on the trail with the rifle at my shoulder and
the headlamp cutting a narrow cone of light into the darkness, and I
let the voice wash over me, and I did not move. This was not courage.
This was experience. Eleven years of work on colony worlds had taught
me one rule that I followed without exception: when something in the
wild seems wrong, it is wrong. Do not investigate. Do not approach.
Stand still, assess, and if necessary, retreat.

The voice called again. "Connell, please."

I activated the directional microphone and aimed it at the source. The
recording equipment captured the voice clearly — waveform, frequency,
amplitude. I would analyze it later. For now, I wanted to observe.

The voice stopped.

The forest was quiet. The wet, layered hum continued, but the voice
did not return. I stood on the trail for ten more minutes, scanning
the undergrowth with my headlamp and the motion sensors. Nothing moved.
Nothing revealed itself. The darkness on either side of the trail was
absolute and impenetrable.

I walked back to the settlement.

                               IV.

I analyzed the voice recording that evening in the admin module, using
the spectrographic equipment I had brought. The results were, in the
precise language of my profession, anomalous.

The voice was human. The waveform was consistent with a human female
vocal apparatus — larynx, vocal cords, oral cavity — producing speech
in the English language. The frequency range was normal. The harmonic
structure was normal. Every measurable parameter said: this is a human
being speaking.

But there was something else. Beneath the voice — audible only when I
filtered out the primary frequencies and amplified the background — there
was a secondary signal. It was low-frequency, rhythmic, and biological.
It was, I realized after several hours of analysis, the same frequency
as the trees' vascular pulse.

The voice was being produced by an organism that was not human but that
had, somehow, learned to replicate human speech with perfect fidelity.
Not approximate fidelity. Not the rough mimicry of a parrot or a
mynah bird. Perfect fidelity. Down to the individual harmonic
signatures that distinguished one human voice from another.

This raised a question that I was not, at that point, prepared to
answer: where had the organism heard my sister's voice?

                               V.

I went back into the forest the next day, and the day after, and the
day after that. Each time, I heard voices. They called names — my name,
the names of settlement personnel, names I did not recognize. They came
from different directions, different distances, sometimes overlapping
in a murmur that was almost conversational, as if a group of people
were talking quietly in the darkness between the trees.

On the fourth day, I heard a voice I recognized. It was not my sister's.
It was the voice of Hans Dekker, the logging crew foreman who had been
the first victim, killed three months ago. His wife, Lena, had played
me a recording of his voice the evening before, so that I would know
it if I heard it. I heard it now, calling from the undergrowth thirty
meters north of the trail, saying, "Lena. Lena, where are you?"

I recorded it. I analyzed it that evening. The voice was a perfect
match for the recording Lena Dekker had provided. And beneath it, the
same low-frequency vascular pulse.

I sat in the admin module and stared at my spectrographic data and
felt the categories of my understanding rearrange themselves into a
configuration that I did not like.

The organisms — whatever they were — were not mimicking human voices.
Mimicry implies hearing a sound and reproducing it. These voices were
too precise for mimicry. They were reproductions — exact copies,
captured and stored and replayed with a fidelity that no biological
mimic had ever achieved.

And some of the voices belonged to people who were dead. To people
who had been killed in the forest. To people whose voices the organisms
could only have captured at the moment of encounter — the moment before
the killing.

The voices were not bait. The voices were memories.

                               VI.

I need to tell you about Evander's Rest.

Not the current settlement. The first one.

Pryce had mentioned it in passing during my initial briefing, and I
had not pursued it at the time, because it did not seem relevant. The
first settlement on Canopy — also called Evander's Rest, after the
colony ship Evander that had transported the initial cohort — had been
established twelve years before the current one, on the same volcanic
plateau. It had consisted of one hundred and forty-seven colonists:
foresters, agricultural workers, scientists, and their families.

It had failed. The colonial records described the failure in the
bureaucratic language of the Colonial Authority: "Settlement Evander's
Rest (Initial) was abandoned after eighteen months of operation due to
unsustainable attrition rates resulting from hostile indigenous fauna."
The surviving colonists — eighty-three of them — had been evacuated.
Sixty-four had died.

The records did not specify how they died. The records did not mention
voices.

I found the information I needed not in the Colonial Authority's files
but in the personal logs of the first settlement's coordinator, a man
named Haldane, whose records had been archived and largely forgotten.
Haldane's logs were detailed, methodical, and increasingly desperate.
They described, over eighteen months, a pattern that was identical to
the one I was investigating: colonists lured off established paths by
voices calling their names. Voices that belonged to friends, family
members, colleagues. Voices that, as the months passed and the deaths
accumulated, began to belong to the dead.

Haldane's final log entry, dated two days before the evacuation, read:

     They are not hunting us. I understand that now. They are
     collecting us. The voices are not lures. They are a library.
     Every person who dies in the forest becomes a voice in the
     trees. The organisms store us. They remember us. I do not know
     why. I do not know what purpose it serves. But I know this:
     the forest is full of voices, and some of them have been calling
     for twelve years, and they will be calling long after we are gone.

Twelve years ago. The first settlement had fallen twelve years ago, and
the voices of its dead had been calling in the forest ever since, calling
names that no living person could hear, calling into the darkness between
the trees with the perfect, patient fidelity of an organism that did not
forget.

And now the second settlement was here. New people. New names. New voices
to collect.

                               VII.

I went into the forest one last time.

I went alone, and I went at night, and I went deeper than the trails,
deeper than the logging stations, into the ancient groves where the
trunks were as wide as buildings and the darkness was so complete that
my headlamp seemed to dissolve into it rather than push it back. I
carried my rifle and my recording equipment, but I carried them more
out of habit than expectation. I was not hunting. I was listening.

The voices began at the edge of the grove. They were soft at first —
whispers, fragments, the ghost-sounds of speech at the threshold of
audibility. As I walked deeper, they grew clearer, and more numerous.
They came from all around me, from the trunks and the canopy and the
undergrowth, from the darkness itself. They were speaking. They were
calling names. They were saying the things that people say to each
other in the ordinary moments of life: Come here. Look at this. Where
are you? I'm over here. Wait for me.

I recognized some of the voices. Hans Dekker: "Lena, where are you?"
A woman I did not know: "Thomas, dinner's ready." A child: "Papa, come
look, come look!" An older man, laughing, telling the beginning of a
joke I would never hear the end of.

And others. Older voices, fainter, eroded by the years but still
present, still speaking in the dark. The voices of the first settlement.
The sixty-four who had died twelve years ago and whose last sounds had
been captured by an organism that did not understand language but
understood, in some way that I could not fathom, the importance of
preserving it.

I stood in the grove and listened, and the voices surrounded me like
water, and I understood — not intellectually but in the way you
understand weather, or grief, or the sound of the sea — what the
organisms were doing.

They were not hunting. Haldane had been right about that. They were not
using the voices as lures, at least not intentionally. The voices were
a by-product. The organisms — whatever they were, dwelling in the trunks
or in the root systems or in the canopy above — were predators, yes.
They killed. They fed. That was their nature, and I would not pretend
that nature was anything other than what it was.

But they also remembered. The voices they captured at the moment of
the kill were stored — encoded in the vascular fluid, perhaps, or in
the fibrous tissue of the trunks, or in some medium I could not detect
— and replayed. Over and over. Endlessly. Not as a hunting strategy.
As a compulsion. As a function of whatever passed, in these organisms,
for memory.

The forest remembered the people it had killed. It remembered their
voices. It called their names into the darkness, and the names echoed
and overlapped and accumulated, and the forest filled with them, year
after year, a growing archive of the dead, and the dead spoke to each
other in the voices they had carried in life, and the forest listened,
and the forest did not forget.

I switched off my headlamp.

The darkness was total. The voices were everywhere. I stood among them
and felt — I will say it plainly — I felt that I was standing in a
graveyard that did not know it was a graveyard. A memorial built by
something that did not understand death but that had, through the
blind, relentless machinery of its biology, created something that
resembled mourning.

A voice, close to my ear, said my name.

"Connell."

Not Ruth. Not anyone I knew. A man's voice, tired and kind, with an
accent I could not place. One of the first settlers, perhaps. One of
the sixty-four who had died here twelve years ago, whose voice had been
calling in the dark ever since, calling a name that no one had come to
answer.

I did not answer it. I switched on my headlamp and walked out of the
grove, and I did not look back, and the voices followed me for a
kilometer down the trail before they faded into the ambient hum of the
forest, and then they were gone, or rather they were still there — they
were always there, they would always be there — but I could no longer
hear them.

                               VIII.

My recommendation to the Colonial Authority was unambiguous: abandon
Evander's Rest. Relocate the colonists. Do not return to Canopy.

The Authority, in its institutional wisdom, has not yet responded. It
has been three months. The settlement continues to operate. The trail
crews work in groups of four now, with strict protocols against
responding to any voice heard from the forest. There have been no
further deaths, which the Authority considers evidence that the problem
is under control.

I consider it evidence that the organisms are patient.

They have been patient for twelve years. They will be patient for twelve
more. The voices are still calling in the trees — I know this because
Pryce sends me weekly reports, and in every report she mentions,
casually, as though it were a weather condition rather than a horror,
that the crews heard voices again today. Hans Dekker's voice. The child
calling for Papa. The old man's unfinished joke.

And my sister's voice. Ruth Briar, who lives on Earth, forty light-years
away, who has never set foot on Canopy, whose voice the organisms should
not possess and cannot have acquired through any mechanism I can explain.

I have turned this over and over. I have considered the possibility that
the organisms accessed the colonial database, which contains voice
recordings of every colonist's registered next-of-kin. I have considered
the possibility that they extracted the voice from my own memory, somehow
— that they read me the way they read the forest, through senses I do
not understand. I have considered the possibility that I am wrong, that
the voice was not Ruth's, that my perception deceived me.

I have considered all of these possibilities, and none of them satisfies
me, and the one that frightens me most is the one I cannot shake: that
the organisms are not merely recording the voices of the people they
kill. That they are recording something deeper. That the contact —
the brief, terrible moment of encounter between the predator and its
prey — opens a channel that goes further than the vocal cords and the
larynx, that reaches into the mind of the dying person and captures
not just the voice but the web of voices that the person carries — the
voices of everyone they have ever loved, everyone they have ever called
by name, everyone who has ever called them.

If this is true, then the forest does not merely remember the dead. It
remembers the dead's memories. And the voices in the trees are not
only the voices of the killed but the voices of everyone the killed
ever knew — an exponentially expanding archive of human sound, growing
with each death, each captured mind, each web of connection preserved
in the alien tissue of a forest that does not understand what it holds
but holds it nonetheless, faithfully, relentlessly, forever.

I do not sleep well. I have not slept well since I left Canopy. Dr.
Ellison says this is expected. She says the nightmares will diminish
with time.

I do not have nightmares about the forest. I have nightmares about
the silence. About what it would be like if the voices stopped — if
the organisms died, or the forest burned, or the colony destroyed the
ancient groves in the name of safety. The voices would stop. The
archive would be lost. And the sixty-four people who died twelve years
ago, and the six who died three months ago, and whoever dies next —
their voices would be gone. Truly gone. The last trace of them erased
from the universe.

The forest remembers them. Nothing else does. Nothing else can.

I lie in my bunk at the Kepler-22 medical facility and listen to the
silence of the room, and I think about the grove, and the voices, and
the darkness, and I wonder whether the worst thing about Canopy is the
killing, or whether the worst thing is that the killing is, in some
terrible and accidental way, an act of preservation.

The forest remembers. The forest calls. The forest does not forget.

I wish I could say the same for us.

                            [THE END]

                         PATCHWORK PLANET

                      by Robert "Bob" Finley

================================================================================

                               I.

Mack Corrado had seen some strange rocks in his time, but this one took
the prize.

"Tell me I'm not looking at what I think I'm looking at," he said,
leaning over the navigation console with both hands flat on the edge
and his jaw about two inches from the deck.

"You're not looking at what you think you're looking at," said Jenny
Liu, the survey team's geologist, from the sensor station. "You're
looking at something much worse."

The planet hung in the main viewport like a bad collage. Mack had
been surveying worlds for the Outer Reach Survey Corps for twelve years,
and in that time he had seen gas giants with rings made of diamond dust,
ice moons that hummed in frequencies you could feel in your fillings,
and a rogue planet so dark it was visible only as a hole in the
starfield. He had developed, through long exposure, a professional
immunity to wonder.

That immunity was failing him now.

The world below was — there was no other word for it — stitched
together. One hemisphere was desert: red sand, dune fields, the
occasional outcrop of sandstone baking under a relentless sun. The
other hemisphere was jungle: green and dense and wet, a carpet of
vegetation so thick you couldn't see the ground. Between them, where
one biome should have transitioned gradually into the other through
some intermediate zone of scrubland or savannah, there was nothing
gradual at all. There was a line. A visible, sharp-edged line where the
red sand ended and the green jungle began, as if someone had taken a
pair of scissors to two different planets and glued the halves together.

And it got worse. Because those weren't the only two pieces.

Along the equator, between the desert and the jungle, there was a
strip of something else entirely: gray, cratered terrain that looked
like the surface of Luna. Airless, barren, pockmarked with impact
craters, sitting right there between the sand dunes and the palm trees
like a stretch of bad road between two neighborhoods.

And to the north, above the desert, a wedge of ocean — actual liquid
water, blue and rolling, with whitecaps — that ended abruptly at a
cliff face where the water simply stopped, as if held back by an
invisible dam, and beyond the cliff was tundra: flat, frozen, windswept
white.

"I count twelve distinct biome patches," Jenny said, her voice carrying
the carefully controlled tone of a scientist confronted with something
that should not exist. "Desert, jungle, lunar-type terrain, ocean,
tundra, volcanic badlands, crystalline flats, some kind of fungal
forest, what appears to be a shallow methane sea, and three zones I
don't have categories for. Each one has discrete atmospheric and
gravitational characteristics."

"Wait — different gravity?"

"Different gravity. The desert patch is pulling 0.8 g. The jungle
is 1.1. The lunar strip is 0.16. Each patch maintains its own
gravitational field independently of the others."

"That's not possible."

"I know."

Mack straightened up and looked at the rest of his crew. They were
all in the bridge, which was not large — the survey ship Terrier was
built for function, not comfort — and they were all wearing the same
expression: a mixture of professional curiosity and the dawning
realization that their survey protocols were about to become very
complicated.

The crew was four people. Mack Corrado, mission commander, a stocky
man from Chicago with a buzz cut and a talent for not panicking. Jenny
Liu, geologist, from San Francisco, small and precise and currently
running six instruments at once. Pete Gallagher, the pilot, a tall
Irishman from Cork who could land a ship on a postage stamp and who
was currently gripping the yoke as if the planet might reach up and
grab them. And Rosa Gutierrez, the biologist, from Tucson, who had
been leaning into her sensor display for the past ten minutes and who
now looked up with an expression that Mack recognized as trouble.

"The jungle patch is alive," Rosa said. "Obviously. But so is the
fungal forest. And the methane sea has microbial signatures. And
Mack — the crystalline flats are generating an electromagnetic field.
I don't know if that counts as alive, but it counts as something."

"Are any of them dangerous?"

"I have no idea. None of these ecosystems should be within a thousand
light-years of each other. They have different atmospheric compositions,
different temperature ranges, different base chemistries. The desert
runs on oxygen-nitrogen. The methane sea is, obviously, methane. The
fungal forest appears to be respiring chlorine. And they're all
existing right next to each other, separated by —" She squinted at
her display. "I don't know what. The boundary zones between patches
show energy readings that don't correspond to anything in our database."

"The exposed bedrock," Jenny said. "Look at the boundaries between the
patches. There's a substrate visible — bare rock, exposed — running
between each biome like mortar between bricks. And Mack, the bedrock
isn't from any of the patch biomes. It's something else entirely. The
spectrographic signature doesn't match any known geological formation."

Mack looked at the planet. It turned slowly in the viewport, showing
them the desert, then the jungle, then the strip of lunar terrain, each
one as distinct and separate as countries on a schoolroom globe, each
one impossible.

"All right," he said. "We're going down."

"Which patch?" Pete asked.

"The desert. Lowest threat profile, easiest landing terrain, and I want
to look at one of those boundary zones up close."

"Copy that. Descent in twenty."

Mack turned to his crew. "Full survey kit. Atmosphere suits rated for
everything — we don't know what we're breathing down there. Weapons on
the belt, not in the hand. We're scientists first, but we're scientists
who are landing on a planet that looks like God's jigsaw puzzle, and I
want everyone ready for the possibility that some of the pieces don't
want us stepping on them."

                               II.

The desert was hot.

This should not have been surprising — deserts are hot; that is more
or less the defining characteristic — but the heat had a quality that
Mack found unsettling. It was too precise. The surface temperature was
47 degrees Celsius, uniform across the entire desert patch, with no
variation for topography, elevation, or time of day. The sun — a
yellow dwarf, unremarkable — beat down from a cloudless sky that was
the exact shade of washed-out blue you'd find over the Sahara. The sand
was red and fine-grained and extended to the horizon in rippling dune
fields that might have been lifted whole from Mars.

The Terrier sat on a flat stretch of hardpan near the eastern edge of
the desert patch. Mack had chosen this landing site for its proximity
to the boundary zone — the visible line where the desert ended and the
jungle began. From the ship, the boundary was about three kilometers
to the east. They could see it: a hard edge of green against red, as
sharp as a knife cut, rising above the dune field like a wall.

They walked toward it.

The sand crunched under their boots in a way that was familiar and
therefore comforting. The air in Mack's suit readout was
oxygen-nitrogen, 21-78, pressure one atmosphere — breathable. He could
have taken his helmet off. He didn't.

"Gravity is 0.79 g," Jenny said, reading her instruments as she walked.
"Consistent across the patch. No variation. Mack, this is artificial."

"What do you mean, artificial?"

"I mean gravity doesn't work like this. Gravity is a function of mass.
The whole planet has one mass, one gravitational field. You can't have
different gravitational strengths in different regions of the same
planet's surface. Not unless someone is — imposing them."

"Imposing them how?"

"I don't know. But the readings are unambiguous. This patch of desert
has a gravitational field that is independent of the planet's overall
mass. Something is generating 0.79 g locally, within this patch, and
0.79 g only."

They walked on. The boundary grew closer, the green wall of the jungle
resolving into individual shapes: broad leaves, thick trunks, tangled
vines. The contrast with the desert was absolute. There was no
transition zone, no strip of scrub or grassland. One step was red sand
at 47 degrees. The next was jungle floor at 28 degrees, with humidity
so high that Mack's visor fogged instantly.

But between those two steps was the bedrock.

                               III.

The boundary zone was a strip of exposed rock approximately ten meters
wide, running in a straight line between the desert and the jungle as
far as they could see in both directions — north to the horizon, south
to the horizon. The rock was smooth, dark gray, and featureless. It
looked like nothing so much as a factory floor — a flat, engineered
surface that existed for the sole purpose of separating the two biomes
on either side.

Jenny knelt on the bedrock and ran her instruments across it. She was
quiet for a long time.

"This isn't rock," she said, finally.

"What is it?"

"I don't know. The spectrometer can't identify it. The molecular
structure is — it's dense. Incredibly dense. And it's emitting a
low-level energy field across a broad spectrum. This is what's
maintaining the boundary. This substrate is actively separating the
two biomes."

Rosa was at the jungle edge, scanning the vegetation with her
biological sensors. "The jungle stops exactly at the bedrock. The
root systems don't cross it. Neither does the humidity. On this side
of the line, it's 47 degrees and dry. On that side, 28 degrees and
98 percent humidity. The transition happens within ten meters. That's
not a natural climate gradient. That's a wall."

Mack stood on the bedrock and looked in both directions. The strip
extended to the horizon — a gray ribbon dividing the world into
incompatible pieces. To his left, the desert shimmered in its uniform
heat. To his right, the jungle steamed and dripped and rustled with
the sounds of organisms he had never heard before.

"Someone built this," he said.

His crew looked at him.

"Someone — something — took pieces of a dozen different worlds and
assembled them here, on this planet, held together by whatever this
substrate is. Each piece maintains its own atmosphere, its own gravity,
its own ecosystem, independent of the others. This isn't a natural
world. This is a — I don't know what. A collection. A museum. A zoo."

"A zoo for what?" Pete asked.

"For worlds. For pieces of worlds. Somebody collected them."

They stood on the boundary, three humans from a world that was not
represented in the collection, and looked at the impossible planet
that someone — something — had assembled from the fragments of a dozen
others, and Mack felt the particular vertigo that came from realizing
that the universe was stranger than his training, his experience, and
his considerable professional immunity to wonder had prepared him for.

"All right," he said. "New plan. We survey every accessible patch. Full
geological, biological, atmospheric workup. We map the boundary zones.
We analyze this substrate. And we try to figure out who built this thing
and why." He paused. "And whether they're still around."

"You think they might be?"

"Someone is maintaining this. The atmospheric boundaries don't leak. The
gravitational fields don't bleed into each other. The ecosystems don't
cross-pollinate. This isn't a ruin. This is operational."

He looked at the sky — the washed-out blue of the desert atmosphere,
which ended, he now noticed, at the boundary line, where the sky above
the jungle was a deeper blue-green, a different sky entirely, as if the
atmosphere itself had been cut and stitched.

"Someone is keeping the lights on," he said. "I want to find the
switch."

                               IV.

They spent three days surveying the planet's surface.

Each patch was a world in miniature — a self-contained ecosystem
complete with atmosphere, weather patterns, geological formations,
and, in several cases, living organisms. The desert was geologically
consistent with a young, arid world: sandstone, iron oxide, wind
erosion patterns suggesting millions of years of steady, dry conditions.
The jungle was a mature tropical ecosystem with a canopy layer, an
understory, a forest floor teeming with invertebrate analogues, and
a soil composition that suggested billions of years of biological
cycling. The lunar strip was exactly what it appeared to be: a fragment
of an airless, cratered body, complete with regolith and micrometeorite
impacts, existing in vacuum within ten meters of a pressurized jungle.

The boundaries held. In every case, the transition from one biome to
another was abrupt, total, and maintained by the dark substrate that ran
between them like the leading in a stained-glass window. Jenny's
analysis of the substrate progressed from confusion to frustration to
a kind of awed resignation. The material was not composed of any element
or compound in the known periodic table. It was generating energy — not
much, but constantly — and that energy was what maintained the impossible
boundaries between the patches.

On the second day, Rosa made a discovery in the fungal forest patch that
changed their understanding of the planet entirely.

The fungal forest was a nightmare landscape of towering, pale-stalked
organisms that rose from a carpet of spore-producing mycelium. The
atmosphere was chlorine-heavy and lethal without a sealed suit. The
organisms were enormous — the largest fungal bodies exceeded fifty
meters in height — and they were, Rosa confirmed, alive in a way that
went beyond passive growth.

"They're communicating," she said, over the comm. "Electrochemical
signals running through the mycelial network. This is a connected
ecosystem — the entire fungal forest is essentially one organism,
sharing information through the root network."

"What kind of information?"

"I can't decode it. But the signal density is — Mack, it's enormous.
This network is processing more information than a human brain. Much
more."

"Is it intelligent?"

Rosa was quiet for a moment. "Define intelligent. It's complex. It's
responsive. When I walked into the patch, the signal density increased
by forty percent. It knows we're here."

Mack felt the hair on the back of his neck rise — a reflex that no
amount of professional composure could override. "Everybody back to
the ship."

"Mack —"

"Back to the ship. Now. We regroup and figure out what we're dealing
with."

                               V.

They regrouped. They argued. They went back out.

Because that was what survey crews did. You found something strange,
you studied it, and you put it in the report. You didn't run. You
didn't panic. You certainly didn't leave a planet-sized mystery
unsurveyed because the fungus got chatty.

On the third day, they found the answer. Or rather, the answer found
them.

They were in the boundary zone between the desert and the crystalline
flats — a patch of gleaming, translucent formations that rose from
the dark substrate like the towers of a city made of glass. Jenny had
been scanning the formations for hours, fascinated by their structure,
when she stopped, lowered her instruments, and said, very quietly,
"Mack. Come look at this."

He went. He looked. The crystalline formation in front of them was
approximately four meters tall and two meters wide, a rough column
of translucent material that caught the light and refracted it into
prismatic patterns on the ground. Inside the column, visible through
the translucent matrix, was a shape.

A shape that was recognizable.

It was a map. A three-dimensional map, rendered in the crystal's
internal structure, showing a planet — not this planet, but a complete,
spherical world with continents and oceans and ice caps. And beside
it, another crystal, containing another map: a different world, a
different configuration of land and sea. And another. And another. A
row of crystal columns stretching into the distance, each one
containing a map of a different world.

"These are the source worlds," Jenny said. "The worlds the patches came
from. Each crystal is a record — a map of the original planet. The
desert patch came from this world." She pointed to the nearest crystal.
"The jungle from that one. The lunar terrain from that one. Someone
didn't just collect the patches. They catalogued them."

"A museum," Rosa said.

"An archive," Jenny corrected. "A sample collection. Someone — something
— is collecting pieces of worlds the way a geologist collects core
samples. A slice of this world. A slice of that one. Each one preserved,
maintained, kept alive in its original conditions. For study, maybe. Or
for preservation. Or for —"

"For remembering," Mack said.

They looked at him.

"Think about it. Twelve patches from twelve worlds. Each one kept in
its original state. Each one maintained by a technology we can't begin
to understand. And a crystal archive that records where each one came
from. This isn't a lab. It isn't an experiment. It's a memorial. Someone
is preserving pieces of worlds. Maybe worlds that don't exist anymore.
Maybe worlds that are dying, or dead, or about to be. Someone who has
the technology to cut a piece out of a world and keep it alive is also
someone who might need to. Because the world itself is gone."

The crystalline flats hummed. The electromagnetic field Rosa had
detected — the field that permeated the entire patch — shifted in
frequency, just slightly. As if something, somewhere in the gleaming
forest of crystal columns, had heard Mack's words, and recognized them,
and responded.

Nobody said anything for a long time.

"Let's go home," Mack said, finally. "Let's write the report. And let's
recommend that nobody touches this place until we figure out who
the collector is, and whether they're still collecting."

They walked back to the Terrier through the desert patch, under the
washed-out sky. Behind them, the patchwork planet turned in the
starlight, its impossible geography held together by a technology and
a purpose that Mack Corrado, twelve years a surveyor, could feel in
his bones but could not name.

Someone was preserving worlds. Someone was keeping the pieces alive.
And somewhere on this planet — or above it, or below it, or in a
dimension of existence that survey equipment could not detect — the
collector was watching, and waiting, and adding to the collection.

Mack didn't look back. He didn't need to. He knew the planet would
be there when they returned, the same way he knew the stars would be
there — patient, strange, and full of more questions than one survey
crew could answer in a lifetime.

He climbed aboard the Terrier and sealed the hatch.

"Take us up, Pete. Nice and easy."

The ship rose through the desert atmosphere, through the sharp boundary
where the washed-out blue gave way to the black of space, and the
patchwork planet fell away below them — red and green and gray and
white and blue, stitched together like a quilt made by a god who
couldn't decide which world was most worth saving, and so saved a
piece of each.

                            [THE END]

                        ABOUT THE AUTHORS

================================================================================

ARTHUR WAINWRIGHT has been writing fiction about robots, computers, and
artificial minds for this magazine since our fourth issue, and with
"Trial of Unit Seven" he has, in the editor's opinion, produced his
finest story to date. A former systems engineer and cryptanalyst,
Wainwright brings a mathematician's precision to questions that
philosophers have debated for centuries. He lives in Montclair, New
Jersey, with his wife Eleanor, and reports that the story was inspired
by a spirited dinner-table argument with a colleague about whether a
chess-playing computer could be said to "want" to win. He has published
two story collections and is at work on a novel about a robot that
writes poetry. We wish the robot well.

FRANKLIN HOYT is a political writer in the truest sense: he writes
about power, who has it, and what they do with it. A former journalist
and veteran of a segregated Army unit, Hoyt has been one of this
magazine's most important and most provocative voices since "The
Committee" appeared in our fourteenth issue. "Embargo" is his most
sustained narrative to date, and its themes — sovereignty, resource
exploitation, the machinery of control — could not be more timely.
He lives in Washington, D.C., with his wife Barbara, and when asked
about the story's inspiration, he replies: "Read the papers. Any papers.
Any decade."

CORDELL BEAUMONT is the master of planetary romance, and "Cartographer's
Last Map" may be his farewell to the form — or so he has hinted in
correspondence with the editor. We hope he is bluffing. No writer in
this magazine's history has rendered alien landscapes with such
intoxicating beauty, and the interior of his living planet is among the
most vivid and extraordinary settings we have ever published. Beaumont
lives in New Orleans, where he tends his garden, drinks bourbon on
his veranda, and insists that the best science fiction is written in
the key of B-flat.

THEODORE CAIN is the most prolific contributor in the history of "Tales
from the Future and Beyond," and if "Third Shift" is any indication,
prolificacy and quality are not mutually exclusive. This lean, taut
story of a graveyard shift that goes wrong is among the finest things
Cain has written for us — a testament to his extraordinary versatility
and his gift for making ordinary people in extraordinary circumstances
feel utterly real. He lives in Dearborn, Michigan, works at General
Motors by day, and writes every evening from eight until midnight. We
are astonished. We are also grateful.

PHYLLIS CRENSHAW is our reigning queen of temporal paradox, and
"Stolen Happiest Day" marks a new direction for her work: darker, more
personal, and more emotionally devastating than the elegant puzzle-
stories that made her reputation. The time-travel device in this story
is the simplest she has ever conceived; the consequences are the most
complex. She lives in Philadelphia with her husband David and their
two children, teaches logic at the university, and assures us that
she has never, to her knowledge, stolen anyone's happiest memory.

LUCIAN MARSH lives alone on the New England coast and writes stories
that make the editor sleep with the lights on. "Voices in the Trees"
is vintage Marsh: atmospheric, unsettling, and possessed of a
melancholy that lingers long after the final page. His use of the
first-person unreliable narrator is, as always, masterful — the reader
is never entirely certain what is real and what is the product of a
mind under strain. Marsh has attended exactly one science fiction
convention and has no plans to attend another. He communicates
exclusively by letter, and his letters are almost as disturbing as
his fiction.

ROBERT "BOB" FINLEY learned to write in the pulps, and he writes with
the speed, energy, and cheerful disregard for plausibility that the
best pulp fiction demands. "Patchwork Planet" is Finley in top form:
a vivid premise, a likeable crew, a pace that never flags, and a sense
of wonder that is as genuine as it is infectious. He lives in Chicago
with his wife Carol and an indeterminate number of cats, follows the
White Sox with religious devotion, and insists that he could write a
5,000-word story in a single sitting "if the coffee holds out."

                    THE BOOKSHELF OF TOMORROW
                         Book Reviews

================================================================================

THE DEATH OF GRASS by John Christopher
(Michael Joseph, 1956, 12s 6d / U.S. edition forthcoming)

Reviewed by Maxwell Sterling

Mr. Christopher has written a novel that should terrify you, and the
reason it should terrify you is that its premise requires no
extrapolation whatsoever. A virus destroys the world's grass crops —
wheat, rice, barley, all of it — and civilization collapses within
weeks. There are no aliens, no atomic weapons, no far-future
technologies. There is only hunger, and what human beings do to each
other when the food runs out. The novel follows a small group of
survivors as they travel through an England descending into barbarism,
and the journey is harrowing, unsentimental, and brilliantly observed.
Mr. Christopher's prose is clean and sharp and refuses to look away
from the worst of human behavior — or the best, which surfaces in
unexpected moments and in unexpected people. This is catastrophe fiction
at its most rigorous and most humane. It is also, I should warn you,
not a comfortable read. But then, the best science fiction rarely is.
Highly recommended.

DOUBLE STAR by Robert A. Heinlein
(Doubleday, 1956, $2.95)

Reviewed by Maxwell Sterling

Mr. Heinlein, who needs no introduction to readers of this or any
other science fiction magazine, has written what may be his most purely
entertaining novel. A down-on-his-luck actor is hired to impersonate a
kidnapped political leader — a premise borrowed from Ruritanian romance
and transplanted, with Heinlein's customary confidence, to a future
solar system where the politics are interplanetary and the stakes are
the relationship between humanity and its alien neighbors. The voice —
the actor's first-person narration — is brilliant: vain, theatrical,
gradually deepening into genuine commitment as the impersonation becomes
more than a role. Heinlein has always been our finest craftsman of the
readable, and "Double Star" reads like a dream. It is also, beneath
the entertainment, a serious meditation on identity, duty, and the
question of whether we become the roles we play. A delight from first
page to last.

THE STARS MY DESTINATION by Alfred Bester
(Signet, 1956, 35 cents)

Reviewed by Maxwell Sterling

Mr. Bester's second novel surpasses his first, and his first was "The
Demolished Man," which I reviewed in these pages three years ago and
called brilliant. "The Stars My Destination" is more than brilliant.
It is incendiary. Gully Foyle — Mechanic's Mate Third Class, survivor,
maniac, and the most compellingly repulsive protagonist in the genre's
history — burns through this novel like a comet through an atmosphere,
destroying everything in his path, including the reader's expectations.
The prose is pyrotechnic, the plotting is savage, the social commentary
is decades ahead of its time, and the final pages achieve a visionary
intensity that I have encountered nowhere else in science fiction.
Buy this book. Read it immediately. Then read it again. Essential.
